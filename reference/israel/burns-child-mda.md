## Epidemiology -- Children and Burns in Israel

- Approximately 78% of burn victims in Israel are children under age 4 (Ministry of Health)
- Roughly 1,850 children require emergency care for burns annually in Israel
- 84% of pediatric burn injuries result from hot liquids (scalds), predominantly in kitchen settings
- Most common scenario: hot tea or coffee spilled onto a child
- Infants have thinner skin -- a burn that causes only discomfort to an adult can cause significantly greater damage in infants, including prolonged hospitalisation, surgery, and permanent scarring

## Burn Classification (דרגות כוויה)

### First Degree (כוויה מדרגה ראשונה) -- Superficial
- Affects only the epidermis (outer skin layer)
- Characterised by redness, mild swelling, pain
- Similar to sunburn
- Heals within 1-2 weeks without scarring
- Caused by: brief contact with heat, prolonged sun exposure

### Second Degree (כוויה מדרגה שנייה) -- Partial Thickness
- Penetrates to the dermis layer
- Characterised by blisters (שלפוחיות), bright red-pink colour, severe pain
- Deeper second-degree burns may appear white
- May or may not leave scars depending on depth
- May require prescription bandages, hospitalisation, or surgery

### Third Degree (כוויה מדרגה שלישית) -- Full Thickness
- Extends through all skin layers to subcutaneous tissue
- Appears white, grey, yellow-brown, or black with stiff, dry texture
- Tissue may be exposed underneath
- Requires immediate professional medical care, hospitalisation, and surgical intervention

## Step-by-Step First Aid Protocol

### 1. Ensure Safety and Remove from Source
- Remove the child from the heat source immediately
- For fire: roll the child on the floor and extinguish flames with cloth
- For electrical burns: use a non-conductive object (such as a broom handle) to separate the child from the source before touching them
- For chemical burns: remove contaminated clothing carefully

### 2. Remove Clothing
- Remove saturated clothing immediately -- hot liquid absorbed in fabric continues damaging skin longer than direct contact
- Gently remove affected clothing; use scissors if necessary
- If clothing adheres to the skin without causing further damage risk, LEAVE IT ON -- do not force removal
- Remove constrictive items (shoes, watches, jewellery, rings) from the burn area and surrounding region to maintain blood circulation before swelling occurs

### 3. Cool the Burn
- **Rinse the affected area under cool (lukewarm, NOT ice-cold) running water for a minimum of 20 minutes**
- Hebrew: "קירור האזור לאורך זמן ממושך מאט את התקדמות הכוויה ומונע את העמקתה" (extended cooling slows burn progression and prevents deepening)
- The 20-minute cooling period is critical -- this is the single most important first aid intervention
- Ministry of Health specifies "lukewarm running water for 20 minutes" (מים פושרים)
- For extensive burns or if continuous water causes discomfort to the child, after several minutes switch to cold-soaked cloths/bandages applied to the burn area, changing them every few minutes to maintain cooling effect

### CHILD-SPECIFIC COOLING CONSIDERATIONS
- Children are more susceptible to hypothermia from prolonged cooling
- Cool the BURN, not the child -- avoid immersing the whole child in cold water
- Monitor for shivering, which indicates the child is getting too cold
- For infants and small children, keep the rest of the body warm while cooling the burn
- Smaller body surface area in children means burns cover a proportionally larger percentage of total body surface area -- this increases hypothermia risk

### 4. Cover the Burn
- After cooling, cover the burn with sterile, loosely-applied bandages or a clean, dry sheet
- Use material that does NOT stick to skin (avoid gauze pads and cotton wool, which adhere to damaged skin)
- Wrap the bandage in water-soaked cloth if no sterile dressing is available
- Continue moistening the dressing until arrival at hospital (Maccabi guidance)

### 5. Pain Management
- Administer appropriate pain relief medication (ibuprofen or paracetamol/acetaminophen)
- Dose by weight for children
- Do not delay pain relief

### 6. Hydration
- Offer water in small sips to prevent dehydration
- Especially important for children with larger or multiple burns

### 7. Call 101 (MDA)
- Call MDA immediately for guidance, especially for serious burns
- MDA dispatchers can guide through first aid steps while dispatching an ambulance

## What NOT to Do (אסור)

- **Do NOT use ice or ice-cold water** -- can damage tissues and blood vessels, worsening the injury
- **Do NOT apply toothpaste, coffee grounds, raw eggs, yogurt, or butter** -- these create a heat-trapping layer and increase infection risk
- **Do NOT puncture or pop blisters** -- the fluid inside protects tissue during healing and prevents infection
- **Do NOT apply ointments, oils, powders, or similar substances** to serious burns before medical evaluation
- **Do NOT use gauze pads or cotton wool** directly on burns (fibres adhere to damaged skin)
- **Do NOT leave the child unattended** if unconscious -- call 101 immediately

## When to Seek Immediate Medical Help (Call 101)

Call MDA (101) or go to the emergency department immediately for:

- **Burns on sensitive areas**: face, eyes, mouth, tongue, hands, feet, genitals, groin, or inner thigh
- **Circumferential burns**: burns encircling an entire limb
- **Burns appearing white, grey, or black** (indicating third-degree burn)
- **Multiple large blisters**
- **Extensive burns** covering a large area of the body
- **Burns to the neck, face, or breathing airways** -- immediate risk of death from airway compromise
- **Electrical burns** -- always require medical evaluation (internal damage may not be visible)
- **Chemical burns**
- **All burns in infants under 1 year of age** -- require immediate medical attention
- **Any burn in a child** that appears worse than a minor first-degree burn

### High-Risk Populations Requiring Immediate Medical Attention
- Infants under 1 year
- Children with immunocompromised conditions
- Children receiving chemotherapy

## Post-Burn Care (Minor Burns Only)

For mild, first-degree burns that do not require emergency care:
- Apply relief creams after cooling: trolamine (Biafine), bepanthen, or similar
- Over-the-counter hydrogel or vaseline-based pads may be used for mild-to-moderate localised burns
- Monitor for signs of infection: increased redness, swelling, fever, pus

## Complications to Watch For

### Infection
- Local infection: requires intensive bandage changes with antibacterial agents plus antibiotics
- Systemic infection (sepsis): presents with fever, lethargy, and potential life-threatening shock
- Children are at higher risk for infection from burns

### Scarring
- Second and third-degree burns typically scar
- Treatment options: sun protection, silicone strips, massage, steroid injections, surgery, laser therapy
- Scars in children may require ongoing management as the child grows

## Prevention -- Israeli Context

The Ministry of Health emphasises prevention as the most effective strategy:

### Kitchen Safety
- Cook on back burners with pot handles turned inward
- Never hold an infant while cooking
- Keep hot beverages away from table edges
- Avoid using tablecloths that children can pull
- Position hot items toward the centre of the table
- Maintain constant supervision during food preparation

### Bathroom Safety
- Measure water temperature at 37 degrees C before bathing
- Install temperature-limiting devices capping water at 49 degrees C
- Prevent children from playing with faucets

### Home Safety
- Install smoke detectors
- Secure heating devices away from flammable materials
- Use electrical outlet covers
- Keep matches, lighters, and candles inaccessible to children

### Israel-Specific Considerations
- **Climate**: Israel's hot climate increases risk of sunburn (a form of thermal burn); ensure sun protection for children
- **Hot water systems**: Solar water heaters (dud shemesh / דוד שמש) common in Israeli homes can produce extremely hot water, especially in summer -- always check water temperature before bathing children
- **Shabbat and holiday candles**: Common household burn risk for young children; keep candles out of reach
- **Electric kettles and finjan**: Hot beverage preparation is frequent; keep cords and hot containers away from counter edges
