## Asthma Protocol (from Ichud Hatzalah)

### Definition
Asthma is a chronic, obstructive, episodic respiratory disease characterised by inflammatory reactions in the lungs when exposed to triggering factors. Marked by airway hyperresponsiveness, bronchial oedema, mucus secretion, and reduced airflow.

### Prevalence
- Most common chronic disease in children
- In Israel, cumulative prevalence estimated at **5--10% among children**
- Prevalence decreases with age into adulthood

### Common Triggers
- Allergens (pollen, pet dander, dust mites)
- Air pollution and cigarette smoke
- Weather changes
- Physical exertion
- Stress and anxiety
- Certain medications (NSAIDs, aspirin)
- Food additives (tartrazine/E102)
- Cold air exposure

### Pathophysiology
Exposure to triggering factors activates mast cells, which release histamine and inflammatory mediators, causing bronchial constriction, mucus production, and airway narrowing.

### Symptoms During an Attack
- Persistent cough (often the initial sign)
- Dyspnoea (difficulty breathing, especially exhaling)
- Prolonged expiration
- Wheezing sounds
- Lip cyanosis from oxygen deficiency
- Tachycardia and sweating
- Accessory muscle use
- Drowsiness (from CO2 buildup -- late and dangerous sign)

### Attack Severity Levels

**Mild--Moderate**
- Difficulty during exertion
- SpO2 >94%
- Normal to elevated respiration rate

**Severe**
- Difficulty at rest
- Inability to complete sentences
- SpO2 <92% on oxygen
- Rapid breathing (>25/min in adults; higher thresholds apply for children)

**Life-Threatening (Status Asthmaticus)**
- Severe hypoxia
- CO2 retention
- Respiratory acidosis
- May require mechanical ventilation

### First Aid Treatment (BLS Level)
1. Position patient upright to maximise lung expansion
2. Administer high-concentration oxygen (10 L/min) if available
3. Calm and reassure the patient
4. Obtain detailed medical history
5. Assist with prescribed rescue inhaler use
6. Monitor for worsening symptoms
7. Transport to medical facility if symptoms do not improve

### Medication (ALS Level -- for reference)
- **Ventolin (salbutamol)**: Beta-2 agonist causing bronchial dilation
- **Atrovent (ipratropium)**: Anticholinergic blocking parasympathetic activity
- **Solumedrol (methylprednisolone)**: Corticosteroid reducing inflammation
- **Adrenaline (epinephrine)**: For severe attacks
- **Magnesium sulfate**: Smooth muscle relaxant for severe cases
- **IV fluids**: Combat dehydration from prolonged breathing effort

### Prevention Strategies
- Remove carpeting from home
- Maintain humidity below 50%
- Use allergen-proof bedding
- Avoid pet exposure
- Minimise smoke and pollution exposure
- Avoid aspirin in sensitive individuals
- Consider air filters for cold-weather exercise

### Timing
Attacks frequently occur early morning or late evening with sudden onset and sensation of choking.

## Child-Specific Considerations (from Paediatric pages)

### Airway Differences in Children
- Children have anatomically smaller airways, more prone to obstruction
- Tongue is large relative to mouth and can block airways
- Airway structure is softer and more easily compromised by improper positioning
- Infants are obligate nasal breathers initially

### Assessment in Children
- Children may not describe breathing difficulty -- observe for:
  - Visible respiratory distress
  - Intercostal retractions
  - Nasal flaring
  - Pale skin
  - Weakness and lethargy
- Stridor (noisy breathing from upper airway narrowing) is common in children 3 months to 5 years

### Approach
- Allow children to sit with parents during assessment
- Introduce yourself by name; ask the child their name
- Use age-appropriate explanations
- Never lie to the child about painful procedures
- Start with cooperative manoeuvres before invasive procedures

### Spacer Use (from general paediatric asthma guidance)
- Children under 6 should use inhaler with spacer device
- Spacer allows medication to be delivered more effectively to small airways
- In gan/school context, caregivers should know child's asthma action plan
