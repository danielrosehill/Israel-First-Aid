## Protocol: Severe Bleeding in Children (United Hatzalah Context)

United Hatzalah's hemorrhage control approach follows the same escalation ladder as their adult protocol, with pediatric considerations applied:

### Escalation Order

1. **Direct Pressure** (primary method)
2. **Israeli Bandage / Pressure Dressing**
3. **Wound Packing** (for junctional wounds -- head, shoulder, groin, axilla)
4. **Pressure Points** (adjunct)
5. **Tourniquet** (when other methods fail)

This escalation order is the same as the adult protocol. United Hatzalah includes wound packing and pressure points in their escalation, which MDA's civilian-facing guidance does not emphasise as strongly.

### Pediatric Adaptations

The following adaptations apply when treating children:

**Direct Pressure:**
- Same technique as adults. One adult hand may cover the entire wound on a small child.
- Maintain continuous pressure -- do not check under the dressing.

**Israeli Bandage:**
- Standard Israeli bandages fit children aged approximately 6+.
- On smaller children, the elastic wrap may need to be folded or doubled to avoid covering too large an area.
- **Verify distal pulse** -- mandatory in children, as the elastic component more easily occludes smaller arteries.

**Wound Packing:**
- United Hatzalah's "sunburst" packing technique applies to children for junctional wounds (groin, neck, axilla) where tourniquets cannot be used.
- Use smaller amounts of gauze proportional to wound size.
- **Contraindications remain the same**: Do not pack penetrating stab wounds; do not enlarge wound openings.

**Pressure Points:**
- Same locations as adults (carotid, subclavian, brachial, femoral, radial-ulnar).
- Easier to compress in children due to smaller limb size and less muscle/fat tissue.

**Tourniquet:**
- CAT Gen 7 is effective for children aged 2+ with limb circumference 13 cm+.
- Apply 5-10 cm above the wound.
- Tighten until bleeding stops completely.
- Record time of application.
- Do not remove without paramedic/physician authorisation.

### Shock Monitoring

United Hatzalah's adult protocol emphasises that **tachycardia should not be attributed to anxiety or pain alone** -- this is even more critical in children:

- Tachycardia is the earliest reliable indicator of blood loss in children.
- Children compensate for blood loss longer than adults, then decompensate rapidly.
- Hypotension is a late, pre-arrest sign in children.
- **Do not wait for additional shock signs** -- call 101 and transport immediately.

## Comparison: United Hatzalah vs MDA (Pediatric Bleeding)

| Aspect | United Hatzalah | MDA |
|--------|----------------|-----|
| **Separate pediatric protocol** | Not published | Not published |
| **Escalation order** | Direct pressure -> Bandage -> Packing -> Pressure points -> Tourniquet | Direct pressure -> Bandage -> Tourniquet |
| **Wound packing for children** | Same technique as adult, with size adaptation | Not emphasised in civilian guidance |
| **Pressure points** | Explicitly listed | Less emphasis in civilian materials |
| **Tourniquet guidance** | Same as adult | Same as adult |
| **Shock recognition** | Strongly emphasised (do not attribute tachycardia to anxiety) | Standard shock monitoring |

**Synthesis**: Both organisations use fundamentally the same approach for pediatric hemorrhage control. Neither publishes a dedicated pediatric protocol for public use. United Hatzalah's more detailed escalation (including wound packing and pressure points) is aimed at trained volunteers/EMTs rather than lay rescuers. **MDA remains the authoritative default** per project convention.

## United Hatzalah Family Safety Course

United Hatzalah offers a **Family Safety Course** designed for parents, grandparents, teachers, and babysitters. This course covers:

- CPR for infants and children
- Choking management for children
- Hemorrhage control
- Emergency response procedures

This is the primary channel through which United Hatzalah disseminates pediatric emergency training to civilians. The course content is not publicly available online but is delivered in-person.
