## Burn Classification

### First-Degree Burns (Superficial)
- Affects only the outer epidermis layer
- Skin appears red and slightly swollen, accompanied by pain but without blisters
- Healing time: 1--2 weeks, typically without scarring

### Second-Degree Burns (Partial Thickness)
Two subtypes:
- **Shallow second-degree:** Redness, severe pain, and fluid-filled blisters that may appear immediately or within hours. Heals in 10--14 days with complete recovery if no infection occurs. Typically no scarring for shallow burns.
- **Deep second-degree:** Takes 25--35 days to heal. Can progress to third-degree if infection develops. Deeper burns may scar.

### Third-Degree Burns (Full Thickness)
- Extends through all skin layers to subcutaneous tissue
- Tissue appears brown-white, grey, yellowish, or black in colour, with a stiff and dry texture
- When pressure is applied to the burn's crust, there is no whitening or redness of the tissue because blood vessels are destroyed or blocked
- Pain may be reduced or absent due to nerve destruction
- Requires immediate medical intervention

## First Aid Protocol -- Step by Step

### 1. Ensure Safety
- Remove the casualty from the heat source
- Ensure the scene is safe for the rescuer

### 2. Cool the Burn
- Cool the burn under cool (not cold) running water for at least 10 minutes
- Some Israeli sources recommend 10--20 minutes, until pain subsides
- Use cool/lukewarm water -- NOT ice or very cold water
- Begin cooling as soon as possible after the burn occurs

### 3. Remove Restrictive Items
- Before swelling begins, remove rings, bracelets, watches, and loose clothing from the affected area
- This prevents circulation problems as the area swells
- If clothing is stuck to the burn, cut around it -- do NOT pull it off

### 4. Cover the Burn
- Loosely cover the burn with cling film (food wrap) or a clean plastic bag
- Alternatively, use a clean, non-fluffy dressing
- Do not wrap tightly

### 5. Position the Patient
- Transfer the patient to a lying-down position to reduce shock damage
- Keep the patient warm (prevent hypothermia, especially with large burns)

### 6. Call Emergency Services (101)
Call MDA at 101 immediately if any of the following apply:
- Extensive burns with severe pain
- White, grey, or black discolouration of the burn
- Multiple blisters
- Burns on sensitive areas: face, mouth, tongue, hands, feet, genitals, or groin
- The burn is larger than the palm of the casualty's hand
- Chemical or electrical burns
- Inhalation injury (burns to airways)
- High-risk patients: infants under one year, pregnant women, elderly people, chemotherapy patients

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT apply ice or ice-cold water** -- extreme cold can cause additional tissue and blood vessel damage
- **Do NOT apply toothpaste, ground coffee, raw egg, yogurt, butter, or other home remedies** -- these create a heat-trapping layer and increase infection risk
- **Do NOT rupture blisters** -- the fluid inside protects the underlying tissue during healing
- **Do NOT use fat-based creams or ointments** on fresh burns
- **Do NOT remove clothing that is stuck to the burn** -- cut around it instead

## Chemical Burns

- Remove contaminated clothing carefully (protect yourself from the chemical)
- Rinse the affected area with a massive amount of cool running water for at least 15--20 minutes
- If chemical has splashed into the eyes, rinse and flush eyes thoroughly with running water for at least 15 minutes
- Do NOT attempt chemical neutralisation (do not apply an acid to a base burn or vice versa)
- Call 101 immediately -- all chemical burns require professional medical evaluation

---

## Electrical Burns

- Ensure the power source is disconnected before approaching the casualty
- Electrical burns may cause internal injuries not visible on the surface
- Call 101 immediately -- all electrical burns require professional medical evaluation
- Be prepared to perform CPR if the patient becomes unresponsive

## Burn Severity Thresholds Requiring Hospital Transport

### Severe Burns (emergency transport required)
- Second-degree burns covering more than 25% of body surface area (BSA) in adults
- Third-degree burns covering more than 10% BSA
- Burns to hands, face, eyes, ears, or genitals
- Inhalation injuries
- Chemical or electrical burns

### Moderate Burns
- Lower thresholds of the same categories

### Rule of Thumb
- Any burn larger than the palm of the patient's hand requires medical evaluation
- Burns affecting the face, joints, or genital area must be evaluated by a specialist

## Israel-Specific Context

### Climate Considerations
- Israel's hot climate increases dehydration risk in burn patients
- Sunburn is common; severe sunburn should be treated as a first-degree burn
- In desert/Negev areas, metal surfaces and sand can reach extreme temperatures

### Burn Care Infrastructure
Israel has five designated burn units at third-level trauma centres (appointed by the Ministry of Health):
1. Rambam Medical Center, Haifa
2. Rabin Medical Center, Petah Tikva
3. Sheba Medical Center, Tel HaShomer
4. Hadassah Medical Center, Jerusalem
5. Soroka Medical Center, Be'er Sheva

Statistics: Approximately 45,000 Israelis suffer burns annually, with 2,700 requiring hospitalisation. Most burns are household accidents caused by hot liquids involving less than 20% total body surface area.

## Pain Management (First Aid Level)

- Ibuprofen or acetaminophen (paracetamol) for mild-to-moderate burn pain
- For mild first-degree burns: aloe vera gel or non-fat-based preparations to soothe the burning sensation
- Specialised burn creams (e.g., Flaminal alginate gel, Silverol) may be applied until professional care is available

---

## Complications to Monitor

- Infection (redness, warmth, pus, fever)
- Respiratory issues (if inhalation injury suspected)
- Kidney dysfunction (in severe burns)
- Hypovolemic shock within 24--48 hours post-injury (in severe burns)
- Hypertrophic scarring and contracture (long-term)
