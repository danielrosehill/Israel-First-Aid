## Israeli Context

Israel has extensive experience with blast injuries from suicide bombings, rocket attacks, and terror incidents. MDA treated 541 blast victims across 93 mass-casualty events between 2000-2004 at Hadassah alone. On October 7, 2023, MDA mobilised 1,200 ambulances within 48 minutes and established 11 casualty treatment stations treating 306 victims.

Israel's civilian population is more likely to encounter blast injury scenarios than most countries. Tourniquet use and hemorrhage control are widely taught in civilian first aid courses.

## Blast Injury Classifications

Blast injuries result from four mechanisms, often occurring simultaneously in the same victim:

### Primary Blast Injury (Barotrauma)
- Caused by the blast wave (rapid changes in atmospheric pressure)
- Affects gas-containing organs: lungs, bowels, ears
- Can cause lung contusion, bowel perforation, tympanic membrane rupture
- Bowel perforations reported in up to 14% of primary blast casualties
- **Warning**: Small bowel perforations may present with delayed symptoms (up to 48 hours)

### Secondary Blast Injury (Penetrating)
- Caused by shrapnel, debris, and fragmentation projected by the explosion
- Penetrating injuries to solid organs and major vessels
- Most common mechanism of blast-related death and injury
- Similar to stab/gunshot wounds in treatment approach

### Tertiary Blast Injury (Blunt)
- Caused by body displacement from the expanding blast wave
- Blunt trauma to solid organs, fractures, head injuries
- Similar to blunt abdominal trauma / high-speed impact injuries

### Quaternary Blast Injury (Thermal/Other)
- Burns from the explosion's heat and flash
- Present in approximately 33% of ICU-admitted blast patients
- Includes smoke/dust inhalation

## MDA First Aid Protocol -- Bystander / Civilian Responder

### 1. Scene Safety -- FIRST PRIORITY
- **Do NOT approach** until security forces confirm the area is safe
- Secondary devices (second bombs) are a known tactic -- maintain distance
- Your safety comes first: "You can't help the victim if you are wounded"
- Be aware of structural collapse, fire, and ongoing threats

### 2. Call MDA -- 101
- Report: location, number of casualties, type of incident (explosion/blast)
- If multiple casualties, report this immediately so MDA can activate mass-casualty protocols

### 3. Hemorrhage Control -- LIFE-SAVING PRIORITY
MDA's approach is based on the "save and run" / "scoop and run" philosophy: perform only immediate life-saving treatment, then evacuate rapidly.

**The only actions performed at the scene are:**
- Hemorrhage control (bandages and tourniquets)
- Airway control (if trained)
- Needle decompression for tension pneumothorax (professional responders only)

#### Direct Pressure
- Apply hand or sterile gauze directly to the bleeding site
- Maintain continuous pressure to allow clot formation
- Transition to pressure bandaging (Israeli bandage) when possible

#### Israeli Bandage (Emergency Bandage)
- Dual-pad design creates direct pressure while wrapping around the wound
- Verify pulse distal to the bandage afterward to confirm no arterial blockage

#### Wound Packing
- For large wounds in limbs, shoulders, or groin
- Fill the wound with gauze to create internal pressure
- Secure with bandaging over the top

#### Tourniquet Application
- Apply when direct pressure fails or when circumstances prevent sustained pressure
- Apply when there is amputation, severe crushing injury, or uncontrollable hemorrhage
- Apply in multi-casualty situations where you cannot maintain pressure on every wound
- Place tourniquet above the injury site
- Tighten until bleeding stops
- **Document the time** of tourniquet application in an obvious location
- **Do NOT remove** once applied -- only medical professionals should make this decision

**See also**: `tourniquet-mda.md` for detailed tourniquet protocol.

### 4. Embedded Objects
- If a piece of shrapnel or debris is embedded in the body, **do NOT remove or move it**
- Apply pressure on either side of the object
- Stabilise the object in place if possible

### 5. Reassurance and Monitoring
- Speak to the victim, try to reassure them
- Do not let them move until MDA arrives
- Look for additional wounds beyond the obvious one -- blast victims often have multiple injury sites
- Monitor for signs of shock: rapid pulse, pale/cold skin, confusion

### 6. Burns (Quaternary Blast Injury)
- Cool burns with clean water if available
- Do not remove clothing stuck to burns
- Cover with clean, non-adherent dressing

## MDA Professional / EMT-Level Protocols

At casualty treatment stations during mass-casualty events, MDA teams perform:
- Tourniquet application for limb bleeding
- Intubation and airway control
- Needle decompression for tension pneumothorax
- IV line placement (14-18G for rapid fluid delivery)
- Administration of IV fluids
- Tranexamic acid (TXA)
- Freeze-dried plasma (FDP) and blood transfusions
- Ketamine for pain management
- Bag-valve-mask ventilation

## Key Statistics (Israeli Experience)

- 80% of blast victims aged 15-44 (reflecting targeting of public spaces)
- 86 of 101 ICU patients had combined injuries (multiple mechanisms)
- Head/facial injuries: 56 patients; chest injuries: 51 patients
- 46% required intubation in the emergency department
- Average ICU stay: 9 days (range 1-80 days)
- Mortality: 6.2% for blast victims vs 3% for other trauma

## Lessons and Warnings

- **Tourniquet duration matters**: During October 7, prolonged tourniquet application (due to inability to evacuate from active combat zones) led to limb ischemia requiring amputation in several cases
- **Rapid evacuation saves lives**: MDA's "scoop and run" approach proved effective in mass-casualty events
- **Expect multiple injuries**: Blast victims typically have injuries from more than one mechanism simultaneously
- **Delayed bowel perforation**: Monitor for abdominal symptoms up to 48 hours after blast exposure
