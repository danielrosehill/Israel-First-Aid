## Spinal Anatomy Overview (from United Hatzalah Article)

The spine comprises 33 vertebrae:
- 7 cervical (C1-C7) -- the most vulnerable segment
- 12 thoracic (T1-T12)
- 5 lumbar (L1-L5)
- 5 sacral (fused)
- 4 coccygeal (fused)

The spinal cord runs through the vertebral canal and transmits signals between the brain and the rest of the body. Spinal nerves exit through inter-vertebral foramina. Damage to the spinal cord can result in permanent paralysis depending on the level of injury.

## When Spinal Immobilisation is Required

United Hatzalah protocol mandates immobilisation when ANY of the following are present:

- Patient reports neck or back pain
- Tenderness during palpation of the spine
- Neurological deficits (numbness, weakness, tingling, paralysis)
- Relevant trauma mechanism (blunt force, fall from height, road accident, blast)
- Altered consciousness or reduced GCS
- Alcohol or drug impairment (cannot reliably report symptoms)
- Communication barriers (language, hearing, cognitive impairment)
- Distracting injuries present (painful injury elsewhere that may mask spinal pain)
- **Any remaining doubt after assessment**

**Key principle from the article:** "Standing or walking does NOT exclude spinal injury." An unstable fracture can exist without immediate neurological deficit.

---

## When Spinal Immobilisation May Be Ruled Out

United Hatzalah teaches that spinal injury can be ruled out (and immobilisation is NOT required) only when ALL of the following criteria are met:

- Patient is fully conscious, alert, and cooperative
- No alcohol or drug influence
- No language or communication barriers
- No pain, tenderness, or deformity upon palpation of the entire spine
- No sensory loss or limb weakness (neurological exam normal)
- No distracting injuries causing pain elsewhere
- The mechanism of injury does not suggest significant spinal risk

This approach is consistent with validated clinical decision rules (NEXUS criteria / Canadian C-Spine Rule) adapted for the prehospital setting.

**Documentation:** If spinal injury is ruled out after proper evaluation, the responder must document their findings and reasoning.

## Penetrating Trauma Exception

Per United Hatzalah protocol:
- Stab wounds or gunshot injuries do NOT require initial spinal immobilisation
- **Exceptions:** Immobilise if:
  - Additional blunt trauma occurred alongside the penetrating injury
  - Neurological deficits develop or are present

This is notable in the Israeli context where penetrating trauma from security incidents is a real consideration.

## Trauma Patient Assessment Protocol (SABCDE)

United Hatzalah uses a systematic approach to trauma assessment:

### Stage S -- Scene Safety

**Before arriving:**
- Assess weather (rain, extreme heat, fog -- all relevant in Israel)
- Traffic conditions and need for police
- Risk factors (smoke, chemicals, exposed electricity, debris)
- Scene type (explosion, stabbing, hazmat, vehicle accident)
- Number of casualties (potential mass casualty incident / MCI)

**Upon arrival:**
- Position vehicle safely with hazard lights
- Wear high-visibility vest
- Gloves for ALL patient contact
- Watch for glass, metal, and other hazards
- Never enter dangerous environments (fires, water, exposed electrical wires)

**Patient safety:** If the casualty is in immediate danger, move them minimally to a secure location. If active severe bleeding is observed, apply direct pressure while performing rapid assessment.

### Stage A -- Airway with C-Spine Protection

**Conscious patient:**
- Approach the patient's face to establish communication and eye contact
- This maintains alertness and provides reassurance
- Check for airway threats: fractured jaw, broken teeth, nasal or oral bleeding, swallowed hot vapours, tongue lacerations

**Unconscious patient:**
- Open airway using **jaw thrust technique** while maintaining spinal stabilisation
- Do NOT use head-tilt chin-lift (this moves the cervical spine)
- Jaw thrust may require two responders -- one maintains stabilisation while the other manages the airway

**Helmet removal (motorcycle riders):**
- Open the face shield first for communication and airway assessment
- Full helmet removal requires **two responders** -- a single responder cannot safely remove a helmet while maintaining C-spine control
- Do NOT remove the helmet unless airway management requires it

### Stage C -- Cervical Spine Stabilisation

**When to stabilise:**
Any trauma casualty with a significant injury mechanism or signs/symptoms suggesting spinal injury should be treated as a suspected spinal injury until ruled out by hospital evaluation.

**Manual stabilisation technique:**
1. Position yourself at the patient's head
2. Place hands on either side of the head
3. Hold the head in neutral alignment with the body
4. This position also gives you full visualisation of the patient for ABC assessment
5. Apply cervical collar if available
6. Proceed to complete spinal immobilisation on a backboard with head blocks when equipment is available

**Core principle (quoted from article):** "Unnecessary movement may cause neurological damage or worsen existing injury."

### Stage D -- Disability / Neurological Assessment

During the first assessment cycle at stage D:
- Physical examination to check for spinal tenderness along the entire spine
- Neurological deficit testing:
  - Can the patient feel sensations in all four limbs?
  - Can the patient move all four limbs?
  - Compare strength left vs right
  - Note any asymmetry

## Head Injury Management

### Assessment

For every head injury patient, United Hatzalah protocol requires:
- Full spinal immobilisation (collar + backboard) as head injury and spinal injury frequently co-occur
- Glasgow Coma Scale assessment
- Pupil size and reactivity check
- Monitoring for signs of raised intracranial pressure

### Signs Requiring Immediate Evacuation

- Loss of consciousness lasting more than 30 seconds
- Progressive deterioration in consciousness level
- Intense headache worsening over time
- Repeated or projectile vomiting
- Blood or clear fluid from nose or ears
- Unequal pupils
- Seizures following head trauma
- Weakness or paralysis on one side

### Concussion Assessment

- Headache, confusion, dizziness
- Nausea
- Balance problems
- Sensitivity to light and noise
- Difficulty concentrating
- Memory problems (cannot recall the event)
- Fatigue, drowsiness
- Emotional changes

**Note:** Loss of consciousness is NOT required for a diagnosis of concussion. Symptoms may be delayed by hours.

## Resuscitation in Trauma (Head and Spinal Injury Context)

If a head/spinal injury patient goes into cardiac arrest:

- **Control haemorrhage first** -- before initiating CPR
  - Tourniquet for limb bleeding (preferred over direct pressure -- faster and more reliable per United Hatzalah protocol)
  - Direct pressure for neck/throat bleeding
- Follow **CAB** sequence (Circulation, Airway, Breathing)
- Use **jaw thrust** (not head tilt) for airway to protect cervical spine
- Ventilation ideally requires two responders: one maintains jaw thrust, second operates bag-valve-mask
- **Epinephrine is generally avoided** in hypovolemic trauma cardiac arrest (vasoconstriction worsens perfusion when blood volume is critically low) -- this is an ALS-level consideration
- Target minimum systolic BP of **100 mmHg if head injury present** (higher than the standard 90 mmHg target for other trauma)
- **Hypothermia prevention is critical** -- remove wet clothing, provide thermal protection

## Differences from MDA Protocol

Both organisations agree on the fundamental principles. Notable points:

| Topic | MDA | United Hatzalah |
|-------|-----|-----------------|
| Primary authority | National statutory EMS | Volunteer supplementary EMS |
| Immobilisation approach | Full immobilisation for all suspected spinal injuries | Same, with explicit criteria for ruling out (NEXUS-style) |
| Penetrating trauma | Immobilisation not required unless neurological deficit | Same approach |
| Airway in spinal injury | Jaw thrust | Jaw thrust (explicit that head-tilt is contraindicated) |
| Blood pressure target (head injury) | Not specified in accessible civilian materials | 100 mmHg systolic minimum |
| Tourniquet preference | Taught in civilian courses | Explicitly preferred over direct pressure for limb bleeding |

**No substantive disagreements were identified.** Both follow international best practice adapted for the Israeli context.

## Israeli Context Notes

### Common Scenarios for United Hatzalah Responders

- **Road accidents:** Ambucycle responders frequently arrive first at RTAs; spinal precautions are standard for any significant mechanism
- **Security incidents:** Blast injuries, stabbings, shootings -- penetrating trauma exception for immobilisation is clinically important in this context
- **Construction and industrial:** Falls from height are common; always assume spinal injury
- **Hiking and outdoor:** Desert wadis, cliff falls, dehydration-related collapses

### Environmental Considerations

- **Heat:** Shade the patient, prevent hyperthermia, but maintain immobilisation
- **Remote locations:** Longer evacuation times in Negev, Judean Desert, and Golan -- maintain stabilisation patiently
- **Mass casualty events:** Triage protocols may apply; spinal injury with intact vitals is typically "urgent" (yellow tag)
