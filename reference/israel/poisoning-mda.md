## Routes of Poisoning

Poisoning can occur through any of the following routes:

1. **Ingestion** -- swallowing of toxic substances (medications, cleaning products, chemicals, plants, contaminated food)
2. **Inhalation** -- breathing in toxic fumes or gases (cleaning product fumes, carbon monoxide, industrial chemicals, smoke)
3. **Skin/mucous membrane contact** -- absorption through skin or eyes (agricultural chemicals, industrial solvents, strong acids/bases)
4. **Injection** -- introduction of toxins directly into the body (venomous bites/stings, intravenous drug use)

---

## Common Poison Sources in Israel

- Medications and pharmaceuticals (the most common cause of poisoning in Israel -- approximately 49% of poison centre calls)
- Cleaning products and household chemicals (approximately 36% of calls)
- Agricultural chemicals and pesticides
- Industrial solvents and chemicals
- Poisonous plants and mushrooms (seasonal risk, especially wild mushrooms in winter)
- Venomous animals: Palestine viper (*Vipera palaestinae*), deathstalker scorpion (*Leiurus quinquestriatus*), Mediterranean recluse spider (*Loxosceles rufescens*)
- Carbon monoxide (from gas heaters, particularly in winter)
- Food poisoning

## Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms vary depending on the substance, amount, route of exposure, and individual factors (age, weight, health status). General indicators include:

### Mild Symptoms
- Mouth or throat irritation
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain or cramping
- Blurred vision
- Unusual taste or smell on breath

### Severe Symptoms
- Confusion, drowsiness, or altered consciousness
- Heart palpitations or arrhythmias
- Breathing difficulty or respiratory distress
- Restlessness or agitation
- Seizures
- Loss of consciousness
- Cyanosis (bluish skin)
- Chemical burns around mouth, lips, or skin

## First Aid Protocol -- Step by Step

### 1. Ensure Scene Safety
- Assess the scene before approaching
- For inhalation poisoning: do NOT enter a confined space with potential toxic fumes without proper protection
- Protect yourself from contamination

### 2. Call for Help
- Call **101** (MDA) immediately for any serious poisoning or if the patient is unconscious, having difficulty breathing, or seizing
- Call the **Israel Poison Information Center at 04-7771900** for guidance on specific substances and treatment
- Be prepared to provide: the substance involved, estimated amount, time of exposure, patient's age and weight, and current symptoms

### 3. Preserve the Substance
- **Collect and preserve the container, packaging, or remaining substance** -- bring it to the hospital or have it available for emergency responders
- This is critical for identification and appropriate treatment
- If the patient has vomited, preserve a sample for analysis if possible

### 4. Ingestion Poisoning

**Do NOT induce vomiting** unless specifically directed to do so by the Poison Information Center or a physician. Reasons:
- Vomiting is often ineffective at removing the substance
- Research shows contraindications in approximately one-third of cases
- Caustic substances (acids, bases) cause additional burns to the oesophagus and airway on the way back up
- Risk of aspiration (inhaling vomit into the lungs)

**For caustic/chemical ingestions (acids, bases, detergents):**
- NEVER induce vomiting
- If the patient is conscious and breathing normally, give water or milk to drink: approximately 1 cup (250 ml) for adults
- This helps dilute the substance
- Seek immediate medical care

**For other ingestions:**
- If poison remains in the mouth, attempt careful removal
- Do NOT give activated charcoal unless directed by medical professionals (effective only within 1--2 hours and only for certain substances)
- Do NOT give food or drink unless directed by the Poison Information Center

### 5. Inhalation Poisoning

- **Move the patient to fresh air immediately** -- remove from the contaminated area
- Do NOT enter the area yourself if there is a risk of toxic gas exposure (wait for trained responders with protective equipment)
- If the patient is not breathing, begin CPR
- **Never mix bleach (Economica) with other cleaning products**, especially acidic substances -- this releases toxic chlorine gas
- Call 101

### 6. Skin or Eye Contact Poisoning

- **Remove contaminated clothing** immediately (protect yourself while doing so)
- **Flush the affected skin** with large amounts of cool running water for at least 15--20 minutes
- **For eye exposure:** Rinse and flush eyes thoroughly with running water for at least 15 minutes, holding eyelids open
- Do NOT attempt to chemically neutralise the substance (do not apply an acid to a base exposure or vice versa)

### 7. Monitor the Patient

- If the patient is unconscious but breathing, place in the **recovery position** (lateral/side-lying position) to prevent aspiration
- Monitor breathing and pulse continuously
- Be prepared to perform CPR if breathing or heartbeat stops
- Keep the patient warm and calm
- Do NOT leave the patient alone

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT induce vomiting** unless specifically directed by the Poison Information Center or a physician
- **Do NOT give food or drink** unless directed by medical professionals
- **Do NOT attempt to neutralise the poison** (e.g., giving acid for base ingestion)
- **Do NOT use home remedies** (salt water, mustard, raw eggs, etc.)
- **Do NOT give activated charcoal** without medical direction
- **Do NOT enter a confined space** with potential toxic fumes without proper protection
- **Do NOT delay calling for help** -- even if symptoms appear mild, they can deteriorate rapidly

---

## Recovery Position

If the patient is unconscious but breathing:
1. Kneel beside the patient
2. Place the arm nearest to you at a right angle to the body
3. Bring the far arm across the chest and hold the back of the hand against the near cheek
4. Pull the far knee up and roll the patient towards you onto their side
5. Adjust the top leg so the knee is at a right angle
6. Tilt the head back slightly to keep the airway open
7. Monitor breathing continuously

## When to Call 101 (MDA) Immediately

- Patient is unconscious or has altered consciousness
- Difficulty breathing or respiratory distress
- Seizures
- Ingestion of caustic/corrosive substances
- Ingestion of unknown substances
- Large quantity of any substance ingested
- Suicidal ingestion (intentional overdose)
- Any poisoning in a patient with underlying medical conditions
- Carbon monoxide exposure
- Symptoms are worsening

## Israel-Specific Context

### Israel Poison Information Center (Rambam)
- The national poison centre at Rambam Health Care Campus in Haifa is available 24/7 at **04-7771900**
- Staffed by physicians with expertise in internal medicine, paediatrics, and clinical toxicology, plus specialised nurses
- Handles more than 31,000 calls annually
- Provides guidance on clinical toxicology, drug information, and reproductive toxicology (substance exposure during pregnancy)
- Drug information and pregnancy/lactation consultations also available Sunday--Thursday, 09:00--15:00 at *5324 or 04-7773568

### Climate Considerations
- Israel's hot climate increases dehydration risk, which can worsen the effects of some poisons
- Heat accelerates chemical reactions and increases the volatility of some toxic substances
- Cleaning product fumes are more dangerous in poorly ventilated spaces during summer

### Seasonal Risks
- **Winter:** Wild mushroom poisoning (the Poison Information Center regularly issues warnings about this); carbon monoxide poisoning from gas heaters in inadequately ventilated rooms
- **Passover (Pesach):** Cleaning product poisoning increases by approximately 80% due to intensive household cleaning (mixing products, exposure to concentrated chemicals)
- **Agriculture:** Pesticide exposure, particularly in agricultural communities

### Common Household Hazards
- Mixing bleach (Economica / "ekonomika") with acidic cleaning products releases toxic chlorine gas
- Descaling agents (kettle cleaners) -- if diluted in kettle water the concentration is reduced and risk is lower, but undiluted ingestion requires immediate medical attention
- Medication storage -- keep all medications locked and out of reach

## Antidotes (Hospital-Level, for Reference)

Specific antidotes exist for certain poisonings and are administered by medical professionals:
- **Naloxone** -- for opioid overdose
- **Flumazenil** -- for benzodiazepine overdose
- **Atropine** -- for organophosphate (pesticide) poisoning
- **N-acetylcysteine (NAC)** -- for paracetamol/acetaminophen overdose

First aiders should NOT administer antidotes. This information is included for awareness only.
