## Content from United Hatzalah Sources

### Definition and Mechanism

Shock occurs when there is an impairment in the ability of blood to perfuse (supply oxygen to) body tissues. This can result from damage to any of three components:

1. **Blood volume** — the fluid
2. **Blood vessel tone** — the container
3. **Cardiac output** — the pump

Early identification of shock and immediate transport to hospital can save the patient's life.

### Three Stages of Shock

United Hatzalah describes shock progression in three stages:

**1. Compensated Stage**
- Body activates the sympathetic nervous system
- Catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine) are released
- Heart rate increases, peripheral vessels constrict
- Breathing rate increases
- Urine output decreases
- Digestive system slows
- **First sign: rapid pulse (tachycardia)**

**2. Uncompensated (Decompensated) Stage**
- Blood pressure begins falling
- Rapid and severe deterioration
- Acidosis develops, reducing effectiveness of medications
- Patient condition worsens quickly

**3. Washout (Irreversible) Stage**
- Peripheral blood containing toxins and lactic acid floods central circulation
- **Typically fatal**

At the cellular level, the body shifts from aerobic to anaerobic metabolism during shock, producing lactic acid that damages cells and can cause irreversible organ damage.

### Clinical Signs and Symptoms

From the United Hatzalah hypovolemic shock article:

- **Rapid pulse** (over 100 bpm in adults; lower thresholds apply in children where normal resting rates are higher)
- **Rapid breathing** (body attempts to eliminate excess acid and compensate for oxygen deficit)
- **Thirst and dry mucous membranes**
- **Altered mental status** — anxiety, confusion, lethargy
- **Pale skin** from vasoconstriction
- **Cold, clammy perspiration**
- **Anxiety and restlessness**

### Blood Loss Classification (Adult Reference Table)

United Hatzalah provides the following classification (adult values):

| Level | Blood Loss | % Lost | Pulse | Blood Pressure | Respirations | Mental Status |
|-------|-----------|--------|-------|----------------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | <750 mL | <15% | <100 | Normal | 14-20 | Mild anxiety |
| 2 | 750-1500 mL | 15-30% | 100-120 | Normal | 20-30 | Increased anxiety |
| 3 | 1500-2000 mL | 30-40% | 120-140 | Declining | 30-40 | Anxious/confused |
| 4 | >2000 mL | >40% | >140 | Declining | >35 | Confused/lethargic |

**Note:** These are adult values. In children, the absolute volumes that cause each stage are much smaller due to lower total blood volume.

### Pediatric-Specific Information

United Hatzalah explicitly highlights:

- **Children have significantly lower blood volumes:**
  - Newborn (~3 kg): approximately 240 mL total blood
  - One-year-old (~10 kg): approximately 800 mL total blood
- **"Each drop of fluid is critical in children"**
- Children may appear relatively stable during compensated shock, then **deteriorate rapidly without warning**
- **Normal blood pressure does NOT exclude shock** — the child may still be in the compensated stage

### Critical Clinical Principle

United Hatzalah emphasises: "Normal blood pressure does not exclude shock — the patient may still be in the compensated stage." This is especially important for children, who maintain blood pressure through strong compensatory mechanisms until they suddenly decompensate.

## Comparison with MDA

United Hatzalah's educational content on shock is **consistent with MDA standards**. Both organisations teach according to Israeli Ministry of Health protocols. Key points of agreement:

- Same three-stage model of shock progression
- Same emphasis on tachycardia as earliest sign
- Same warning about children's ability to compensate and then crash suddenly
- Same treatment priorities: call emergency services, control bleeding, position, keep warm, monitor

No material disagreements were identified between United Hatzalah and MDA sources on this topic.

## Gaps and Limitations
