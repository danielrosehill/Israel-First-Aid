## 1. Background: What is Diabetes?

Diabetes (Diabetes Mellitus) is a metabolic disease characterised by elevated blood glucose levels. It is the most common endocrine disorder.

### Type 1 (IDDM -- Insulin-Dependent Diabetes Mellitus)
- Results from insufficient insulin production by the pancreas.
- Treatment: insulin injections.

### Type 2 (NIDDM -- Non-Insulin-Dependent Diabetes Mellitus)
- Caused by decreased receptor sensitivity or reduced receptor quantity for insulin.
- Treatment: weight management, oral medications, sometimes insulin.

## 2. Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar / Nefillat Sukkar)

### Definition
Blood glucose drops below approximately 60-70 mg/dL. This is the MORE IMMEDIATELY DANGEROUS of the two diabetic emergencies.

### Common Causes
- Excess insulin relative to food intake
- Skipping or delaying a meal
- Excessive physical activity without compensating food intake
- Taking too much diabetes medication

### Signs and Symptoms
**Rapid onset** -- symptoms develop quickly:
- Rapid deterioration of consciousness
- Behavioural changes: anger, confusion, irritability, restlessness
- Weakness and trembling/tremors
- Hunger
- Headache
- Seizures/convulsions
- Rapid pulse (tachycardia)
- Sweating (diaphoresis)
- Pallor (pale skin)
- Anxiety

### First Aid Treatment

#### If the patient is CONSCIOUS and able to swallow:
1. Give sugar immediately -- rapidly absorbed forms:
   - Sweetened drink (fruit juice, sugary soft drink -- NOT diet)
   - Sugar cubes or glucose tablets
   - Honey or jam
   - Sweet food (fruit, sandwich)
   - **Important**: Choose foods/drinks WITHOUT added fat (fat slows sugar absorption)
2. If symptoms improve, follow up with a complex carbohydrate (bread, crackers) to maintain blood sugar levels.
3. Monitor the patient and do not leave them alone.
4. If symptoms do not improve within 10-15 minutes, call 101 (MDA).

#### If the patient has REDUCED CONSCIOUSNESS (confused, drowsy, but partially responsive):
- If safe to do so, apply a sweet paste (honey, chocolate spread) under the tongue or to the inside of the cheek. This allows absorption through the oral mucosa without swallowing risk.
- Call 101 (MDA) immediately.

#### If the patient is UNCONSCIOUS:
- **DO NOT give food or drink** -- risk of choking/aspiration.
- **DO NOT attempt to give insulin** -- insulin will further lower blood sugar.
- Place patient in the recovery position (on their side) to maintain an open airway.
- Call 101 (MDA) immediately.
- If the patient has a glucagon injection kit and you are trained to use it, administer glucagon.
- If the patient has an insulin pump, disconnect it gently (to stop further insulin delivery).
- Monitor breathing; be prepared to perform CPR if breathing stops.
- Wait for MDA paramedics, who can administer IV dextrose (glucose).

## 3. Hyperglycemia (High Blood Sugar / Aliyyat Sukkar)

### Definition
Blood glucose rises significantly above normal levels (typically >250 mg/dL). Can progress to diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) in Type 1, or hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS) in Type 2.

### Common Causes
- Eating too much food
- Taking too little medication or skipping doses
- Illness or infection
- Stress
- Insufficient physical activity

### Signs and Symptoms
**Gradual onset** -- symptoms develop over hours to days:
- Excessive thirst (polydipsia) and dry mouth
- Frequent urination (polyuria)
- Acetone/fruity breath odour (sign of ketoacidosis)
- Fast, deep breathing (Kussmaul breathing)
- Weakness, fatigue
- Dry mucous membranes
- Nausea and vomiting
- Warm, dry skin
- Rapid pulse (tachycardia) and low blood pressure
- Confusion progressing to loss of consciousness

### First Aid Treatment
1. **Call 101 (MDA) immediately** if diabetic ketoacidosis or severe hyperglycemia is suspected.
2. Ensure the airway is open and clear.
3. Loosen tight clothing that may restrict breathing.
4. Allow the patient to drink water if conscious (to combat dehydration).
5. **DO NOT administer insulin** -- insulin dosing requires medical expertise and equipment. Leave this to MDA paramedics/physicians.
6. If the patient loses consciousness, place in the recovery position.
7. Monitor breathing; be prepared to perform CPR.

## 4. When in Doubt: Sugar is Safer

**Critical MDA principle**: If you are unsure whether the patient has high or low blood sugar, treat for HYPOGLYCEMIA (give sugar). The rationale:
- Giving sugar to a hypoglycaemic patient can be life-saving.
- Giving sugar to a hyperglycaemic patient will not cause significant immediate harm (the existing hyperglycemia is already dangerous and additional sugar is marginal).
- Withholding sugar from a hypoglycaemic patient can result in brain damage or death.

---

## 5. General Protocol Notes

- **Check blood sugar** in all suspected diabetic emergencies if a glucometer is available.
- **Rule out hypoglycemia** when stroke is suspected -- symptoms can overlap (confusion, weakness, altered speech). A glucose test can differentiate.
- **Do NOT give insulin** as a first aider -- this is a paramedic/physician intervention.
- **Sharps safety**: If the patient has used needles/syringes, handle with care and dispose in a sharps container.
- **Medical identification**: Look for a medical alert bracelet, necklace, or card in the patient's wallet that may confirm diabetes diagnosis and medications.

---

## 6. Israel-Specific Context

- **Emergency number**: 101 (MDA ambulance dispatch)
- **Climate consideration**: Dehydration from Israel's hot climate can worsen both hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia. Ensure hydration.
- **Insulin storage**: In Israeli summer heat, insulin left in a vehicle or bag can degrade. If a patient's insulin pump or pen has been exposed to extreme heat, it may not be effective.
- **Glucometers**: MDA volunteers and United Hatzalah responders carry glucometers as standard equipment.
