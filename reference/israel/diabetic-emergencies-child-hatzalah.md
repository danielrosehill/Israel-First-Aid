## Background: Diabetes in Children

### Type 1 Diabetes (סוכרת סוג 1)

An autoimmune disease in which the immune system attacks the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. It develops rapidly and is characterised by hyperglycemia events and rapid metabolic acidosis. The genetic component is weaker than Type 2 diabetes. This is the most common form of diabetes in children.

### Type 2 Diabetes (סוכרת סוג 2)

A metabolic disorder in which cells develop insulin resistance. Initially the pancreas produces excess insulin without hypoglycemic episodes due to tissue resistance. Over time, the pancreas "tires" and reduces baseline function, requiring medication. Type 2 has a stronger genetic component and develops more slowly. Less common in children but increasing.

## Warning Signs of Undiagnosed Diabetes in Children

Parents and caregivers should recognise these indicators:

- Unexplained weight loss
- Excessive thirst and hunger despite high blood glucose ("cellular starvation" -- cells cannot access glucose in the bloodstream)
- Increased urination (the body attempts to dilute blood glucose through increased fluid intake and excretion)
- In severe cases: nausea, vomiting, weakness, abdominal pain, loss of appetite, lethargy
- Metabolic acidosis produces "Kussmaul breathing" (נשימת קוסמאול) -- compensatory deep, rapid hyperventilation
- Extreme cases may cause cerebral oedema and death

## Diagnostic Values

| Test | Normal | Prediabetes | Diabetes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fasting glucose | 70-100 mg/dL | 100-126 mg/dL | Above 126 mg/dL |
| Random glucose | Up to 200 mg/dL | -- | Above 200 mg/dL |
| A1C (haemoglobin) | 4-6% | 6-7% | Above 7% |

## Hypoglycemia (היפוגליקמיה) -- Low Blood Sugar

### Signs and Symptoms

**Stage 1 -- Compensatory Response (adrenergic):**
The body releases adrenaline to attempt to raise blood sugar:
- Pale appearance (חיוורון)
- Sweating (הזעה)
- Trembling (רעד)
- Rapid heartbeat (דפיקות לב מהירות)
- Tremors
- Anxiety and restlessness

**Stage 2 -- Neurological Effects (neuroglycopenic):**
Glucose deprivation to the brain causes:
- Confusion (טשטוש)
- General weakness (חולשה)
- Cognitive impairment (פגיעה קוגניטיבית)
- Headaches (כאבי ראש)
- Decreased consciousness (ירידה בהכרה)
- Seizures (פרכוסים)
- Potentially death if untreated

**United Hatzalah states**: "The main symptom indicating low blood sugar levels is decreased consciousness" -- medical professionals should check blood glucose in any patient with altered mental status.

### Causes of Hypoglycemia

- Excessive insulin dosing
- Malabsorption disorders, vomiting, diarrhoea
- Prolonged fasting
- Intense physical activity
- Medication interactions
- Pancreatic tumours (rare)

## First Aid Treatment Protocol

### Conscious Patient -- Can Swallow

1. **Administer simple/fast-acting sugars** to raise blood glucose quickly:
   - Glucose gel syringe (גלוקוג'ל) -- 15 grams glucose (preferred)
   - A teaspoon of honey (דבש)
   - A cup of juice (מיץ)
   - A cup of milk (חלב)
   - Sugar cubes or glucose tablets

2. **Wait and reassess** -- monitor for improvement

3. **If improvement occurs**, follow with **complex carbohydrates** to sustain glucose levels:
   - A slice of bread with cheese
   - A pastry or cracker
   - Any carbohydrate-containing food

4. **Avoid chocolate** as the initial sugar source -- it is less effective due to fat and protein content that slows absorption

### Insulin Pump Users

**Gently disconnect the insulin pump** without causing damage to the attached equipment (usually on the abdomen or thigh). This prevents further insulin delivery which would worsen the hypoglycemia.

### Severe Hypoglycemia -- Decreased or Lost Consciousness

1. **Do NOT give food or drink by mouth** -- choking risk
2. **Call emergency services**: United Hatzalah at **1221** or MDA at **101**
3. **If no improvement with glucose gel** or the patient cannot take anything orally, an **ALS (Advanced Life Support) team** is needed to provide **intravenous glucose**
4. **Recovery position** -- place the child on their side

## Glucagon Emergency Kit (היפו-קיט / Hypo-Kit)

### Description

Available for high-risk diabetic children in educational settings. The kit contains a syringe with injection liquid and a glucagon ampoule. Glucagon is a synthetic hormone that mimics natural glucagon, triggering glucose release from liver glycogen stores.

### Dosing for Children

| Age / Weight | Dose |
|---|---|
| **Under 6 years / under 25 kg** | Half the ampoule -- **0.5 mL** |
| **Over 6 years / over 25 kg** | Full ampoule -- **1.0 mL** |

### Onset and Duration

- Effect begins approximately **10-15 minutes** after injection
- Effect lasts approximately **30 minutes**
- Use this window to provide complex carbohydrates once the child can swallow

### Scope of Practice Limitation

**United Hatzalah explicitly states**: "We do not have authority to administer or inject glucagon" within their organisation's scope of practice. Administration is limited to **trained and authorised caregivers** (parents, school nurses, designated staff trained by the child's medical team).

## Hyperglycemia (היפרגליקמיה) -- High Blood Sugar

### Common Causes in Children

- Corticosteroid use
- Fever / infection
- Malfunctioning insulin pump or disconnected infusion set
- Spoiled or heat-damaged insulin (relevant in Israeli climate)
- Psychological stress (releases cortisol and adrenaline)

### First Aid

United Hatzalah guidance states that hyperglycemia treatment is typically pharmaceutical and non-emergent. Patients should be **evaluated at a medical facility** rather than treated in prehospital settings.

**Do NOT administer insulin** -- this is beyond first aid scope.

### Signs of Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) -- Call Emergency Services

- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain
- Kussmaul breathing (deep, rapid breathing)
- Fruity/acetone breath odour
- Lethargy progressing to unconsciousness

## Israeli Context

- Approximately 8-9% of Israelis have diabetes; 20% remain undiagnosed
- United Hatzalah volunteers carry **glucometers** in their standard medic bags, enabling rapid blood glucose measurement on scene
- Each volunteer's medic bag also includes IV equipment for ALS-level responders to administer glucose intravenously
- Emergency number for United Hatzalah: **1221**
- Emergency number for MDA: **101**

## Insulin Management Context (for Awareness)

Understanding insulin types helps first aiders recognise the context:

- **Basal insulin** (e.g., Lantus / לנטוס) -- 24-hour coverage, continuous glucose regulation
- **Bolus insulin** (e.g., Novorapid / נובורפיד) -- rapid-acting, given before meals; begins working within 10 minutes, peaks at 1 hour, clears in 2-3 hours
- **Insulin pumps** -- small devices delivering continuous subcutaneous insulin through thin tubing; tubing/needle replaced every 3 days

First aiders do NOT manage insulin. This information is for context only.

## When to Call Emergency Services

Call United Hatzalah at **1221** or MDA at **101** if:

- The child has **decreased or lost consciousness**
- The child is **having seizures**
- The child **cannot swallow** safely
- There is **no improvement** after administering oral glucose/sugar
- Signs of **diabetic ketoacidosis** are present
- There is **any uncertainty** about the child's condition
- Glucagon has been administered (follow-up medical care required)

## Key Differences from MDA Guidance

United Hatzalah's content is broadly consistent with MDA's guidance. Notable differences:

1. **Scope of practice**: United Hatzalah explicitly states their volunteers cannot administer glucagon. MDA's guidance mentions glucagon administration by trained caregivers but does not restrict it to specific roles.
2. **Emergency number**: United Hatzalah uses **1221**; MDA uses **101**. Both are valid emergency numbers in Israel.
3. **Detail level**: United Hatzalah provides more detailed educational background on diabetes types and insulin management. MDA's public-facing guidance is more focused on immediate first aid steps.
4. **Glucometer**: United Hatzalah specifically mentions that their volunteers carry glucometers as standard equipment.

## Gaps and Limitations

- United Hatzalah's website does not publish a single consolidated "diabetic emergency protocol" document. The information above is compiled from three separate pages on their site (diabetes in children, hypoglycemia in the general population, and general diabetes overview).
- No child-specific dosing for oral glucose (10g vs 15g based on age) was found on the United Hatzalah pages. The child-specific glucagon dosing (0.5 mL vs 1.0 mL) was referenced in search results but sourced from Israeli hospital guidance rather than directly from United Hatzalah's published text.
- United Hatzalah does not appear to publish separate adult vs. child protocols for diabetic emergencies on their public website.
- No information was found on newer glucagon delivery systems (nasal glucagon / Baqsimi, auto-injector pens) on the United Hatzalah site.
- The publication dates of the United Hatzalah pages are unknown; recency cannot be verified.
