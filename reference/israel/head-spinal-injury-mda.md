## Overview

Head injuries (Hebrew: chavlat rosh / pgi'at rosh) and spinal injuries (Hebrew: pgi'at amud hashidra) are among the most serious trauma presentations encountered in civilian first aid in Israel. Common causes include road traffic accidents (te'unot drakhim), falls from height during hiking (especially in Negev desert wadis and Golan trails), security incidents involving blast or shrapnel, and workplace accidents.

**Critical principle:** Any head injury should be treated with concurrent suspicion of spinal injury until proven otherwise. Do NOT move the patient unless there is immediate danger to life (fire, active threat, structural collapse).

## When to Call 101 (MDA)

**Call 101 immediately -- always -- for any suspected head or spinal injury.**

Specific triggers:
- Any loss of consciousness, even momentary
- Confusion or altered mental state following a blow to the head
- Neck or back pain after trauma
- Numbness, tingling, or weakness in any limb
- Inability to move arms or legs
- Visible deformity of the skull or spine
- Blood or clear fluid draining from the nose or ears
- Unequal pupil size
- Seizures after head trauma
- Mechanism of injury suggests significant force (road accident, fall from height greater than standing, blast injury, direct blow to head/neck)

When calling 101, report:
- Location (as precise as possible)
- Mechanism of injury (how it happened)
- Whether the patient is conscious and breathing
- Whether there is visible bleeding
- Whether the patient can move their limbs

## Mechanism of Injury Assessment

### Israeli Context -- Common Scenarios

**Road traffic accidents (te'unot drakhim):**
- Israel's roads present significant trauma risk; always suspect spinal injury in any vehicle collision, motorcycle accident, or pedestrian strike
- Ejection from a vehicle or rollover is a high-risk mechanism

**Security incidents:**
- Blast injuries from explosions can cause primary (pressure wave), secondary (shrapnel), tertiary (body thrown), and quaternary (burns, crush) injuries
- Any person caught in a blast radius should be assessed for head and spinal injury regardless of visible wounds
- Stab wounds or gunshot wounds: spinal immobilisation is NOT required for isolated penetrating trauma unless neurological deficits are present or additional blunt trauma occurred (per Israeli EMS protocols)

**Hiking and outdoor falls:**
- Falls in wadis (nahal), rocky terrain, and cliffs -- common in Negev, Judean Desert, Golan Heights
- Dehydration and heat exhaustion in desert conditions may contribute to falls
- Any fall from a height greater than the patient's standing height warrants spinal precautions

**Other mechanisms requiring spinal precautions:**
- Diving into shallow water
- Sports injuries (especially contact sports, cycling, horse riding)
- Falls from ladders or rooftops
- Heavy object falling onto head or back
- Any mechanism involving axial loading (force along the spine)

## Signs and Symptoms

### Signs of Head Injury (Chavlat Rosh)

**Immediate signs:**
- Loss of consciousness (even brief)
- Confusion or disorientation
- Amnesia (cannot recall the event or events before/after)
- Headache, especially worsening over time
- Nausea and vomiting (repeated vomiting is a red flag)
- Dizziness or balance problems
- Visual disturbances (blurred or double vision)
- Sensitivity to light and noise

**Serious warning signs (require urgent evacuation):**
- Unequal pupil size (anisocoria) -- suggests intracranial pressure
- Clear fluid (CSF) or blood draining from ears or nose -- suggests skull base fracture
- Bruising behind the ears (Battle's sign) or around the eyes (raccoon eyes) -- suggests skull base fracture
- Progressive deterioration of consciousness
- Seizures
- Projectile vomiting
- Weakness on one side of the body
- Slurred speech
- Abnormal posturing (flexion or extension)

### Signs of Concussion (Zizua Moach)

Concussion is a mild traumatic brain injury. Signs include:
- Headache (most common symptom)
- Feeling "foggy" or confused
- Difficulty concentrating
- Dizziness
- Nausea
- Sensitivity to light and noise
- Fatigue and drowsiness
- Emotional changes (irritability, sadness)
- Sleep disturbances
- Balance problems

**Important:** A person does NOT need to lose consciousness to have a concussion. Concussion symptoms may be delayed and appear hours after the injury.

### Signs of Spinal Injury (Pgi'at Amud HaShidra)

- Pain in the neck (tzavar) or back (gav) -- especially along the midline
- Tenderness when the spine is palpated
- Numbness or tingling (nimul) in the arms, hands, legs, or feet
- Weakness or inability to move limbs (paralysis)
- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Difficulty breathing (if cervical spine injury affects the diaphragm)
- Deformity or step-off felt along the spine
- Muscle spasm along the spine

**Critical warning:** A patient who is standing or walking does NOT necessarily have an intact spine. Unstable spinal fractures can exist without initial neurological deficit. Always assess based on mechanism of injury and symptoms.

## First Aid Protocol

### Step 1: Ensure Scene Safety

- Assess for ongoing danger: traffic, fire, structural instability, active security threat
- Do NOT approach if the scene is unsafe -- call 101 and wait for professional responders
- If there is immediate life-threatening danger to the patient (fire, active shooting, imminent structural collapse), move the patient only as a last resort using a log-roll technique keeping the spine aligned

### Step 2: Call 101 (MDA)

- Call Magen David Adom at 101 immediately for any suspected head or spinal injury
- Do not wait to see if symptoms develop
- Provide clear information about the mechanism of injury

### Step 3: Manual In-Line Stabilisation (Yitzuv Yadani)

This is the single most important first aid intervention for suspected spinal injury:

1. Kneel or lie behind the patient's head
2. Place your hands on both sides of the patient's head, cupping the ears gently
3. Hold the head in a neutral position aligned with the body -- do NOT twist, flex, or extend the neck
4. Maintain this stabilisation continuously until professional help arrives
5. Do NOT attempt to straighten the head if it is found in a rotated position and the patient resists or reports pain -- stabilise in the position found
6. Talk to the patient to keep them calm and still

**If a cervical collar is available:**
- Apply it while a second person maintains manual stabilisation
- A collar alone is NOT sufficient -- continue to support the head even with a collar applied
- Sizing must be correct; an improperly sized collar can cause harm

### Step 4: Assess Airway and Breathing

- If the patient is conscious and talking, the airway is open
- If the patient is unconscious:
  - Use the **jaw thrust manoeuvre** (mishlat leset) to open the airway -- do NOT use head-tilt chin-lift, as this moves the cervical spine
  - Jaw thrust: place your fingers behind the angles of the lower jaw on both sides and push the jaw forward without moving the neck
  - If the patient is not breathing and has no pulse, begin CPR with spinal precautions (jaw thrust for ventilations; compressions as normal)

### Step 5: Control Bleeding

- If there is bleeding from the scalp, apply gentle direct pressure with a clean dressing
- Do NOT apply pressure if you suspect a skull fracture (depressed bone, visible brain matter) -- cover loosely with a sterile dressing
- If blood or fluid is draining from ears, do NOT plug the ears -- allow drainage and cover loosely
- For associated limb bleeding, apply direct pressure or a tourniquet as indicated

### Step 6: Monitor and Reassure

- Keep the patient still and calm -- explain what you are doing
- Monitor breathing and consciousness continuously
- Note any changes in neurological status (can they move fingers/toes? are they becoming more or less alert?)
- Keep the patient warm -- cover with a blanket or clothing (hypothermia worsens trauma outcomes)
- Do NOT give food or drink
- Do NOT remove a motorcycle or bicycle helmet unless the patient cannot breathe -- helmet removal requires two trained people

### Step 7: If the Patient Must Be Moved (Life-Threatening Situation Only)

- Use a log-roll technique: minimum 3 people, one controlling the head
- The person at the head commands the roll
- Roll the patient as a single unit onto their side or onto a firm surface (door, board)
- Maintain spinal alignment throughout

## What NOT to Do

- Do NOT move the patient unless there is immediate danger to life
- Do NOT twist, flex, or extend the neck
- Do NOT attempt to sit the patient up
- Do NOT remove a helmet without proper training and a second person
- Do NOT give the patient food or drink
- Do NOT leave the patient alone
- Do NOT apply pressure to a suspected skull fracture
- Do NOT plug the ears or nose if fluid is draining
- Do NOT pull the patient by the arms or legs

## Special Considerations -- Israeli Context

### Desert and Outdoor Settings
- In the Negev and Judean Desert, shade the patient from direct sun while maintaining immobilisation
- Dehydration exacerbates shock -- but do NOT give oral fluids to a patient with suspected head/spinal injury (risk of vomiting and aspiration)
- Evacuation in remote areas may take longer -- maintain stabilisation patiently and monitor for deterioration
- Note the GPS coordinates of your location when calling 101

### Security Incidents
- In a mass casualty event (pigua), triage may require prioritisation -- a patient with suspected spinal injury who is breathing and has a pulse is typically categorised as "urgent" (yellow) rather than "immediate" (red) unless airway is compromised
- Tourniquet use for associated extremity haemorrhage follows standard Israeli civilian training (CAT tourniquet application)
- In an active threat situation, the priority is to move casualties to cover; spinal precautions may be secondary to immediate life-saving extraction

### Road Traffic Accidents
- Do NOT attempt to extract a patient from a vehicle unless the vehicle is on fire or there is another immediate life threat
- Support the patient's head in position and wait for MDA and fire services
- If multiple casualties, call 101 and report the number of patients

## Recovery Position with Spinal Precautions

If the patient is unconscious but breathing and must be placed on their side (e.g., vomiting):
- Use the log-roll technique with one person maintaining head/neck alignment
- Roll the patient onto their side as a unit
- Continue to support the head in alignment with the body
