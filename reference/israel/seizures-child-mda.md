## What Are Febrile Seizures?

Febrile seizures (פרכוסי חום) are caused by abnormal electrical activity in the brain triggered by a rapid rise in body temperature. They are the most common type of seizure in children.

- **Age group affected**: Children aged 6 months to 5-6 years
- **Peak incidence**: 18-22 months
- **Prevalence**: Approximately 3-4% of children (some sources cite up to 10%)
- **Prognosis**: Febrile seizures do not cause brain damage, do not lead to learning disabilities, and do not increase epilepsy risk (unless other risk factors are present, such as family history of epilepsy). There are no recorded deaths from simple febrile seizures.

## Types of Febrile Seizures

### Simple Febrile Seizures
- Involve the entire body (generalised tonic-clonic)
- Last up to 15 minutes
- Do not recur more than once within 24 hours
- Appear at the onset of fever, usually on the first day of illness

### Complex Febrile Seizures
- May involve only one side of the body
- Last more than 15 minutes
- May recur more than once within 24 hours

## What to Do During a Seizure

1. **Lay the child on their side** with head supported/tilted to the side or downward -- this keeps the airway clear and prevents choking if they vomit
2. **Loosen tight clothing**, especially around the neck
3. **Remove dangerous objects** from the child's vicinity to prevent injury
4. **Time the seizure** -- note when it starts and how long it lasts
5. **Wrap the child in a damp (lukewarm) towel** to help reduce fever (MDA guidance)
6. **If in a vehicle**, keep the child secured in their safety seat
7. **Administer prescribed seizure-stopping medication** if the child has one (e.g., rectal diazepam prescribed by their doctor)
8. **Stay with the child** and observe

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT force the child's mouth open** -- the child will not swallow their tongue
- **Do NOT insert anything into the mouth** -- this can injure teeth or jaw
- **Do NOT attempt resuscitation movements** during the seizure -- this can only cause harm (MDA explicit warning)
- **Do NOT put the child in a bath** or shower during the seizure
- **Do NOT splash water on the child** during the seizure
- **Do NOT try to wake the child** or stop the seizure by restraining them
- **Do NOT give medications by mouth** during the seizure
- **Do NOT attempt to cool the child rapidly** (no cold water, no ice)

## When to Call MDA (101)

Call Magen David Adom at **101** immediately if:

- **This is the child's first seizure** -- always seek immediate medical evaluation
- **The seizure lasts more than 5 minutes**
- **Severe symptoms present**: difficulty breathing, choking, skin discolouration (blueness/pallor)
- **The child has received anti-seizure medication but the seizure persists**
- **The child does not regain consciousness** after the seizure ends
- **Multiple seizures occur** in a short period

## Managing Fever to Reduce Recurrence Risk

When a child's temperature rises to 37.8 C (100 F):

1. Administer fever-reducing medication (paracetamol/acetaminophen or ibuprofen) at a dose appropriate for the child's weight (dose should be determined by the family doctor)
2. You may cool the child's body with a lukewarm bath for about 20 minutes (only when NOT actively seizing)
3. You may wrap the body with damp towels
4. Dress the child in light clothing
5. Ventilate the room
6. Give the child plenty of fluids

**Important note**: Giving antipyretics during fever may improve comfort but has NOT been proven to prevent febrile seizures (per Israeli Ministry of Health and current medical evidence).

## After the Seizure

- The child may be drowsy, confused, or sleepy -- this is normal (post-ictal state)
- Place in the recovery position (on their side)
- Monitor breathing
- After a febrile seizure, the child should be examined by a paediatrician
- For a first seizure, MDA will transport the child to hospital for evaluation
