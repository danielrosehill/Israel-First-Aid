## United Hatzalah Spinal Injury Assessment Protocol

### Modern Approach: Selective Immobilisation

United Hatzalah's protocol reflects a paradigm shift from historical practice. The key principle is:

> **"We can exclude spinal cord injury through examination"** -- rather than automatically immobilising all trauma patients.

This follows updated international PHTLS protocols.

### When Immobilisation Can Be OMITTED

Full spinal immobilisation may be omitted **only** if ALL of the following conditions are met:
- Patient is fully conscious and cooperative
- No substance or alcohol impairment
- No language barriers preventing reliable assessment
- Physical examination reveals no spinal tenderness, sensitivity, or deformity
- No neurological deficits (normal sensation and limb movement in all extremities)
- No distracting injuries causing severe pain elsewhere
- Penetrating-only mechanism (knife/gunshot without blunt trauma signs)

**Note for children**: Many of these exclusion criteria are difficult or impossible to apply to young children (communication barriers, inability to cooperate, distracting pain from crying). **In practice, children with significant trauma mechanisms should generally be immobilised.**

### When Full Immobilisation IS Required

Cervical collar + spinal board + head blocks are mandatory when:
- Patient reports neck or back pain
- Physical examination reveals tenderness along the spine
- Neurological deficits are present (sensation loss, movement impairment)
- Mechanism suggests blunt trauma (motor vehicle accidents, falls from height)
- Patient has altered consciousness, is intoxicated, or has communication barriers
- Distracting injuries are present (pain elsewhere may mask spinal symptoms)
- **Any uncertainty remains after assessment**

### Critical Warning

> **Standing or walking ability does NOT exclude spinal cord injury.**

A patient who is already immobilised must not be removed from immobilisation -- this decision is outside first responder / paramedic authority.

## United Hatzalah Trauma Assessment: ABCDE Approach

### Step S -- Scene Safety
- Assess environmental hazards before approaching
- Wear high-visibility vest and gloves
- Move patients from dangerous areas with minimal movement if spinal injury is suspected

### Step A -- Airway with Cervical Spine Protection
- **Use jaw thrust manoeuvre (NOT head-tilt chin-lift)** to open airways while protecting the cervical spine
- Position yourself near the patient's face for communication
- Maintain eye contact and conversation to keep the patient alert
- Two providers are ideal for jaw thrust technique
- **Never remove motorcycle/bicycle helmets alone** -- two rescuers are mandatory

#### Cervical Spine Stabilisation
- Treat ALL trauma victims with severe impact mechanisms as suspected spinal injuries until cleared by hospital evaluation
- Begin **manual in-line stabilisation** immediately (hands on either side of the head)
- Progress to cervical collar, spinal board, and head blocks as equipment becomes available
- **"Any unnecessary movement may cause neurological damage or worsen existing injury"**

### Step B -- Breathing
- Assess breathing rate, depth, and symmetry
- Check for chest injuries

### Step C -- Circulation
- Control external haemorrhage with direct pressure
- **Scalp bleeding can cause shock, especially in children and infants** (due to smaller blood volume)
- Assess pulse, skin colour, and capillary refill

### Step D -- Disability (Neurological Assessment)
- **AVPU scale** (BLS level): Alert, Verbal stimuli, Painful stimuli, Unresponsive
- **Glasgow Coma Scale** (ALS level): for more detailed evaluation
- **Pupil examination**: check diameter (narrow/wide/normal), equality between eyes, and light response
  - **Unequal pupils suggest potential brain injury**
- **Spinal cord injury assessment**: evaluate limb sensation and motor function in all extremities
- Check for pain, numbness, weakness, or sensation loss

#### Key Principle for Head Injury
> **"Any trauma patient exhibiting hostile, combative, or uncooperative behaviour should be suspected of suffering from hypoxia or head injury until proven otherwise, especially if no obvious threat is apparent."**

### Step E -- Exposure / Environment
- Rapidly expose and inspect the body for additional injuries
- Examine each area separately, then cover
- **Trauma patients face elevated hypothermia risk** -- maintain warmth with blankets
- Hidden injuries may have small external signs or be concealed by blood-soaked clothing

## Blast / Missile Attack Trauma (Israel-Specific)

United Hatzalah's guidance for missile/rocket events is relevant to paediatric head/spinal injury:

### Blast Injury Categories
1. **Primary**: Pressure wave affects air-filled cavities -- lungs, GI tract, ears, skull. Can cause brain concussion, pneumothorax, organ rupture.
2. **Secondary**: Shrapnel and debris propelled by explosion -- penetrating wounds.
3. **Tertiary**: Victim thrown against objects or falls -- blunt trauma including head and spinal injuries.
4. **Quaternary**: Burns, smoke inhalation, toxic gases, crush injuries.

### Treatment Protocol
- Follow strict PHTLS systematic assessment: Safety, then Haemorrhage Control, then ABCDE
- Multiple casualties may require rapid triage
- Enclosed spaces amplify blast effects
- Maintain detailed scene information for hospital handoff

**Note**: No paediatric-specific modifications are published in United Hatzalah's blast injury article, but children are particularly vulnerable to all blast injury categories due to smaller body mass.

## Paediatric Considerations (Synthesised from United Hatzalah Training Principles)

While United Hatzalah does not publish a dedicated paediatric head/spinal injury guide, the following child-specific considerations are consistent with their PHTLS-based training:

### Assessment Challenges in Children
- Young children cannot reliably communicate symptoms (pain, numbness, weakness)
- Crying, fear, and non-cooperation may mask neurological signs
- Children who are hostile or combative after trauma should be assumed to have head injury
- The AVPU scale must be interpreted with age-appropriate expectations (an infant's "alert" behaviour differs from an adult's)

### Anatomical Differences
- Proportionally larger head increases risk of head-first impacts in falls
- More flexible paediatric spine with ligamentous laxity
- Scalp bleeding can cause haemodynamic compromise (shock) more rapidly than in adults
- Upper cervical spine (C1-C3) is more commonly injured in young children

### Practical Points
- **Do not move the child** unless in immediate life-threatening danger
- Manual cervical spine stabilisation should be initiated even before equipment arrives
- Cervical collars must be appropriately sized for children -- an adult collar on a child can worsen the injury
- Helmet removal requires two trained rescuers and should generally be left for arriving EMS
- Keep the child warm -- hypothermia risk is higher in children

|--------|----------------|-----|
| Assessment framework | PHTLS ABCDE | PHTLS ABCDE (same) |
| Spinal immobilisation | Selective (modern approach -- can exclude by exam) | Same approach in ALS protocols |
