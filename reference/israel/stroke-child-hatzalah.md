## FAST Recognition Protocol (United Hatzalah)

United Hatzalah teaches the FAST mnemonic for stroke recognition:

| Letter | Assessment | Description |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **F** - Face | Facial asymmetry | Check for drooping on one side of the face |
| **A** - Arm | Arm weakness | Check for weakness or inability to raise one arm; unequal strength between sides |
| **S** - Speech | Speech difficulty | Check for slurred speech, difficulty forming words, confusion in language |
| **T** - Time | Time awareness | If any of the above are present, call for emergency evacuation immediately |

### Anatomical Explanation (from United Hatzalah article)

United Hatzalah's educational material explains why FAST focuses on face, arm, and speech:
- The left hemisphere of the brain controls motor function on the right side of the body and contains the speech centres (in 87-90% of right-handed people)
- Blood clots from the heart preferentially enter the left carotid artery due to anatomy
- Therefore, left-hemisphere strokes are statistically more common, producing the classic FAST deficits
- Larger lesions may produce additional symptoms beyond FAST, but these three signs are the most reliable for field-level screening

## Types of Stroke (United Hatzalah Educational Material)

### Ischemic (Occlusive) Stroke
- Accounts for approximately 85% of all strokes
- Caused by an artery in the brain becoming blocked, cutting off oxygen-rich blood supply
- Blood clots from the heart can travel through the carotid arteries to the brain

### Hemorrhagic (Bleeding) Stroke
- A blood vessel in the brain ruptures, causing bleeding into brain tissue
- Creates pressure from accumulated blood, damaging surrounding tissue
- Accounts for approximately 13% of stroke events
- Subtypes: epidural, subdural, subarachnoid, intracerebral haemorrhage
- Signs include: sudden severe headache, altered consciousness, neck stiffness, nausea/vomiting, seizures, photophobia

### Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)
- Symptoms typically resolve within 1-2 hours (up to 24 hours)
- Approximately 40% of TIA patients experience a full stroke within three months
- Should be treated as a medical emergency

## Additional Warning Signs Beyond FAST (United Hatzalah)

- Sudden change in consciousness
- First seizure in an adult (noted by United Hatzalah; in children, new-onset seizures are also a warning sign)
- Sudden vision loss
- Sudden memory loss
- Acute behavioural changes

---

## Emergency Response (United Hatzalah Material)

United Hatzalah's material for EMS responders includes:
- Immediate vital signs monitoring (pulse, oxygen saturation, blood pressure, glucose)
- **Glucose testing is critical** - hypoglycaemia symptoms can closely resemble stroke
- Oxygen administration for respiratory distress or low oxygen levels
- Rapid hospital transport to designated stroke centres with neurosurgical capabilities
- **Therapeutic window**: approximately 6 hours from symptom onset
- Field providers **cannot differentiate stroke types** and should not attempt to do so

## Pediatric Considerations

**United Hatzalah does not publish pediatric-specific stroke guidance.** The following notes contextualise their general guidance for children:

- The FAST protocol applies to children but may miss atypical presentations
- Children are more likely than adults to present with seizures at stroke onset
- Sudden severe headache with vomiting and sleepiness is a particularly important warning sign in children
- In children, stroke may be mistaken for other conditions (migraine, epilepsy, behavioural changes)
- Very young children may present with subtle signs (irritability, feeding difficulty, lethargy)
- United Hatzalah's note that "glucose testing is critical" is relevant for children as well, since hypoglycaemia can mimic stroke

## First Aid Steps (Derived from United Hatzalah General Guidance)

1. **Recognise FAST signs** - Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty
2. **Call for emergency help immediately** - 101 (MDA) or 1221 (United Hatzalah)
3. **Note the time of symptom onset** - critical for treatment decisions
4. **Do not give food or drink** - risk of aspiration if swallowing is impaired
5. **Do not give medications** - especially not aspirin (dangerous if haemorrhagic stroke)
6. **Keep the person calm and still**
7. **Monitor vital signs** if trained and equipped to do so
8. **Be prepared to perform CPR** if the person becomes unresponsive and stops breathing

## Comparison with MDA

United Hatzalah's stroke guidance is **fully consistent with MDA's FAST-based approach**. Both organisations:
- Use the FAST mnemonic
- Emphasise immediate emergency activation (101/1221)
- Stress the importance of noting symptom onset time
- Recommend against giving food, drink, or medications
- Emphasise rapid transport to a stroke-capable hospital

There are **no meaningful differences** between the two organisations' published guidance on stroke recognition and first aid.
