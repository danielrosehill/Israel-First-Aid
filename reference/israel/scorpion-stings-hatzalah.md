## Dangerous Scorpion Species in Israel

United Hatzalah identifies two primary dangerous species, both containing neurotoxic venom:

| Species | Common Name | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| *Leiurus quinquestriatus* | Yellow scorpion (Deathstalker) | Most hazardous; nearly ubiquitous across Israel |
| (Thick-tailed black scorpion) | Black scorpion | Present throughout Israel; venom potency rivals yellow species |

Additional species mentioned:
- **Southern killer scorpion**: Found in North Africa and southern Sinai
- **Two-clawed scorpion**: Central-southern areas; painful but less dangerous
- **Hebrew black scorpion**: Least dangerous of harmful species

**Total**: Approximately 21 scorpion species in Israel, of which five are dangerous to humans.

**Important myth correction**: The common belief that black scorpions are not venomous is FALSE.

## First Aid Protocol -- What To Do

### BLS Level (Basic Life Support -- First Responder)

1. **Maintain ABC** (Airway, Breathing, Circulation) support
2. **Calm the victim** -- anxiety and agitation increase heart rate and accelerate venom absorption
3. **Immobilise the affected limb** -- keep still to slow venom spread
4. **Apply cold compress** to the sting area:
   - Use ice water in a bag or cold compress
   - Apply for **10 minutes with breaks**
   - **Avoid direct ice contact on skin** (wrap in cloth)
   - Purpose: reduces pain and slows venom distribution
5. **Provide oxygen** if respiratory distress is present
6. **Rapid evacuation to hospital** -- mandatory for all scorpion stings
7. **Document** patient details and treatment provided

### ALS Level (Advanced Life Support -- Paramedic)

1. Continue ABC support
2. Administer medications for seizures if they occur (benzodiazepines)
3. Consider atropine for excessive parasympathetic stimulation (bradycardia, excessive secretions)
4. Provide pain management when available
5. Cardiac monitoring

## Symptoms

### Local signs
- Immediate and intense pain at the sting site
- Mild swelling and redness

**Note**: Local symptom severity does NOT indicate systemic danger level.

### Systemic signs (severe cases)

**Neurological**: Restlessness, altered consciousness, seizures, numbness at sting site, ineffective breathing, elevated muscle tone, temperature dysregulation (hypothermia or hyperthermia)

**Cardiovascular**: Heart failure potentially worsening to pulmonary edema, cardiogenic shock with severe hypotension, arrhythmias from excessive dopamine and adrenaline release

**Autonomic (sympathetic activation)**: Tachycardia, tachypnea, hypertension, sweating, urinary retention

**Autonomic (parasympathetic activation)**: Bradycardia, diarrhea (may produce opposite effects to sympathetic)

### High-risk groups
- Children under 8 years old
- Elderly patients
- Those with underlying medical conditions

## What NOT To Do

United Hatzalah does not provide an explicit "do not" list for scorpion stings on these pages, but the following prohibitions are implicit from their guidance and consistent with Israeli first aid standards:

| Prohibited Action | Reason |
|-------------------|--------|
| Do NOT apply a tourniquet | Traps venom locally, causes tissue damage |
| Do NOT attempt to suck out venom | Ineffective and harmful |
| Do NOT cut the sting area | Increases infection risk |

---

## Hospital Monitoring

United Hatzalah emphasises that **urgent hospital transfer is mandatory** for all scorpion stings. Reasons include:

- Delayed reactions are possible with some scorpion species
- Arrhythmias may develop requiring cardiac monitoring and supportive care
- Swelling and pain may require hospital-level pain management
- In rare cases, scorpion antivenom may be administered

## Differences from MDA Protocol

| Topic | United Hatzalah | MDA / Ministry of Health |
|-------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| Cold compress | Recommends applying cold compress for 10 min with breaks | Conflicting: MDA snippets suggest cooling; Ministry of Health says do NOT cool |
| BLS/ALS distinction | Explicitly distinguishes BLS and ALS treatment levels | Published guidance is aimed at lay public |
| Oxygen | Mentions oxygen administration at BLS level | Not mentioned in public-facing guidance |
| Core agreement | Both agree on: calm victim, immobilise limb, call emergency services, urgent hospital transport |

**Note**: Where MDA and United Hatzalah disagree, MDA is the authoritative default per project conventions. The cold compress guidance is the primary area of conflict. See the MDA reference file for the synthesised recommendation.

## Israel-Specific Context

- **Peak season**: Spring through autumn; highest density in desert regions (Negev, Arava, Judean Desert)
- **Climate**: Extreme desert heat drives scorpions to seek shelter in shoes, clothing, tents, and buildings
- **Emergency numbers**: MDA = **101**, United Hatzalah = **1221**, Police = **100**, Fire = **102**
- **Israel Poison Information Center**: **04-7771900** (Rambam Health Care Campus, Haifa; 24/7 hotline)
