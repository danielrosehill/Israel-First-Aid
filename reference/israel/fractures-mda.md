## Identifying a Fracture

### Signs and Symptoms

- Severe pain that worsens with any attempt to move the limb
- Swelling and bruising: the area becomes red, bluish and swells quickly
- Deformity: the limb appears crooked, shortened, or at an unnatural angle
- Limitation in movement: the injured person is unable to move the injured limb
- Possible crepitus (grinding sensation) at the injury site
- Change in the shape of the bone visible through the skin

### Open vs Closed Fractures

- **Closed fracture (shever sagur):** The bone is broken but the skin remains intact. This is the more common presentation.
- **Open fracture (shever patu'ach):** The broken bone pierces through the skin, creating a wound. This is a medical emergency with high risk of infection, bleeding, and vascular damage.

## First Aid Protocol -- Closed Fractures

### Core Principle

**"Minimum movement, maximum stability"** (MDA guidance).

### Step-by-Step

1. **Call MDA at 101**
   - Keep the call on speaker and provide your location
   - You can receive phone guidance from MDA on how to manage the fracture at the scene

2. **Do NOT move the limb unnecessarily**
   - Allow the injured person to sit or lie in the most comfortable position
   - Do NOT attempt to straighten the limb, pull it, or return a bone to its place -- such actions may tear arteries and nerves

3. **Immobilise the injured area**

   **For arm/upper limb injuries:**
   - Support the injured arm with the person's healthy hand
   - Use an improvised sling (shirt, scarf, triangular bandage) and attach it to the chest
   - MDA ambulances carry triangular cloth bandages for this purpose
   - Place the apex of the triangle at the elbow, pass one side under the affected area and one above, and tie at the neck to create a sling that reduces movement
   - Even if someone simply holds their own hand in place, this is effective -- the key principle is minimising movement

   **For leg/lower limb injuries:**
   - Ask the injured person not to move the leg
   - Place pillows or rolled blankets on both sides of the leg to prevent involuntary movement
   - For thigh fractures and below, use a triangular cloth above the injury site and tie firmly to prevent movement, securing the knot on the uninjured leg

4. **Apply cold therapy**
   - Apply ice wrapped in a cloth (never directly on skin) to reduce swelling and pain
   - Apply for 15-minute intervals
   - Begin cooling as soon as possible after the injury

5. **Remove restrictive items**
   - Remove rings, bracelets, watches, and jewellery immediately from the affected limb
   - This prevents tourniquet effects as swelling occurs

6. **Monitor the patient**
   - Watch for signs of shock (pale skin, rapid breathing, weak pulse)
   - Keep the patient warm and comfortable until MDA arrives

## First Aid Protocol -- Open Fractures

An open fracture is a medical emergency. Call 101 immediately.

1. **Control bleeding**
   - Apply direct pressure to stop bleeding
   - Use a tourniquet if bleeding is severe and cannot be controlled with direct pressure

2. **Cover the wound**
   - Cover the exposed bone and wound with a sterile dressing or clean cloth
   - Do NOT push the bone back in -- leave it as found

3. **Immobilise the limb**
   - Follow the same immobilisation principles as for closed fractures
   - Support the limb in the position found; do not attempt to realign

4. **Monitor for shock**
   - Open fractures carry high risk of blood loss and shock
   - Keep the patient lying down, warm, and reassured

## First Aid Protocol -- Sprains (Neka)

A sprain occurs when ligaments connecting bones at a joint are stretched or torn, typically at the ankle, wrist, or knee.

### Signs and Symptoms

- Pain at the joint, especially with movement
- Swelling around the joint
- Bruising (may appear within hours)
- Limited ability to move the joint
- Possible "popping" sensation at the time of injury

### RICE Protocol

MDA training aligns with the internationally recognised RICE method:

1. **Rest (menucha)**
   - Stop all activity involving the injured joint
   - Do not bear weight on the injured limb

2. **Ice (kerach)**
   - Apply an ice pack wrapped in a cloth to the injured area
   - Apply for 15--20 minutes every 2--3 hours
   - Continue for the first 48--72 hours after injury
   - Do NOT apply ice directly to skin

3. **Compression (chevisha)**
   - Bandage the area firmly but not too tightly around, above, and below the injury
   - Use an elastic bandage if available
   - The bandage should reduce swelling without cutting off circulation
   - If fingers or toes become numb, pale, or blue, loosen the bandage

4. **Elevation (harama)**
   - Elevate the injured limb above the level of the heart
   - Use pillows or cushions to support the limb in a raised position
   - This helps reduce swelling by promoting fluid drainage

### When to Distinguish a Sprain from a Fracture

It can be difficult to tell a sprain from a fracture without imaging. Seek medical evaluation (call 101 or go to an emergency department) if:
- The person cannot bear any weight on the limb
- There is visible deformity
- Pain is severe and unrelenting
- Numbness develops below the injury
- Swelling is extreme or does not improve with RICE

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT attempt to straighten or realign a broken limb** -- this may tear arteries and nerves
- **Do NOT push a protruding bone back in** during an open fracture
- **Do NOT apply ice directly to skin** -- always wrap in cloth
- **Do NOT apply heat** to a fresh fracture or sprain (heat increases swelling)
- **Do NOT allow the patient to walk on a suspected leg fracture**
- **Do NOT remove embedded objects** from a wound associated with a fracture

---

## When to Call MDA (101)

Call 101 immediately in any of the following situations:

- Suspected fracture of any kind (all fractures warrant professional evaluation)
- Open fracture (bone visible through skin)
- Fracture involving the head, neck, spine, pelvis, or hip
- Loss of sensation or circulation below the injury (numbness, cold, pale or blue skin)
- The person shows signs of shock
- Inability to immobilise the injury adequately
- Multiple injuries or high-energy mechanism (fall from height, road traffic accident)
- Any doubt -- MDA provides phone guidance and will advise whether ambulance dispatch is needed

## Israel-Specific Context

### Climate Considerations
- In extreme desert heat (Negev, Arava, Jordan Valley), fracture patients are at elevated risk of dehydration -- offer water while waiting for MDA
- Hot surfaces (metal, asphalt, sand) can cause secondary burns if a patient falls and lies on them -- move the patient to shade if safe to do so

### Security Context
- Blast and shrapnel injuries frequently involve compound (open) fractures alongside penetrating trauma
- Israeli civilian first responder training includes tourniquet use, which is relevant to open fractures with severe bleeding
- Falls during rapid movement to shelters (mamad) are a common mechanism for fractures, particularly in elderly populations

### Equipment Context
- MDA ambulances carry triangular bandages, rigid splints, and traction devices
- MDA first aid kits (tik chovesh) include triangular cloth bandages, elastic bandages, and wooden splints for fracture immobilisation
- Cooling sprays (available at pharmacies and from United Hatzalah shop) can be used as an alternative to ice packs in the field
