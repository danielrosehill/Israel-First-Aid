## United Hatzalah Context

United Hatzalah is a volunteer-based rapid-response EMS organisation. Their average response time is under 3 minutes nationwide and 90 seconds in metropolitan areas. Volunteers carry medic bags with IV equipment, oxygen, defibrillators, and other supplies. Their training materials are used to train volunteer EMTs and first responders.

## Key Principles from United Hatzalah Sources

### Choking as Leading Cause of Paediatric Arrest

United Hatzalah training materials state clearly:

> "Most resuscitation cases in young children and infants result from choking" (רוב מקרי ההחייאה בילדים קטנים ובתינוקות נגרמות כתוצאה מחנק)

Common causes include:
- Small object aspiration
- Food-related choking
- Drowning
- Electrocution

### Age Definitions (Consistent with MDA)

## Choking Protocol (as derived from United Hatzalah sources)

United Hatzalah's publicly available materials focus primarily on CPR and the transition from choking to cardiac arrest. Their choking-specific protocol aligns with the MDA/national standard:

### Infant (Under 1 Year)
- **Back blows**: 5 strikes between shoulder blades with infant face-down on forearm, head lower than body
- **Chest thrusts**: 5 compressions with two fingers at centre of chest
- Alternate until object is expelled or infant becomes unresponsive
- **No abdominal thrusts** for infants

### Child (1-8 Years)
- **Abdominal thrusts** (Heimlich manoeuvre): inward and upward thrusts above navel, below rib cage
- Continue until object is expelled or child becomes unresponsive

### Transition to CPR (If Victim Becomes Unresponsive)

This is where the Hatzalah materials provide detailed guidance:

**Solo Responder Rule**: Perform 2 minutes of CPR (5 cycles of 30:2, approximately 200 chest compressions) BEFORE calling for help. This prioritises airway intervention for children, since choking is the most likely cause.

**Compression Techniques**:
- Infant (solo): Two-finger technique
- Child (solo): One-hand compression
- Infant (two rescuers): Thumb-encircling method
- Child (two rescuers): One-hand technique

**Compression-to-Ventilation Ratios**:
- Solo responder (any age): 30:2
- Two responders: 15:2 for children; 30:2 for adults

**Airway Positioning**:
- Infants: "Look to sky" position -- slight head tilt only. Full head extension blocks the infant airway.
- Children: Standard head-tilt chin-lift

**Ventilation**:
- Infants: Mouth-to-mouth-and-nose or pocket mask
- Children: Standard mouth-to-mouth

**Rescue Breathing (if pulse present but no breathing)**:
- Infants: 1 breath per 3 seconds
- Children: 1 breath per 4 seconds

**AED Use**:
- If paediatric pads overlap when positioned normally on a small child, apply one pad front-centre and one between the shoulder blades

## Real-World Response Examples

From United Hatzalah's published rescue accounts:

1. **Choking baby incident**: An EMT picked up a choking baby, tilted her downwards, and gave "sharp and measured back blows to dislodge fluids trapped in the airway" -- consistent with the back-blow protocol.

2. **Repeat choking incident**: A volunteer EMT saved the same baby from choking twice in one night, demonstrating that choking can recur and caregivers should remain vigilant.

---

## Differences from MDA Protocol

**There are no substantive differences.** Both organisations follow the same national/international standard (ILCOR-aligned guidelines). The protocols are identical in their core steps:
- 5 back blows / 5 chest thrusts for infants
- Abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) for children 1+
- Transition to CPR if unresponsive

United Hatzalah's publicly available training materials provide more detail on the CPR transition and compression techniques, while MDA sources provide more detail on the choking intervention itself.
