## Israeli Context

Tourniquet use is widely taught to Israeli civilians. Unlike many Western countries where tourniquet training was historically reserved for military/professional responders, Israel's security reality means that tourniquet application is a core component of civilian first aid courses. MDA trained approximately 500,000 people in 2024 across various course levels.

The Israeli-developed Emergency Bandage ("Israeli bandage") and the Combat Application Tourniquet (CAT) are standard equipment in Israeli first aid kits, including in schools, public buildings, and vehicles.

---

## When to Apply a Tourniquet

Apply a tourniquet for **life-threatening limb hemorrhage only**, specifically when:

1. **Direct pressure fails** to control bleeding
2. **Amputation** -- partial or complete
3. **Severe crushing injury** to a limb
4. **Uncontrollable hemorrhage** from a limb wound
5. **Multi-casualty situations** where you cannot maintain direct pressure on every wound simultaneously
6. **Circumstances prevent sustained direct pressure** (e.g., you need to move the casualty, treat others, or the environment is too dangerous to remain)

### When NOT to Use a Tourniquet

- Tourniquet is **NOT suitable** for junctional zones: groin, armpit, neck
- **NOT suitable** for head or abdominal wounds
- For these locations, use direct pressure, wound packing, or pressure points instead

## MDA Tourniquet Application Protocol

### Step-by-Step (CAT Tourniquet or Improvised)

1. **Expose the wound** -- cut or remove clothing to identify the bleeding source
2. **Position the tourniquet** -- place it **above the injury site** on the limb (between the wound and the heart), over a single bone if possible (upper arm or upper thigh preferred)
3. **Tighten the tourniquet** -- pull the strap tight and secure it
4. **Twist the windlass** (on CAT tourniquets) -- turn the rod until bleeding stops completely
5. **Secure the windlass** -- lock the rod in the clip/holder so it cannot unwind
6. **Verify bleeding has stopped** -- check that there is no further blood flow from the wound
7. **Document the time** -- write the time of tourniquet application on the tourniquet's white tab, on the patient's skin, or in any obvious visible location
8. **Call MDA at 101** -- always mention that a tourniquet has been applied when calling emergency services

### Critical Rules

- **Do NOT remove the tourniquet once applied** -- only medical professionals should decide whether to release or convert a tourniquet
- **If bleeding does not stop**, apply a second tourniquet above the first one
- **If a commercial tourniquet is not available**, improvise using "whatever is available (shirt, towel, etc.) and apply tightly until the bleeding stops" (MDA guidelines)
- **Document the time** in an obvious location -- this is critical for hospital teams to assess limb viability

## Improvised Tourniquets

MDA acknowledges that commercial tourniquets may not always be available. Their guidance states:

> "Place a tourniquet above the injury site using whatever is available (shirt, towel, etc.) and apply tightly until the bleeding stops."

Real-world examples from MDA cases include:
- A bystander using a belt as an improvised tourniquet to control bleeding until MDA arrived
- A United Hatzalah EMT using tzitzit (ritual fringed garment) as an improvised tourniquet during October 7

Improvised tourniquets are less reliable than commercial devices but can be effective as a last resort.

## Tourniquet in the MDA Hemorrhage Control Sequence

MDA teaches a progressive approach to hemorrhage control:

1. **Direct pressure** -- first attempt for all external bleeding
2. **Israeli bandage (Emergency Bandage)** -- provides sustained direct pressure with wrapping
3. **Wound packing** -- for large wounds in limbs, shoulders, groin (fill wound with gauze, apply pressure)
4. **Pressure points** -- compress artery against bone upstream of the wound
5. **Tourniquet** -- when all above fail, or when situation demands immediate definitive control

---

## Tourniquet Conversion (Professional Responders Only)

In MDA's professional protocols, tourniquet conversion (replacing a tourniquet with direct pressure/bandaging) may be performed:
- Only under medical direction
- Only when circumstances have changed (e.g., patient evacuated to safe area, more responders available)
- Civilian bystanders should NEVER attempt conversion

## Warnings and Complications

### Limb Ischemia
- Tourniquets stop ALL blood flow below the application point
- Prolonged application risks tissue death
- During October 7, "several individuals who had tourniquets applied for extended periods experienced limb ischemia, necessitating subsequent amputation"
- Despite this risk, a tourniquet that saves a life is always the correct choice -- loss of a limb is preferable to loss of life

### Time Documentation
- Recording the application time is critical because hospital teams need this information to assess whether the limb can be saved
- If you forget to document, estimate the time as accurately as possible

## MDA "First Seven Minutes" Initiative

MDA's civilian training philosophy emphasises the critical window before professional help arrives (typically 7 minutes in urban areas). Core skills taught include:
- Hemorrhage control (including tourniquet application)
- CPR
- Choking management
- Basic wound care

This programme specifically targets the general public with the understanding that early bystander intervention saves lives.
