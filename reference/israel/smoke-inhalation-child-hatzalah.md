## Inhalation Poisoning Protocol (from Ichud Hatzalah)

### Overview
Injuries from inhaling dangerous substances affect the ABC (airway, breathing, circulation) system and pose life-threatening risks.

### Types of Inhalation Hazards

**Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning**
- Colourless, odourless toxic gas from incomplete combustion in enclosed spaces
- Binds to haemoglobin much more strongly than oxygen
- Prevents proper oxygen transport
- Causes multi-system hypoxia

**Cyanide Poisoning**
- Released when plastics, foam, and paints burn at low temperatures
- Prevents cells from producing energy by binding to mitochondrial enzymes
- Can be absorbed through inhalation, skin contact, or ingestion

**Organophosphate Poisoning**
- Agricultural pesticides causing continuous nerve cell stimulation
- Produces excessive secretions (tears, saliva, mucus, sweat)

**Chemical Burns to Airways**
- Cleaning products or vapours causing airway tissue damage
- Risk of complete or partial airway blockage

### Symptoms to Recognise
- Headaches, weakness, dizziness
- Persistent cough
- Stridor (noisy breathing)
- Difficulty swallowing
- Altered consciousness
- Seizures
- Respiratory distress
- Pink/reddish lips (CO poisoning)

### First Aid Steps
1. **Ensure responder safety** -- use protective equipment, maintain distance
2. Remove victim from exposure source to fresh air
3. Provide maximum oxygen concentration if available
4. Call emergency services (101 MDA, 102 Fire Department)
5. Position patient upright to ease breathing
6. Monitor breathing and provide supportive care
7. Be prepared to perform CPR if breathing stops

## Child-Specific Considerations

### From Ichud Hatzalah Paediatric Materials
- Children have anatomically smaller airways, more prone to obstruction
- Airway structure is softer and more easily compromised
- Infants are obligate nasal breathers initially
- Infants utilise approximately 95% of oxygen supply (vs 50% in adults)
- Children lose heat rapidly -- prevent hypothermia after rescue

### Why Children Are More Vulnerable to Inhalation Injuries
- Higher respiratory rate per kilogram = more toxin inhaled
- Smaller airway diameter = disproportionate impact from swelling
- Closer to ground level where heavier gases concentrate
- Less physiological reserve
- Faster progression to respiratory failure
- May not be able to self-evacuate or communicate symptoms

### Stridor in Children (from Respiratory Distress page)
- Noisy breathing from upper airway narrowing
- Common in children 3 months to 5 years
- Worsens in evenings and with crying
- After smoke exposure, stridor indicates significant airway compromise

### Management of Stridor (from Paediatric page)
- Calm the child
- Position upright
- Provide cool/fresh air
- Use cool mist humidifier if available
- Ensure adequate hydration
- **Danger signs requiring ALS**: visible respiratory distress, intercostal retractions, nasal flaring, pale skin, weakness

### Israeli Context
- Lag B'Omer bonfires: common cause of paediatric smoke inhalation
- Winter heating fires in apartments
- Tear gas exposure near security incidents
- Agricultural areas: organophosphate pesticide exposure
- Cleaning product inhalation in home settings
