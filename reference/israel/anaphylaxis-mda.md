## Definition

Anaphylaxis is a severe, acute, multi-system allergic reaction that is life-threatening. It can develop within seconds to hours of allergen exposure. Without immediate treatment it can be fatal -- over 50% of deaths from allergic reactions occur within the first 60 minutes of onset.

## Common Triggers

- Food allergens (nuts, milk protein, eggs, shellfish, sesame)
- Medications
- Insect stings (bee, wasp)
- Latex

## Signs and Symptoms

### Skin
- Localised or widespread rash, hives, itching, flushing
- Swelling around eyes, lips, mouth, tongue, neck, and throat
- Facial pallor, excessive sweating

### Respiratory
- Breathing difficulty, shortness of breath
- Stridor, wheezing, coughing
- Throat tightness or burning sensation
- Hypoxia

### Cardiovascular
- Weak or rapid pulse
- Low blood pressure
- Pallor or cyanosis
- Confusion or loss of consciousness
- Shock symptoms

### Gastrointestinal
- Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea
- Abdominal cramping and pain

## Diagnostic Criteria (Sampson Classification, as used in Israeli protocols)

High probability of anaphylaxis when any ONE of the following is met:

1. **Acute onset** (minutes to hours) with skin/mucosal involvement PLUS respiratory compromise OR hypotension/end-organ dysfunction
2. **Two or more** of the following occurring rapidly after exposure to a likely allergen: skin/mucosal symptoms, respiratory compromise, hypotension, persistent GI symptoms
3. **Sudden hypotension** after exposure to a known allergen for that patient

## First Aid Protocol (Lay Responder / BLS Level)

### Step 1 -- Scene Safety and Recognition
- Recognise signs of a severe allergic reaction (see symptoms above)
- Distinguish from a mild allergic reaction: involvement of breathing, circulation, or consciousness indicates anaphylaxis

### Step 2 -- Call MDA (101)
- Call **101** immediately
- Keep the phone on speaker and follow the dispatcher's instructions
- Call for help from bystanders

### Step 3 -- Airway Management
- If facial or tongue swelling is present, assume airway compromise
- Ensure the tongue is not obstructing the airway
- Remove any food debris from the mouth
- Loosen tight clothing around the neck and chest

### Step 4 -- Patient Positioning
- **If conscious and breathing**: Position the patient lying down (supine) with legs elevated, unless breathing difficulty is severe -- in that case, allow sitting upright
- **If unconscious and breathing**: Recovery position
- **If unconscious and not breathing**: Begin CPR

### Step 5 -- Administer Epinephrine Auto-Injector (EpiPen)
Epinephrine is the primary and most critical treatment. It works by:
- Constricting blood vessels (raises blood pressure, reduces swelling)
- Dilating bronchial airways (improves breathing)
- Stabilising mast cells (slows allergic cascade)

**EpiPen administration technique:**
1. Remove the EpiPen from its plastic case
2. Remove the blue safety cap
3. Hold with the orange tip pointing downward
4. Position at 90 degrees to the middle of the outer thigh
5. Press firmly (swing and jab) until a click is heard
6. Hold in place for **10 seconds** to ensure full delivery
7. Can be administered **through clothing** -- do not waste time removing trousers
8. After removal, massage the injection site for 10 seconds to aid absorption
9. Dispose of the used auto-injector in a sharps container (or hand to paramedics)

**Dosage:**
- Adults (over 50 kg): 0.3 mg auto-injector (standard adult EpiPen)
- Note: The MDA ALS protocol specifies 0.5 mg IM adrenaline for adults over 50 kg (this is the paramedic-level dose using a syringe, not the auto-injector)

### Step 6 -- Reassess
- Check the patient's condition after 5 minutes
- If no improvement, a **second dose** may be administered
- Epinephrine effects last approximately 20 minutes; symptoms may recur (biphasic reaction)

### Step 7 -- Monitor Until EMS Arrives
- Continuously monitor airway, breathing, and circulation
- Be prepared to begin CPR if the patient loses consciousness and stops breathing
- All suspected anaphylaxis requires hospital evaluation, even if symptoms resolve

## Additional Notes (from MDA ALS Protocol, April 2024)

The following are ALS-level (paramedic) interventions, not lay first aid, but included for completeness:

- **Epinephrine IM dosing by weight**: up to 10 kg: 0.01 mg/kg; 10-25 kg: 0.15 mg; 25-50 kg: 0.3 mg; over 50 kg: 0.5 mg
- **Repeat dosing**: Up to 3 doses at 10-minute intervals
- **IV access**: Crystalloid fluids (Ringer's lactate or normal saline) for hypotension
- **Oxygen**: Administer to any patient with respiratory distress
- **Antihistamines**: May be given for symptom relief but are NOT a substitute for epinephrine
- **Corticosteroids**: Per protocol, though emerging evidence questions their efficacy in preventing biphasic reactions
- **Airway**: Consider preventive intubation if airway swelling is progressing

## Israeli Legal Context

- Educational institutions in Israel are required to stock EpiPen auto-injectors
- Public venues with anaphylaxis risk (e.g., restaurants) may legally obtain EpiPens without a physician prescription
- MDA first aid kits at train stations and on trains include EpiPens
- MDA developed the **EPIMADA** app (in partnership with Bar-Ilan University) that sends proximity alerts to registered allergy patients who carry EpiPens, enabling them to assist nearby anaphylaxis victims
