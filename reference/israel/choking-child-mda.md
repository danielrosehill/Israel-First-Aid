## Recognition of Choking

### Signs of Severe (Complete) Airway Obstruction
- Inability to speak, cry, or make sounds
- Weak or absent cough that cannot expel the object
- Weak, strained, or wheezy breathing sounds
- Difficulty breathing with visible chest/rib retractions
- Bluish skin colour (cyanosis)
- Loss of consciousness (when airway remains blocked)

### Signs of Partial Obstruction
- Forceful coughing
- Ability to cry or make sounds
- Some air exchange still present

### Key Distinction from Adults
Young children may NOT grasp their throats (the universal choking sign). Caregivers must recognise the warning signs listed above.

---

## When NOT to Intervene

- If the child is coughing vigorously or crying strongly -- allow the body to attempt to expel the object naturally
- If the child aspirated a foreign body but is breathing normally -- do NOT attempt removal, as you risk pushing it deeper
- If an object is in the nose and blocking nasal breathing -- instruct the child to breathe through the mouth instead; do not attempt removal
- Do NOT use this protocol if choking is caused by asthma, infection/swelling (e.g. epiglottitis, croup), or head injury -- seek immediate medical care

## Protocol: Infant (Under 1 Year) -- Conscious

### Step 1: Call for help
- Have someone call 101 (MDA) immediately
- If alone: perform 1-2 minutes of intervention first, then call 101

### Step 2: Back Blows (5)
1. Place the infant face-down on your forearm, supporting the jaw and head with your hand
2. Rest your forearm on your thigh for support
3. Tilt the infant's head downward -- head must be lower than the body
4. Deliver up to **5 quick, forceful strikes** between the shoulder blades using the heel of your other hand

### Step 3: Chest Thrusts (5) -- if object not dislodged
1. Turn the infant face-up, supporting the body and head on your other arm
2. Locate an imaginary horizontal line between the nipples
3. Place **two fingers** just below this line at the centre of the chest (sternum)
4. Perform **5 chest compressions**, approximately **4 cm deep**
5. These should be gentle but firm -- avoid excessive force that could break ribs

### Step 4: Repeat
- Continue alternating **5 back blows** and **5 chest thrusts** until the object is expelled

### If Infant Loses Consciousness
- Stop back blows/chest thrusts
- Place infant on a firm, flat surface
- Call 101 if not already done
- Begin CPR (infant protocol): 30 compressions to 2 breaths
- Before each set of rescue breaths, look in the mouth -- remove any visible object (do NOT perform blind finger sweeps)
- If alone, perform 5 cycles (approximately 2 minutes) of CPR before calling 101

### CRITICAL WARNING: No Abdominal Thrusts for Infants
Abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver) are **CONTRAINDICATED** in infants under 1 year due to the risk of organ damage. Use only back blows and chest thrusts.

## Protocol: Child (1-8 Years) -- Conscious

### Step 1: Encourage coughing
- If the child can cough forcefully, encourage them to keep coughing
- The body maintains "spare air" that creates pressure when expelled -- coughing is the most effective mechanism

### Step 2: Call for help
- Have someone call 101 (MDA) immediately

### Step 3: Abdominal Thrusts (Heimlich Manoeuvre)
1. Stand or kneel behind the child (adjust to child's height)
2. Wrap your arms around the child's waist
3. Make a fist and place it **above the navel, below the rib cage**
4. Grasp your fist with your other hand
5. Perform **quick, forceful thrusts inward and upward**
6. Do NOT use excessive force that would lift the child off the ground
7. Continue until the object is expelled

### Alternative positioning (from MDA sources)
- For smaller children: position the child against your leg with your fist placed precisely below the ribcage

### If Child Loses Consciousness
- Stop abdominal thrusts
- Lay the child flat on a firm surface
- Call 101 if not already done
- Begin CPR (child protocol): 30 compressions to 2 breaths (one-hand technique)
- Before each set of rescue breaths, check the mouth -- remove any visible object
- If alone, perform 5 cycles (approximately 2 minutes) of CPR before calling 101

---

## Things You Must NOT Do

1. **Never insert fingers blindly** into the mouth to probe or search for objects (blind finger sweeps)
2. **Never force anything into the mouth** -- you risk pushing the object deeper
3. **Do not attempt mouth manipulation** if teeth are clenched
4. **Do not give food or fluids** to a choking victim
5. **Do not use abdominal thrusts on infants** (under 1 year)
6. **Do not intervene** if the child is coughing effectively or breathing normally after aspiration

## Special Situations

### Pregnant or Obese Child/Adolescent
- Use **chest thrusts** instead of abdominal thrusts
- Position fist on the middle of the breastbone (sternum)

### Child Becomes Unresponsive -- CPR Summary
- **Compression-to-ventilation ratio**: 30:2 (solo rescuer), 15:2 (two rescuers)
- **Compression depth**: Approximately 1/3 of chest depth
- **Infant airway positioning**: "Look to sky" -- slight head tilt only; full head tilt-back blocks the infant airway
- **Child airway positioning**: Standard head-tilt chin-lift

## Choking Prevention (Israeli Context)

### High-Risk Foods (Ages 0-5)
- Nuts (avoid entirely until age 5 unless finely ground)
- Popcorn
- Hard candies
- Uncooked hard vegetables (carrots, celery)
- Cylindrical/round foods: hot dogs, cherry tomatoes, grapes, cucumbers -- **must be cut lengthwise into small pieces**
- Thick nut butters (spread thin, not in clumps)

### General Prevention
- Supervise feeding closely; keep children seated at a table
- Remove small toys, coins, buttons, and batteries from infants' reach
- Dr. Moshe Yosef (Meuhedet pediatrician) notes: "Foreign body choking is a leading cause of illness and death in children, especially under the age of 4" due to narrower airways, underdeveloped chewing mechanism, and insufficient cough reflex

## Key Statistic

According to United Hatzalah training materials: "Most pediatric cardiac arrests result from choking" (רוב מקרי ההחייאה בילדים קטנים ובתינוקות נגרמות כתוצאה מחנק). This makes choking the single most important paediatric first aid skill.
