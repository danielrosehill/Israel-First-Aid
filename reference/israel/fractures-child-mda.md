## Children and Fractures -- Israeli Context

- Children's bones are softer, more flexible, and more porous than adult bones, making them prone to unique fracture types
- Common settings: playground falls, sports injuries (school PE, after-school activities), bicycle and scooter accidents, trampoline injuries
- Israel's warm climate means more year-round outdoor activity and correspondingly more opportunity for musculoskeletal injury
- MDA first responder training includes paediatric immobilisation techniques as part of the standard 22-hour and 44-hour first aid courses

## Types of Fractures in Children

### Greenstick Fracture (שבר ענף ירוק)
- The bone bends and partially breaks, like a young green twig
- Unique to children because their bones are softer and more flexible
- Signs: swelling, tenderness, visible bend or deformity in the limb; child may still be able to partially move the limb but with pain
- Requires medical evaluation and treatment (casting)

### Growth Plate Fracture (שבר לוחית גדילה)
- Affects the cartilaginous growth plates at the ends of long bones
- Growth plates are the weakest part of the developing skeleton
- Higher risk of affecting future bone development and growth
- Requires prompt medical attention; may need surgical intervention
- More common around the wrist, ankle, and knee

### Complete Fracture (שבר שלם)
- Bone breaks all the way through
- Less common in young children than greenstick fractures
- More common in older children and adolescents as bones become less flexible

### Open (Compound) Fracture (שבר פתוח)
- Bone pierces through the skin -- a medical emergency
- Risk of severe bleeding and infection
- Requires immediate call to MDA (101)

## Sprains in Children (נקע)

- Stretching or tearing of ligaments around a joint
- Less common in young children (whose ligaments are stronger than their growth plates -- what appears to be a sprain in a child may actually be a growth plate fracture)
- Signs: pain, swelling, bruising, difficulty moving the joint
- Any suspected "sprain" in a child should be evaluated by a doctor to rule out growth plate injury

## Signs and Symptoms of Fracture

Recognise a suspected fracture by these classic signs (as described in MDA training):

1. **Severe pain** that worsens with any attempt to move the limb
2. **Visible swelling and bruising** around the injury site
3. **Limb deformity** -- limb appears crooked, bent at an unnatural angle, or shortened
4. **Inability to move** the injured area or bear weight
5. **Tenderness** when the area is gently touched
6. **Audible snap or crack** at the time of injury (sometimes reported by the child)
7. **Child refuses to use the limb** or holds it protectively against their body

### Child-Specific Considerations
- Young children may not be able to articulate where or how much it hurts
- A child who suddenly stops using an arm or leg after a fall should be evaluated for fracture
- Irritability, crying, and refusal to be consoled may be the primary indicators in very young children

## Step-by-Step First Aid Protocol

### 1. Stay Calm and Reassure the Child

- **Emotional management is critical** -- a frightened child in pain will tense muscles, increasing pain and making the injury harder to manage
- Speak calmly and reassuringly; get down to the child's eye level
- Explain simply what you are doing and why ("I'm going to help your arm feel better")
- Do not display panic or alarm -- children take cues from adults
- Allow a parent or trusted adult to stay close for comfort
- Distraction techniques (talking, counting, singing) can help manage the child's fear

### 2. Call MDA -- 101

- Call Magen David Adom at **101** for guidance and to dispatch an ambulance if needed
- Put the phone on speaker so you can follow dispatcher instructions while providing care
- Describe: the child's age, location and nature of the injury, whether bone is visible, level of consciousness, and any bleeding
- The MDA dispatcher will guide you through appropriate steps

### 3. Do NOT Move or Realign the Injury

- **NEVER attempt to straighten, pull, or push a bone back into place**
- Hebrew principle: "לעולם אל תנסו ליישר את הגפה, למשוך אותה או להחזיר עצם למקומה" (Never try to straighten the limb, pull it, or return a bone to its place)
- Such actions can tear arteries and nerves, converting a simple fracture into a life-threatening injury
- If the limb is in an unnatural position, leave it as found
- This applies especially to children, where growth plate damage from manipulation could affect future development

### 4. Immobilise the Injury

The MDA principle is: **"Minimum movement, maximum stability"** (תנועה מינימלית, יציבות מקסימלית)

The best immobilisation for non-professionals is **immobilisation to the body itself** or to a stable surface:

#### Arm Injuries (שבר יד)
- Support the injured arm using the child's healthy hand
- Create an improvised sling using a shirt, scarf, sweater, or any available fabric
- Fold fabric into a triangle, place the injured arm inside, and tie around the child's neck
- Secure the sling to the child's chest to prevent swinging
- The goal is to prevent all movement of the injured limb

#### Leg Injuries (שבר רגל)
- Ask the child not to move the leg
- Place pillows, rolled blankets, towels, or clothing on both sides of the leg to prevent involuntary movement
- For additional stability, gently bandage the injured leg to the healthy leg with padding between them
- For suspected hip fractures: do NOT move the child; call for ambulance transport immediately

#### Spine or Neck Injuries (חשד לפגיעה בעמוד השדרה)
- **Do NOT move the child at all**
- Call 101 immediately -- ambulance transport only
- Hold the child's head steady if you must prevent movement
- This is a life-threatening emergency

#### General Splinting Principles for Children
- If you are not a certified medic, do not try to build complex splints
- Improvised splints can be made from cardboard, rolled newspapers, magazines, rulers, or any rigid material
- The splint must extend beyond the joints above and below the fracture
- Pad the splint with soft material (clothing, towels) to prevent pressure on the injury
- Tie the splint in place with fabric strips, scarves, or bandages -- firm but not tight enough to restrict circulation
- Check fingers or toes below the splint for warmth, colour, and sensation -- if they turn blue, cold, or numb, loosen immediately

### 5. Apply Cold

- Apply an ice pack or cold compress wrapped in a towel (never direct ice on skin)
- Apply for **15-minute intervals** to reduce pain and swelling
- Remove for 15 minutes, then reapply
- Be especially gentle with children -- explain that it may feel cold but will help
- In Israel's hot climate, cold packs from a first aid kit or even a bag of frozen vegetables wrapped in cloth can be used

### 6. Remove Constrictive Items

- Remove jewellery, watches, bracelets, and rings from the affected hand or fingers immediately
- Remove shoes from an injured foot or ankle
- Swelling occurs rapidly after a fracture, and constrictive items can cut off circulation

### 7. Manage Bleeding (Open Fractures Only)

- If bone is protruding through the skin (open/compound fracture), this is a medical emergency
- Control bleeding through **indirect pressure** around (not on) the wound
- Cover the exposed bone with a sterile dressing or the cleanest available cloth
- **Do NOT attempt to push the bone back in**
- Apply a tourniquet only if haemorrhage is severe and uncontrollable (MDA protocol for mass casualty / security events)
- Keep the MDA dispatcher on speaker for continuous guidance

### 8. Prevent Shock

- Keep the child warm with a blanket or clothing (but avoid overheating in Israel's climate)
- Keep the child lying down if possible
- Elevate the legs slightly if no spinal injury is suspected and the child is not vomiting
- Monitor breathing and consciousness

### 9. Do Not Give Food or Drink

- Avoid giving the child food or drink, as surgery under general anaesthesia may be required
- If the child is very thirsty, moisten their lips with a wet cloth

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT attempt to straighten or realign the bone** -- this can damage arteries, nerves, and growth plates
- **Do NOT move the child unnecessarily** -- especially if spinal injury is suspected
- **Do NOT apply heat** to the injury (no hot compresses, no warm water)
- **Do NOT apply ice directly to skin** -- always wrap in cloth
- **Do NOT tie splints or bandages too tightly** -- monitor circulation below the injury
- **Do NOT allow the child to bear weight** on an injured leg
- **Do NOT remove clothing by pulling over the injury** -- cut it away if necessary
- **Do NOT give aspirin** to children (risk of Reye's syndrome); use paracetamol (acamol) or ibuprofen (nurofen for children) if needed for pain

## When to Call 101 (MDA) Immediately

Call MDA at 101 for:

- **Open fractures** -- bone visible through skin
- **Suspected spinal or neck injury**
- **Suspected hip or pelvis fracture**
- **Loss of sensation or circulation** below the injury (cold, blue, numb fingers/toes)
- **Severe deformity** of the limb
- **Heavy bleeding** accompanying the fracture
- **Child is unconscious, confused, or having difficulty breathing**
- **Multiple injuries** (e.g., from a fall, traffic accident, or security incident)
- **Any fracture in an infant or very young child** -- always seek medical evaluation

For less severe suspected fractures or sprains, transport the child to the nearest emergency department (with the limb immobilised) or call 101 for guidance.

## Prevention -- Israeli Context

### Playground and Sports Safety
- Ensure playground equipment meets Israeli safety standards
- Supervise children on trampolines -- a leading cause of paediatric fractures in Israel
- Ensure proper protective equipment for sports (helmets for cycling, wrist guards for skateboarding/rollerblading)
- School PE teachers are required to hold first aid certification (MDA 22-hour course per Ministry of Education requirement)

### Home Safety
- Secure furniture that can tip over onto children
- Use safety gates on stairs for young children
- Ensure adequate lighting to prevent falls

### Climate Considerations
- Israel's year-round warm weather means children are active outdoors more often
- Dehydration and fatigue increase risk of falls and injuries
- Ensure children stay hydrated during outdoor activities
