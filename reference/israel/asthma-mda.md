## Recognition — Mild vs Severe Attack

### Early Warning Signs (Mild Attack)
- Persistent coughing (especially at night)
- Shortness of breath
- Weakness and fatigue after physical activity
- Wheezing or coughing after exertion
- Irritability, mood changes
- Nasal congestion or runny nose
- Headache or sore throat

### Severe Attack Signs — CALL 101 IMMEDIATELY
- **Cannot complete a sentence** due to shortness of breath
- Severe wheezing, particularly early morning or at night
- Extreme exertion of chest muscles to breathe (accessory muscle use, visible rib retraction)
- Breathing rate exceeds 20 breaths per minute in adults
- No improvement after using rescue inhaler
- Bluish discolouration of lips or fingertips (cyanosis)
- Difficulty eating, speaking, or sleeping
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Silent chest (no wheezing despite obvious distress — dangerous sign indicating minimal air movement)

## First Aid Steps

### 1. Position the Person Upright
- Help the casualty sit upright or lean slightly forward
- This position makes breathing easier
- Do NOT lay them down

### 2. Reassure and Calm
- Stay with the person; do not leave them alone
- Speak calmly; anxiety worsens bronchospasm

### 3. Administer Rescue Inhaler
- If the person has their own inhaler (Ventolin / mashaf), provide it immediately
- If they cannot use it themselves, operate the inhaler for them
- **Dosage**: 2-4 puffs initially
- Wait several minutes to assess effect
- If no improvement, repeat 2-4 puffs
- Use a spacer device (merkhav / "spacer") if available — improves drug delivery significantly

### 4. Monitor and Reassess
- Allow several minutes for the person to regain normal breathing
- Watch for worsening: skin colour changes, increased breathing effort, decreased responsiveness

### 5. Call MDA at 101
- If symptoms do not improve within minutes of inhaler use
- If the person cannot speak in full sentences
- If lips or fingertips turn blue
- If the person loses consciousness
- If no inhaler is available

## Medications — Israeli Brand Names

### Rescue Inhalers (Short-Acting Beta-Agonists / SABA)
- **Ventolin (ונטולין)** — Salbutamol (Salbutamol sulfate 200mcg per puff)
  - The most common rescue inhaler in Israel
  - Works within approximately 5 minutes
  - Duration of action: approximately 4-6 hours
  - Available at all pharmacies in Israel (requires prescription)
- **Bricanyl (בריקניל)** — Terbutaline
  - Alternative rescue inhaler

### Controller Medications (Not for Acute Attacks)
- Inhaled corticosteroids (e.g., Budesonide, Fluticasone)
- These treat underlying inflammation but do NOT provide immediate relief during an attack

### Hospital Treatment
- Oxygen supplementation
- Nebulised bronchodilators (Ventolin via nebuliser)
- Systemic steroids (oral, IV, or IM)
- Magnesium sulfate IV (for severe attacks)

---

## Important Notes

- **Ventolin provides symptom relief but does NOT treat the underlying inflammatory process** — the Israeli Asthma Association specifically warns about this
- An alternative inhaler may be used if the person's own inhaler is not available
- Ventolin is contraindicated for children under 4, and should be used with caution in people with heart disease or thyroid hyperactivity
- Side effects include tremors, nervousness, dry throat, dizziness, headache, and rapid heartbeat

## Israeli Context

- **Climate triggers**: Khamsin (hot, dry desert wind) conditions can trigger attacks; also spring pollen season (February-April)
- **Air quality**: Urban pollution in Tel Aviv and Haifa industrial zone
- **Dead Sea region**: Unique atmospheric pressure may affect some asthmatics
- **Availability**: Ventolin is widely available at Israeli pharmacies (Beit Merkakhat) with a prescription. Many asthmatics carry one at all times.
- **Kupot Kholim**: All four health funds (Clalit, Maccabi, Meuhedet, Leumit) cover asthma medications in the health basket (sal habriut)
