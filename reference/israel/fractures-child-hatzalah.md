## Research Findings

### What United Hatzalah Publishes

United Hatzalah's public-facing websites (1221.org.il, israelrescue.org, hatzalah.org.il) do **not** publish detailed first aid protocols for fractures and sprains aimed at the general public. Their publicly available life-saving guides (as of 2026-03-15) cover:

1. Eye injury first aid
2. Animal bites
3. Severe diarrhoea management
4. Post-accident procedures

Fractures and sprains are **not** among the currently published online guides.

### Safe Family Program (משפחה בטוחה)

United Hatzalah runs the "Safe Family" training program throughout Israel:

- **Duration**: 4 hours
- **Target audience**: Parents, caregivers, teachers, educational staff
- **Course content** (as described on the program page):
  - Basic resuscitation principles
  - Choking treatment in adults and infants
  - Emergency response for common situations including heat cramps, burns, and haemorrhage control
- **Cost**: Approximately 1,800 NIS per course (20 participants)
- **Contact**: [email protected] / 02-5371221
- **Sponsor partner**: Nurofen for Children

The Safe Family program description does **not** explicitly list fractures or sprains in its published curriculum summary, though these topics may be covered within the broader "common situations" module. The 44-hour first aid provider course (available via shop.1221.org.il) would cover fracture management as part of the standard Israeli first aid curriculum.

### United Hatzalah's Role in Fracture Response

United Hatzalah's operational model is relevant to understanding fracture response in Israel:

- Over 8,000 volunteers (ambucycle riders, paramedics, EMTs, physicians) nationwide
- Average response time: approximately 90 seconds
- Volunteers carry first aid equipment including splints and immobilisation materials
- Dispatch via the LifeCompass app and 1221 call centre
- United Hatzalah responders provide pre-hospital stabilisation and immobilisation before MDA ambulance transport

In practice, a United Hatzalah volunteer responding to a child's fracture would follow standard Israeli first aid protocols consistent with MDA training (see fractures-child-mda.md), as all United Hatzalah volunteers are certified through courses that align with the MDA/Ministry of Health curriculum.

### Alignment with MDA Protocols

United Hatzalah volunteer training follows the same Israeli first aid standards as MDA. The 22-hour and 44-hour first aid courses (offered by both MDA and United Hatzalah) cover:

- Fracture recognition and assessment
- Immobilisation and splinting techniques
- Paediatric considerations
- Open fracture management
- When to call for advanced medical transport

There are **no known differences** between United Hatzalah and MDA regarding fracture first aid protocols. Both organisations train to the same national standard.

## Summary

United Hatzalah does not publish standalone fracture/sprain first aid protocols on their public website. Their training programmes teach fracture management as part of broader first aid certification, following the same Israeli national standards as MDA. For the detailed protocol, refer to **fractures-child-mda.md** in this directory.

## Gaps and Limitations

- United Hatzalah's public website does not include fractures or sprains among its published first aid guides. Their focus online is on life-threatening emergencies (cardiac arrest, choking, severe bleeding) and their rapid-response operational model.
- The "Safe Family" 4-hour course curriculum summary does not explicitly mention fractures, though the topic may be covered in practice.
- United Hatzalah's internal training materials and volunteer protocols are not publicly accessible. These would contain detailed fracture management procedures consistent with the national first aid curriculum.
- The 1221.org.il Hebrew site blocked some content extraction; information was gathered from search engine excerpts and successfully fetched pages.
- No child-specific fracture guidance from United Hatzalah was found in any publicly available source.
