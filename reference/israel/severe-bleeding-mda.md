## Protocol: How to Stop Bleeding (כיצד לעצור דימום)

### Scene Safety

1. Ensure your own safety before approaching the casualty.
2. Wear disposable gloves if available (standard in Israeli first aid kits).

### Step 1 -- Call for Help

- Call **101** (Magen David Adom) immediately for any severe or uncontrolled bleeding.
- Provide location, number of casualties, and nature of injuries.

### Step 2 -- Apply Direct Pressure (Primary Method)

1. Apply strong, direct pressure to the source of bleeding -- **initially with your bare or gloved hand**.
2. Replace hand pressure with a **sterile bandage, towel, or clean cloth** as soon as available.
3. Maintain **continuous, firm pressure** -- do not lift the dressing to check the wound, as this disrupts clot formation.
4. If the first dressing soaks through, **add additional layers on top** (do not remove the original dressing). Use a triangular bandage, sheet, or clean towel if needed.

### Step 3 -- Apply a Pressure Dressing (Israeli Bandage / Emergency Bandage)

The Israeli Emergency Bandage (invented by Bernard Bar-Natan) is standard equipment in Israeli civilian and military first aid kits. Application:

1. Apply the white sterile pad directly over the wound.
2. Wrap the elastic bandage around the injured area, passing through the pressure bar.
3. Reverse direction and continue wrapping to apply circumferential pressure.
4. Secure using the built-in closure bar.
5. **Warning**: The elastic component can occlude arteries -- verify distal pulse after application. The bandage should be tight enough to control bleeding but not so tight as to act as a tourniquet (unless that is the intent).

### Step 4 -- Elevate the Limb

- If possible, raise the injured limb above heart level to reduce blood flow to the area.
- This is an adjunct to direct pressure, not a replacement.

### Step 5 -- Tourniquet (חוסם עורקים) -- When Direct Pressure Fails

**Indications for tourniquet use:**
- Amputation or partial amputation
- Bleeding that cannot be controlled by direct pressure
- Severe crush injury with exposed bone
- Multiple wound sites on the same limb
- Cardiac arrest requiring hands-free resuscitation
- Scene safety concerns requiring rapid hemorrhage control (e.g., under fire, mass casualty)

**Application:**
1. Place the tourniquet **5-10 cm above the source of bleeding** (proximal, toward the heart).
2. Tighten the windlass/mechanism until bleeding **completely stops**.
3. Secure the windlass in place.
4. **Record the time of application** -- write on the casualty's forehead or another visible location: "TOURNIQUET" + time in 4-digit format (e.g., "TQ 1430").
5. Leave approximately 15 cm of strap, which can be folded in an "R" shape for visibility.

**Warnings:**
- A tourniquet completely blocks blood flow distal to the application point, risking tissue death and gangrene if left too long.
- **Do not remove or loosen a tourniquet once applied** unless instructed by a paramedic or physician. Conversion (replacing a tourniquet with direct pressure) requires paramedic/physician authorisation.
- A properly applied tourniquet will cause significant pain -- this is expected.

### Step 6 -- Embedded/Impaled Objects

- **Never remove an embedded object** (knife, shrapnel, glass, etc.) from the body.
- Stabilise the object in place using padding and bandages around it.
- Apply pressure around the object, not on it.

### Step 7 -- Monitor for Shock

- Watch for signs of hypovolemic shock: rapid pulse, pale/cool/clammy skin, confusion, rapid breathing.
- Keep the casualty warm and lying down.
- Do not give food or drink.
- Reassure the casualty and wait for MDA responders.

## Tourniquet Types Used in Israel

| Type | Context |
|------|---------|
| **CAT (Combat Application Tourniquet)** | Most common in civilian and military use; recommended by CoTCCC; windlass-based one-handed application |
| **Israeli/LST (Life Saving Tourniquet)** | Developed by Yaakov Tsadik (former MDA volunteer); handles both extremity and junctional bleeding |
| **Improvised ("Russian") Tourniquet** | Historically used by IDF; triangular bandage + rigid object as windlass; being phased out in favour of commercial devices |

## Civilian Tourniquet Context

Israel has one of the highest rates of civilian tourniquet training in the world due to its security environment. Key points:

- MDA's **"First 7 Minutes" program** trains civilians in hemorrhage control, triage, and emergency communication in a 4-hour seminar. The program teaches bleeding control **both with and without tourniquets**.
- MDA conducts **Stop the Bleed** trainings in shelters and community settings, particularly intensified since October 2023.
- CAT tourniquets are available in many public trauma kits and civilian first aid kits across Israel.
- Israeli law and MDA guidelines encourage civilian tourniquet use in life-threatening bleeding.

## Hemostatic Agents

- **QuikClot Combat Gauze (QCG)** is used by MDA and IDF in prehospital settings. A large Israeli case series (122 patients, IDF Medical Corps) demonstrated 79% hemorrhage arrest rate with no adverse effects.
- Hemostatic gauze is primarily for junctional wounds (groin, neck, axilla) where tourniquets cannot be applied.
- Civilian first aid kits in Israel may include hemostatic gauze but it is not standard in basic kits.

## Advanced (ALS) Context

MDA's ALS protocols (2024 edition) include:
- Tranexamic acid (TXA) administration for hemorrhagic shock
- Freeze-dried plasma (FDP) -- used by Israeli EMS for over a decade
- Low-titer group O whole blood (LTOWB) -- deployed on MDA mobile intensive care units since 2023
- These are paramedic/physician-level interventions, not civilian first aid

## Summary of Key Differences from US Protocols

| Aspect | Israel (MDA) | USA (AHA/ARC) |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Civilian tourniquet training emphasis | Very high -- integrated into public preparedness | Growing but less universal |
| Israeli Bandage | Standard kit item | Common in tactical/trauma kits, less in household |
| Emergency number | 101 | 911 |
| Hemostatic gauze | Increasingly available in civilian kits | Mostly tactical/professional |
| Tourniquet time marking | On forehead or visible area | On tourniquet strap or visible area |
| Mass casualty / security context | Heavily emphasised | Increasingly taught post-2013 |
