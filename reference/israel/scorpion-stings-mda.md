## Dangerous Scorpion Species in Israel

Israel has approximately 21 scorpion species, of which five are considered dangerous to humans:

| Species | Hebrew Name | Common Name | Distribution | Danger Level |
|---------|-------------|-------------|--------------|--------------|
| *Leiurus quinquestriatus* | עקרב צהוב | Yellow scorpion / Deathstalker | Nearly ubiquitous across Israel | Most dangerous -- potentially lethal |
| *Hottentotta judaicus* | עקרב שחור עבה-זנב | Thick-tailed black scorpion | Throughout Israel | Venom potency rivals the yellow scorpion |
| *Androctonus australis* | הרוגים דרומי | Southern killer scorpion | North Africa and southern Sinai | Highly dangerous |
| *Compsobuthus werneri* | עקרב דו-צבתי | Two-clawed scorpion | Central and southern Israel | Painful but less dangerous |
| *Scorpio maurus* | עקרב שחור עברי | Hebrew black scorpion | Various regions | Least dangerous of the five |

**Important**: The common myth that black scorpions are not dangerous is FALSE. Several black scorpion species in Israel are medically significant.

The **deathstalker scorpion** (*Leiurus quinquestriatus*) is the most dangerous species in Israel and one of the most dangerous scorpions in the world. It is common throughout the country, including in the Negev desert and urban periphery areas.

## First Aid Protocol -- What To Do

### 1. Call MDA immediately -- 101

Call Magen David Adom at **101**. Request an ambulance. Follow dispatch instructions until the medical team arrives.

### 2. Keep the victim calm and still

- Reassure the victim; reduce stress and anxiety
- Have the victim lie down
- **Minimize movement** -- movement and elevated heart rate accelerate venom spread through the bloodstream
- Panic and agitation worsen the clinical outcome

### 3. Immobilise the affected limb

- Keep the stung limb as still as possible
- Position the limb at or below heart level

### 4. Remove constrictive items

- Remove jewellery, watches, rings, and tight clothing near the sting site
- The area may swell

### 5. Monitor for systemic reaction

Watch for warning signs of severe envenomation:
- Restlessness, altered consciousness, confusion
- Difficulty breathing or swallowing
- Excessive sweating, salivation, or tearing
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat
- Seizures or muscle twitching
- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
- Changes in blood pressure (hypertension or hypotension)

**Any systemic symptom beyond local pain requires urgent hospital transport.**

## What NOT To Do

These prohibitions are consistent across MDA training, Israeli Ministry of Health guidance, and corroborating Israeli sources:

| Prohibited Action | Hebrew | Reason |
|-------------------|--------|--------|
| Do NOT apply a tourniquet | אין להניח חוסם עורקים | Traps venom locally, causes tissue damage; does not prevent systemic spread |
| Do NOT suck the venom out | אין למצוץ את הארס | Ineffective and potentially harmful |
| Do NOT cut the sting area | אין לחתוך את אזור העקיצה | Increases infection risk, does not remove venom |
| Do NOT give food or drink | אין לתת לנפגע לשתות | Risk of venom dispersal; may complicate hospital treatment |

### Conflicting guidance on cooling / ice

There is a notable discrepancy among Israeli authorities on whether to apply ice or cold compresses:

- **Israeli Ministry of Health (2026)**: Explicitly states "do NOT cool the area" (לא לקרר את האזור). Recent studies cited suggest cooling may cause more harm than benefit.
- **MDA search snippets (news sources citing MDA)**: Some MDA-attributed quotes recommend cooling the sting area with cold water or ice to relieve pain and slow venom spread.
- **United Hatzalah**: Recommends applying a cold compress for 10 minutes with breaks, avoiding direct ice contact on skin.

**Resolution**: Given the conflict, this protocol follows the more conservative Ministry of Health position. If cooling is applied, it should be indirect (ice wrapped in cloth) and brief. When in doubt, follow MDA dispatcher instructions on the phone (101).

## Symptoms of Scorpion Envenomation

### Local symptoms
- Immediate, intense pain at the sting site
- Mild to moderate swelling and redness
- Numbness or tingling at the sting site

**Note**: Local symptom severity does NOT reliably predict systemic danger.

### Systemic symptoms (severe cases)

**Neurological**: Restlessness, altered consciousness, seizures, muscle rigidity, elevated muscle tone, temperature dysregulation (hypothermia or hyperthermia), ineffective breathing

**Cardiovascular**: Tachycardia, hypertension (sympathetic activation), arrhythmias, heart failure progressing to pulmonary edema, cardiogenic shock with severe hypotension. Caused by excessive catecholamine (dopamine and adrenaline) release.

**Autonomic**: Excessive sweating, salivation, tearing, urinary retention (sympathetic). Bradycardia, diarrhea (parasympathetic activation -- may produce opposite effects).

### High-risk groups
- Children under 8 years old (see child protocol)
- Elderly patients
- Those with underlying cardiac or respiratory conditions

## Hospital Treatment

### Observation
- All scorpion sting victims should be observed in the emergency department for a minimum of **6 hours** from the time of sting (Israel Poison Information Center guideline)
- Admission is required if symptoms beyond local pain develop

### Antivenom
- Scorpion antivenom is available in Israeli hospitals
- Administered in rare, severe cases based on clinical presentation
- Decision to administer is made by the treating physician

### Supportive care
- Pain management (analgesics)
- Cardiac monitoring for arrhythmias
- Respiratory support if needed
- Seizure management (benzodiazepines) if seizures occur
- Atropine for excessive parasympathetic stimulation (ALS level)

## Israel-Specific Context

- **Peak season**: Scorpion stings are most common in warm months (spring through autumn). MDA reported 212 scorpion sting cases from June to August 2023 alone, with 109 in the Negev.
- **Climate**: Israel's desert regions (Negev, Arava, Judean Desert) have the highest scorpion density. Extreme heat drives scorpions to seek shelter in shoes, clothing, and buildings.
- **Prevention**:
  - Wear closed, high-top shoes outdoors, especially at night
  - Walk in well-lit areas
  - Check shoes, clothing, and bedding before use
  - Clear campsite areas of rocks and debris
  - Avoid placing items directly on the ground
  - Do not reach into dark crevices or under rocks
- **Emergency numbers**: MDA = **101**, Police = **100**, Fire = **102**
