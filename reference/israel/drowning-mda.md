## Definition

**Drowning (טביעה)**: A process resulting in respiratory impairment from submersion or immersion in liquid. Israeli medical sources classify drowning into two types:

- **Dry drowning (טביעה יבשה)**: Laryngospasm prevents fluid from entering the lungs; death occurs from asphyxiation without aspiration
- **Wet drowning (טביעה רטובה)**: Fluid enters the lungs, causing pulmonary damage and asphyxiation

Both types result in hypoxia (oxygen deprivation) to the body and brain.

**Secondary drowning (טביעה משנית)**: A delayed complication where small amounts of aspirated water cause progressive pulmonary edema hours after the initial incident. Symptoms can develop 1-24 hours after water exposure. (See United Hatzalah reference for more detail.)

## MDA Protocol: Step-by-Step

### 1. Ensure your own safety

- Do NOT enter the water to rescue the person unless you are a trained swimmer/lifeguard
- Use reaching aids (pole, rope, flotation device) if possible
- Alert a lifeguard if one is present

### 2. Call for help

- **Call 101** (Magen David Adom) immediately
- Keep the dispatcher on speakerphone
- Provide your exact location (beach name, pool address, GPS coordinates if possible)
- If another person is present, send them to locate the nearest **AED (defibrillator)**
  - AEDs are commonly found at lifeguard stations, first aid rooms, and public facilities with 500+ occupants in Israel

### 3. Remove the person from the water

- Extract the drowning person from the water as quickly as possible
- Do not endanger yourself in the process
- Move them to a dry area away from the water's edge

### 4. Check responsiveness and breathing

- Check if the person is responsive (call to them, tap shoulder)
- Observe the chest: is it rising and falling?

### 5. If the person is NOT breathing -- begin CPR

- Position the person **on their back** on a firm, flat surface away from the water
- Begin **chest compressions** at a rate of **100 compressions per minute**
  - MDA's public guidance emphasises compressions as the primary action for lay rescuers
  - Use the song "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees as a tempo guide (referenced in MDA-cited Israeli media)
- **Continue CPR without stopping** until MDA paramedics arrive

#### Rescue breaths (for trained rescuers)

MDA trains its volunteers and professional staff in full CPR including ventilation. For drowning specifically:

- Drowning is a **hypoxic** (oxygen-deprivation) emergency, making rescue breaths especially important
- Trained rescuers should provide rescue breaths as part of CPR
- The international consensus (AHA 2024 update, which Israeli EMS aligns with) recommends the **ABC approach** for drowning: Airway, Breathing, then Compressions -- rather than the standard CAB sequence used for cardiac arrest
- Ratio: **2 rescue breaths to 30 compressions** (standard BLS ratio)
- MDA updated its protocol approximately 2020-2021 to allow CPR to begin on rescue boats rather than waiting to reach shore

#### AED use

- If an AED is available, use it
- **Critical**: Dry the patient's chest and move them to a dry surface before applying AED pads
- Follow the AED's voice prompts

### 6. Critical warning

**NEVER attempt to remove water from the victim's lungs.** MDA explicitly states this is:
- Impossible to achieve
- Dangerous to the victim
- A waste of critical time

Do not attempt the Heimlich manoeuvre, turning the person upside down, or any other method to "drain" water.

### 7. If the person is breathing but unconscious

- Place them in the **recovery position** (on their side)
- Monitor breathing continuously until MDA arrives
- Keep them warm; remove wet clothing if possible and cover with dry blankets or clothing

### 8. After recovery / regaining consciousness

Even if the person regains consciousness and appears stable:

- **Mandatory hospital transport** is required
- Do NOT allow the person to walk on their own or refuse treatment
- Dangerous **electrolyte imbalances** (changes in blood salt levels) can develop after drowning that may threaten the person's life even if they appear well
- Risk of **secondary drowning** (delayed pulmonary edema) -- symptoms can appear hours later

## Israel-Specific Context

### Drowning locations and statistics (2024)

Beach drownings in Israel increased **37% in 2024** compared to previous years. Breakdown by location (2024 data from Israel Life Saving Federation):

| Location | Drowning incidents |
|---|---|
| Mediterranean Sea | 189 |
| Public pools | 52 |
| Private pools | 43 |
| Dead Sea | 28 |
| Lake Kinneret (Sea of Galilee) | 26 |
| Red Sea (Eilat) | 12 |
| Guesthouse pools | 8 |

Demographics: 74% male, 26% female. Children under 10 account for 26% of all drownings; the majority of child drownings occur in swimming pools.

### Mediterranean beaches

- Western winds (especially afternoons) create strong currents along Israel's Mediterranean coastline
- Most adult drownings in Israel occur in the Mediterranean Sea
- Major risk: swimming at **unpatrolled beaches** or outside lifeguard hours
- Flags system: **Black flag** = swimming prohibited; **Red flag** = dangerous conditions, swimming not recommended

### Dead Sea -- unique dangers

The Dead Sea presents **unique drowning hazards** distinct from other bodies of water:

- **Extreme salinity** (~34% salt concentration, ~10x normal seawater) makes sinking nearly impossible but creates different dangers
- **Aspiration of hypersaline water**: Even small amounts of Dead Sea water entering the lungs causes severe damage:
  - Osmotic gradient draws fluid from blood vessels into lung tissue
  - Causes non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema (fluid in the lungs)
  - Chemical pneumonitis (inflammation and damage to lung lining)
  - Can cause rapid respiratory failure even with less water than ordinary seawater drowning
- **Swallowing Dead Sea water**: Causes acute hypercalcemia and hypermagnesemia (dangerous elevation of calcium and magnesium in the blood), which can damage the heart and kidneys
- **How Dead Sea drowning occurs**: People do not sink in the traditional sense; they trip, fall face-down, and cannot right themselves due to the water's density. The buoyancy makes it difficult to get arms or legs down through the water to turn over
- **Time limit**: Experts recommend not staying in Dead Sea water for more than 20 minutes due to rapid dehydration and electrolyte disruption
- **First aid is the same** (CPR protocol), but hospital treatment is more urgent due to electrolyte toxicity

### Climate considerations

- **Heat and dehydration**: Israel's hot climate (especially Negev, Dead Sea, Jordan Valley) increases drowning risk through heat exhaustion leading to loss of consciousness in water
- **Summer bathing season**: Peak drowning risk during Israeli summer (June-September)

### Emergency infrastructure

- **MDA ambulances** carry advanced resuscitation equipment including intubation capability
- **AED locations**: Increasingly available at beaches, public pools, and large venues
- MDA average response time varies by location; beach access can be slower than urban areas

## Prevention guidelines (MDA)

- Swim only at **declared/official beaches** with lifeguard services
- Swim only during **lifeguard duty hours**
- Never enter the water when a **black or red flag** is flying
- Never swim after consuming **alcohol**
- Maintain **constant supervision** of children near water -- within arm's reach
- Do not use mobile phones while supervising children near water
- Learn to swim; teach children to swim from an early age
- Be aware of changing water conditions, especially afternoon currents on the Mediterranean
- In the Dead Sea: enter slowly, stay upright, avoid submerging the face, limit time to 20 minutes

## Key differences from standard cardiac arrest CPR

| Aspect | Standard cardiac arrest | Drowning |
|---|---|---|
| Primary cause | Cardiac arrhythmia | Hypoxia (oxygen deprivation) |
| CPR sequence | CAB (Compressions, Airway, Breathing) | ABC (Airway, Breathing, Compressions) preferred for trained rescuers |
| Rescue breaths | Optional for lay rescuers | Especially important; significantly improves outcomes |
| Defibrillation | Often primary intervention | Secondary to ventilation |
| Post-event monitoring | Cardiac monitoring | Electrolyte monitoring + pulmonary monitoring for delayed edema |
