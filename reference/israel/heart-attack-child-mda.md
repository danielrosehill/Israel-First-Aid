## Important Context -- Chest Pain and Heart Attack in Children

### Why this protocol exists

True heart attacks (myocardial infarction) are exceedingly rare in children. However, chest pain is a relatively common complaint in pediatric emergency rooms and is almost always benign (musculoskeletal, anxiety, gastroesophageal reflux). The rare cardiac causes that DO occur in children include:

- **Myocarditis** -- inflammation of the heart muscle, often following a viral illness
- **Congenital heart defects** -- undiagnosed structural abnormalities
- **Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy** -- a leading cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes
- **Commotio cordis** -- sudden cardiac arrest from blunt impact to the chest during sports (e.g., baseball, football, martial arts)
- **Kawasaki disease** -- can cause coronary artery aneurysms in young children
- **Long QT syndrome and other arrhythmias** -- inherited electrical conduction disorders

### Key difference from adults

- Children almost never have atherosclerotic coronary artery disease
- Aspirin is NOT given to children for suspected heart attack (risk of Reye's syndrome; cardiac cause is almost never thrombotic MI)
- Pediatric cardiac arrest is more commonly caused by respiratory failure than by primary cardiac events
- Presentation differs: children may present with fatigue, poor feeding, vomiting, abdominal pain, or syncope rather than classic "chest pain radiating to the arm"

## GAPS IN AVAILABLE SOURCES

**MDA does not publish a specific public-facing protocol for "heart attack in children."** This is consistent with the medical reality that myocardial infarction in children is extremely rare. MDA's publicly available first aid materials focus on:

1. **Adult heart attack** -- recognition of chest pain, calling 101, aspirin administration (for adults), positioning
2. **Pediatric CPR/BLS** -- for cardiac arrest in children from any cause
3. **General pediatric emergencies** -- including recognition of serious illness

The protocol below is synthesised from:
- MDA's general heart attack first aid guidance (adapted with pediatric exclusions)
- MDA's pediatric BLS guidelines
- MDA training curriculum topics (cardiac emergencies are covered in their courses)
- International consensus guidelines that MDA aligns with (ILCOR/AHA 2020-2025)

Where specific MDA child-cardiac guidance could not be confirmed from publicly available documents, this is clearly noted.

## Recognition -- Warning Signs in Children

### Call 101 immediately if a child experiences:

- **Chest pain** that is persistent, severe, or worsening (especially during or after physical activity)
- **Chest pain with exertion** that limits activity or causes the child to stop playing
- **Syncope (fainting)** or near-fainting during physical activity
- **Palpitations** -- child reports heart "racing," "skipping," or "pounding"
- **Unexplained shortness of breath** disproportionate to activity level
- **Sudden collapse** during sports or physical activity
- **Cyanosis** (bluish discolouration of lips, fingertips, or skin)
- **Chest pain accompanied by fever** (may indicate myocarditis or pericarditis)
- **History of known heart condition** with any new symptoms

### Red flags that suggest cardiac (rather than benign) cause:

- Pain occurs during exertion, not at rest
- Associated with syncope, near-syncope, or dizziness
- Child has a known congenital heart condition
- Family history of sudden cardiac death at a young age
- Recent viral illness followed by new chest pain, fatigue, or breathlessness (possible myocarditis)
- Direct blow to the chest immediately before collapse (commotio cordis)

## First Aid Steps

### Step 1: Assess and Call 101

1. Stay calm and reassure the child
2. **Call 101 (MDA)** immediately if the child has any of the warning signs listed above
3. Describe the child's symptoms, age, and any known medical conditions to the dispatcher
4. Follow all instructions from the MDA dispatcher

### Step 2: Position the Child

- If the child is **conscious and breathing**: help them into a comfortable position -- typically **sitting up** or in a **semi-reclined position** (this eases breathing)
- If the child is **unconscious but breathing**: place in the **recovery position** (on their side)
- If the child is **not breathing or has no pulse**: begin CPR immediately (see Step 4)

### Step 3: Keep the Child Calm and Monitor

- Do NOT give the child any medications unless specifically instructed by the 101 dispatcher or a physician
- **Do NOT give aspirin to children** -- unlike adults, aspirin is not indicated for pediatric chest pain and carries risk of Reye's syndrome
- Loosen tight clothing around the chest and neck
- Keep the child warm but not overheated
- Do NOT allow the child to eat or drink (in case hospital procedures are needed)
- Continuously monitor breathing and level of consciousness
- Note the time symptoms began -- relay this to the ambulance crew

### Step 4: CPR if Needed (Pediatric BLS per MDA/ILCOR)

If the child becomes unresponsive and is not breathing normally:

**For a single rescuer:**
- Compression-to-ventilation ratio: **30:2**
- Compression rate: **100-120 per minute**
- Compression depth: approximately **5 cm** (one-third of chest depth) for children aged 1-puberty
- After 5 cycles (approximately 2 minutes), call 101 if not already done

**For two rescuers:**
- Compression-to-ventilation ratio: **15:2**
- Compression rate: **100-120 per minute**

**AED use:**
- Use an AED as soon as one is available
- Use pediatric pads/dose attenuator if available for children under 8 years or under 25 kg
- If only adult pads are available, use them -- do not delay defibrillation
- MDA has deployed defibrillators across Israel; their locations are available via the MDA app and website

### Step 5: Commotio Cordis -- Special Scenario

If a child collapses immediately after a **direct blow to the chest** during sports:

1. This is likely **commotio cordis** -- a time-critical emergency
2. Check responsiveness immediately
3. If unresponsive and not breathing: **call 101 and begin CPR immediately**
4. **Use an AED as fast as possible** -- defibrillation within 3 minutes gives the best chance of survival
5. Do not delay CPR while waiting for the AED

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT give aspirin** to a child (unlike adult heart attack protocol)
- **Do NOT give nitroglycerin** or any cardiac medication unless prescribed and directed by a physician
- **Do NOT dismiss chest pain** in a child who has recently been ill, has a known heart condition, or collapsed during activity
- **Do NOT attempt to drive the child to hospital yourself** if cardiac arrest is suspected -- call 101 for an ambulance equipped with monitoring and defibrillation
- **Do NOT leave the child unattended** while waiting for the ambulance

---

## When to Seek Urgent Medical Evaluation (Non-Emergency)

Not all pediatric chest pain requires an ambulance. However, a child should be evaluated by a physician if:

- Chest pain recurs over several days
- Pain is associated with exercise
- There is a family history of cardiac conditions or sudden death
- The child has a known heart condition
- Chest pain follows a recent febrile illness
