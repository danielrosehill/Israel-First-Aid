## Definition

Anaphylaxis in children is a severe, rapid, multi-system allergic reaction that is life-threatening. It can develop within seconds to hours of allergen exposure. The most common triggers in children differ from adults (see below). Without immediate treatment it can be fatal -- over 50% of deaths from allergic reactions occur within the first 60 minutes of onset.

## Common Triggers in Israeli Children

Based on Israeli epidemiological data, the most common food allergens in young Israeli children are:

| Allergen | Estimated prevalence |
|----------|---------------------|
| Cow's milk | 1.0% |
| Sesame | 0.93% |
| Eggs | 0.88% |
| Tree nuts | 0.57% |
| Peanuts | 0.2% |
| Fish | 0.2% |

**Israel-specific notes:**
- Sesame allergy is notably more prevalent in Israel than in most Western countries, and is second only to cow's milk as a cause of anaphylaxis in Israeli children
- Peanut allergy is significantly lower in Israel (0.17%) compared to the UK (1.85%), attributed to early introduction of peanut-containing foods (Bamba) in the Israeli diet
- Food allergies are the most frequent cause of anaphylaxis in children, followed by medications and insect stings

**Other triggers:**
- Medications (antibiotics, NSAIDs)
- Insect stings (bee, wasp)
- Vaccines (rare: approximately 1 case per million doses)
- Latex
- Exercise-induced anaphylaxis
- Idiopathic (unknown cause)

## Signs and Symptoms in Children

### Skin
- Rash, hives, itching, flushing
- Swelling of lips, tongue, mouth, face, eyes, and throat
- Facial pallor, excessive sweating

### Respiratory
- Coughing, wheezing, stridor (whistling sound)
- Shortness of breath, breathing difficulty
- Throat tightness, burning sensation, or swelling
- Hoarse voice or change in cry (in infants/toddlers)

### Cardiovascular
- Weak or rapid pulse
- Pallor or cyanosis (bluish discolouration)
- Low blood pressure
- Confusion, lethargy, or loss of consciousness

### Gastrointestinal
- Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea
- Abdominal cramping and pain

### Behavioural (child-specific indicators)
- Sudden irritability, inconsolable crying (infants)
- Refusal to eat or drink
- Clinging behaviour, appearing frightened
- Pulling at ears or scratching (young children who cannot articulate itching)

## Diagnostic Criteria (Sampson Classification)

Same criteria as for adults. High probability of anaphylaxis when any ONE of the following is met:

1. **Acute onset** (minutes to hours) with skin/mucosal involvement PLUS respiratory compromise OR hypotension/end-organ dysfunction
2. **Two or more** of the following occurring rapidly after exposure to a likely allergen: skin/mucosal symptoms, respiratory compromise, hypotension, persistent GI symptoms
3. **Sudden hypotension** after exposure to a known allergen for that patient

## First Aid Protocol -- Children (Lay Responder / BLS Level)

### Step 1 -- Scene Safety and Recognition
- Recognise signs of a severe allergic reaction (see symptoms above)
- In young children and infants, watch for behavioural cues (sudden distress, inconsolable crying, drooling, difficulty swallowing)
- Any involvement of breathing, circulation, or consciousness indicates anaphylaxis -- do not wait

### Step 2 -- Call MDA (101)
- Call **101** immediately
- Keep the phone on speaker and follow the dispatcher's instructions
- State the child's age and approximate weight if known
- Mention if the child has a known allergy and whether an EpiPen is available

### Step 3 -- Airway Management
- If facial or tongue swelling is present, assume airway compromise
- Ensure the tongue is not obstructing the airway
- Remove any food debris from the mouth
- Loosen tight clothing around the neck and chest
- In infants: tilt head slightly back (neutral position) -- do not over-extend the neck

### Step 4 -- Patient Positioning
- **Conscious child with breathing difficulty**: Allow to sit upright or in a position of comfort (do not force to lie down)
- **Conscious child without breathing difficulty**: Lie flat on back with legs elevated
- **Unconscious and breathing**: Recovery position (on the side)
- **Unconscious and not breathing**: Begin paediatric CPR

### Step 5 -- Administer Epinephrine Auto-Injector (EpiPen)

Epinephrine is the primary and most critical treatment. **Do not delay administration.** Do not substitute antihistamines (e.g., Benadryl) for epinephrine in anaphylaxis.

#### Paediatric Dosing (Auto-Injector)

| Child's weight | Auto-injector dose | Device |
|----------------|-------------------|--------|
| Under 15 kg | 0.01 mg/kg (drawn from vial -- requires trained professional) | No auto-injector available for this weight range |
| 15-30 kg | **0.15 mg** | EpiPen Junior (green label) / FASTJEKT Junior |
| Over 30 kg | **0.3 mg** | EpiPen (yellow label) / FASTJEKT |

**Important clinical note from Israeli sources:** Many Israeli allergists prescribe the 0.3 mg (adult) dose from around 20 kg body weight, based on clinical judgement. The strict 30 kg threshold on the product label is a general guideline; follow the prescribing allergist's recommendation for the individual child.

**For children under 15 kg (approximately under 3 years):** No paediatric auto-injector is currently marketed for this weight range. The 0.15 mg EpiPen Junior delivers a higher per-kilogram dose than ideal but is still recommended over withholding treatment. The MDA ALS protocol specifies 0.01 mg/kg drawn from a 1:1000 vial for this weight range (professional-level intervention).

#### Administration Technique -- Children

1. Remove the EpiPen from its plastic case
2. Remove the **blue safety cap** (blue to the sky)
3. Hold with the **orange tip pointing downward** (orange to the thigh)
4. Position at **90 degrees** to the middle of the **outer thigh**
5. **For small or thin children**: Pinch the thigh muscle to create a firm surface and to avoid hitting the bone
6. Press firmly until a **click** is heard
7. Hold in place for **10 seconds** (some sources say 3 seconds -- follow the device instructions printed on the specific auto-injector)
8. Can be administered **through clothing** -- do not waste time removing trousers
9. After removal, massage the injection site for 10 seconds
10. Dispose of the used auto-injector safely (hand to paramedics or place in sharps container)

**Critical warning:** Do not touch the orange tip. Do not inject into fingers, hands, feet, or buttocks. Thigh only.

### Step 6 -- Reassess
- Check the child's condition after **5 minutes**
- If no improvement and a second auto-injector is available, a **second dose** may be administered
- Epinephrine effects last approximately 20 minutes; symptoms may recur (biphasic reaction)
- Biphasic reactions can occur up to 72 hours after initial reaction

### Step 7 -- Monitor Until EMS Arrives
- Continuously monitor airway, breathing, and circulation
- Keep the child calm and reassured
- Be prepared to begin CPR if the child loses consciousness and stops breathing
- **All suspected anaphylaxis in children requires hospital evaluation**, even if symptoms fully resolve after epinephrine
- Hospital monitoring is typically 4-6 hours minimum for children

## ALS-Level Protocol (Professional Responders -- from MDA ALS Manual, April 2024)

For completeness, the MDA ALS weight-based dosing for paediatric patients:

| Weight | Epinephrine dose (IM) |
|--------|----------------------|
| Up to 10 kg | 0.01 mg/kg |
| 10-25 kg | 0.15 mg |
| 25-50 kg | 0.3 mg |
| Over 50 kg | 0.5 mg |

- Repeat dosing: Up to **3 doses** at **10-minute intervals**
- IV access: Crystalloid fluids (Ringer's lactate or normal saline) -- dose 20 mL/kg bolus for paediatric hypotension
- Oxygen: Administer to any child with respiratory distress
- Antihistamines: May be given for symptom relief but are NOT a substitute for epinephrine
- Nebulised salbutamol for bronchospasm
- Corticosteroids: Per protocol, though emerging evidence questions efficacy for preventing biphasic reactions

## School and Gan (Kindergarten) Context in Israel

### Legal Requirements
- Israeli educational institutions are **legally required** to stock epinephrine auto-injectors (per Ministry of Education and Ministry of Health directives)
- The Israel Ministry of Education directive (HealthEpinephrineinSchools.pdf) mandates availability in schools and kindergartens
- Staff must be trained in recognising anaphylaxis and administering EpiPen

### Practical Guidance for Schools/Ganim
- Children with known allergies must have a personal emergency action plan on file
- Parents must provide the school/gan with:
  - A letter from the treating allergist specifying the allergy and prescribed EpiPen dose
  - At least one auto-injector, stored at the institution in a labelled, accessible location (not locked away, not refrigerated)
  - An emergency contact list
- Staff should know the child's specific allergens and be trained to administer the EpiPen
- Children over age 12 should be trained in self-administration
- Younger children (gan age, 3-6) are entirely dependent on staff -- rapid recognition is critical

### Storage
- Store at room temperature (15-25 degrees C)
- Do not refrigerate
- Do not leave in a car or in direct sun -- Israeli climate makes this particularly important given extreme summer temperatures (40+ degrees C)
- Check expiry dates regularly -- shelf life is approximately 18-20 months
- Replace when the viewing window shows discolouration or particles

## Prevention and Preparedness

- Complete avoidance of known allergens
- **Always carry two auto-injectors** (in case a second dose is needed or the first misfires)
- Inform all caregivers, teachers, and family members of the allergy
- Medical alert bracelet recommended for children with known anaphylaxis risk
- Regular follow-up with a paediatric allergist
- Practice using the training device (included in the EpiPen pack) so caregivers are confident in an emergency

## Israeli Context -- Additional Considerations

- **Climate**: Israeli heat (particularly in Negev, Arava, Jordan Valley) can degrade EpiPen medication. Never leave in a vehicle or bag in direct sunlight. Carry in an insulated pouch if outdoors.
- **Insect stings**: Bee and wasp stings are common triggers, particularly in agricultural areas and during outdoor activities (tiyulim/hikes)
- **Sesame prevalence**: Tahini and halva are ubiquitous in Israeli cuisine. Parents of sesame-allergic children must be vigilant about cross-contamination in hummus, bakeries, and school meals.
- **Military service (future consideration)**: Adolescents with severe allergies should document their condition for Tzahal (IDF) medical screening
