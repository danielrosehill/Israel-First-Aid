## Israeli Context

Smoke and tear gas inhalation scenarios in Israel include:
- **Wildfires**: Israel experiences significant wildfire seasons; MDA regularly responds to mass-casualty wildfire events (e.g., Yom HaZikaron 2023 central Israel wildfires)
- **Residential fires**: Standard urban fire emergencies
- **Tear gas exposure**: Encountered in protest situations, border areas, and military operations
- **Rocket attacks**: Secondary fires from rocket impacts can produce smoke inhalation casualties
- **Building fires in wartime**: Incendiary devices, secondary fires from missile strikes

During wildfire emergencies, MDA deploys ambulances, Mobile Intensive Care Units (MICUs), Rapid Response Vehicles, and the Medicycle unit. They coordinate with the Israel Fire and Rescue Service (dial 102), IDF, and police.

## First Aid Protocol -- Smoke Inhalation

### 1. Scene Safety
- **Do NOT re-enter** a smoke-filled area to rescue someone unless you are trained and equipped (firefighter with breathing apparatus)
- Ensure the area is safe before approaching
- If indoors, stay low where air is cleaner (smoke rises)

### 2. Move to Fresh Air
- Remove the person from the smoke-filled environment immediately
- Move them to an area with clean, fresh air
- If you cannot safely move the person, wait for Fire and Rescue (102) or MDA (101)

### 3. Call MDA -- 101
- Every second counts in smoke inhalation
- Report: location, number of casualties, whether the fire is still active
- Also call Fire and Rescue at 102 if fire is ongoing

### 4. Position the Patient
- Sit the person in an **upright position** -- this helps with easier breathing
- If unconscious but breathing, place in the recovery position (on their side)
- If unconscious and not breathing, lay flat for CPR

### 5. Monitor Breathing
- Watch for signs of respiratory distress: coughing, wheezing, difficulty breathing, hoarse voice
- Look for soot around mouth/nose, singed facial hair, or burns to the face/mouth
- **Warning**: Symptoms may worsen over hours as airway swelling develops

### 6. CPR if Needed
- If the person stops breathing, begin CPR immediately (if trained)
- Continue until the person starts breathing or MDA arrives
- Use standard 30:2 compression-to-ventilation ratio

### 7. Burns Assessment
- Check for burns, especially to the face, neck, and hands
- Cool burns with clean water
- Do not remove clothing stuck to burns
- Cover with clean, non-adherent dressing

### 8. Keep Warm
- Smoke inhalation patients may become hypothermic
- Cover with a blanket while waiting for MDA

## First Aid Protocol -- Tear Gas Exposure

### Israeli Context
Tear gas (CS gas, CN gas) may be encountered in protest situations, border areas, and during security operations. The following is standard first aid guidance consistent with emergency medicine protocols.

### 1. Remove from Exposure
- Move the person away from the tear gas source to fresh air
- Move upwind if possible
- Effects are normally self-limiting once exposure ends

### 2. Eye Care
- **Do NOT rub the eyes** -- this worsens the irritation
- Flush eyes with large amounts of clean, tepid water for at least 15 minutes
- Water or saline are the safest options for eye washing
- Avoid using other substances (milk, etc.) due to infection risk

### 3. Skin Decontamination
- Remove contaminated clothing
- Wash affected skin with soap and water
- Blow the nose to clear chemical residue from nasal passages

### 4. Monitor for Deterioration
- Symptoms should begin to subside once exposure is eliminated
- If symptoms do not improve or continue to worsen after removal from exposure, call MDA at 101
- Seek medical attention for: persistent breathing difficulty, chest pain, severe eye pain, vomiting

### 5. When to Call 101
- Pre-existing respiratory conditions (asthma, COPD)
- Prolonged or heavy exposure
- Symptoms not resolving after 30 minutes in fresh air
- Children, elderly, or pregnant women exposed
- Any difficulty breathing

## MDA Professional-Level Care

At the professional level, MDA teams may provide:
- 100% high-flow oxygen (critical for carbon monoxide poisoning)
- Continuous monitoring of oxygen saturation
- Airway management including intubation if needed
- IV access and fluid management
- Transport to hospital (burn units for severe cases)

### Hospital Referral Indicators
- Soot in mouth/nose or singed facial/nasal hair
- Burns to face, neck, or airway
- Hoarse voice or stridor (noisy breathing)
- History of being in an enclosed space with smoke
- Altered consciousness
- Carbon monoxide poisoning suspected

## MDA Wildfire Response -- Documented Example

During the Yom HaZikaron wildfires in central Israel, MDA:
- Deployed ambulances, MICUs, and Rapid Response Vehicles
- Reinforced the Medicycle unit for patients stuck in vehicles
- Treated pregnant women and babies for smoke inhalation
- Treated burn patients
- Deployed the Wheels of Hope Mobile Intensive Care Bus and 20+ ambulances to evacuate a nursing home
- Conducted evacuations per Ministry of Health guidelines while maintaining continuity of medical care
