## Paediatric-Specific Anatomical Considerations

Children are at higher risk for head and spinal injury due to:
- **Proportionally larger head**: A child's head is larger and heavier relative to their body, raising the centre of gravity and increasing fall risk
- **Softer skull bones**: Particularly in infants under 1 year; skull fractures occur more easily from lower-energy impacts
- **More flexible spine**: The paediatric cervical spine is more mobile and ligamentously lax, making it more vulnerable to injury
- **Incomplete ossification**: Vertebrae are not fully formed, and growth plates are vulnerable
- **Higher cervical injury pattern**: Children under 8 tend to sustain upper cervical (C1-C3) injuries, whereas adults more commonly injure the lower cervical spine

## Common Causes in Children

- **Falls from playground equipment** (climbing frames, slides, swings)
- **Falls from bicycles, scooters, and electric kick-scooters**
- **Falls from changing tables** (infants -- a particularly preventable cause)
- **Falls from height exceeding 1 metre** (windows, balconies, stairs)
- **Motor vehicle accidents** (as passengers or pedestrians)
- **Falls during sports and physical activity**
- **Being struck by a moving heavy object**
- **Blast/shrapnel injuries** (Israel-specific security context)

## Recognition of Head Injury in Children

### Concussion Signs in Children

Children, especially younger ones, often cannot articulate their symptoms. Look for behavioural indicators:

- **Irritability and inconsolable crying** (key sign in infants/toddlers)
- **Won't stop crying** or unusually fussy behaviour
- **Vomiting** (single episode may be normal from crying; repeated vomiting is a red flag)
- **Altered behaviour**: confusion, disorientation, "not acting like themselves"
- **Excessive drowsiness** or difficulty waking
- **Refusal to eat or nurse** (infants)
- **Pallor** (pale face)
- **Dizziness** or unsteady walking (older children)
- **Headache** (if child can report it)
- **Brief loss of consciousness** (even for seconds)

### Red Flag Warning Signs -- Call 101 Immediately

The following signs require immediate emergency evaluation. **Call 101 (MDA)**:

- **Loss of consciousness** (even briefly)
- **Seizures or convulsions** after the injury
- **Repeated vomiting** (more than once)
- **Altered level of consciousness**: excessive drowsiness, confusion, difficulty waking
- **Unequal pupils** (one pupil larger than the other)
- **Clear fluid or blood draining from nose or ears** (possible skull base fracture)
- **Sudden unsteady walking or standing** (ataxia)
- **Weakness or inability to move a limb** (possible spinal cord involvement)
- **Severe or worsening headache**
- **Sudden vision changes**
- **Behavioural changes**: unusual aggression, lethargy, or disorientation
- **Bulging fontanelle** (in infants -- the soft spot on top of the head)
- **Large or rapidly expanding scalp swelling** (haematoma)

### High-Risk Injury Mechanisms -- Always Call 101

Regardless of initial symptoms, **always call 101** when the mechanism of injury includes:
- Fall from height exceeding 1 metre (or more than twice the child's height)
- Motor vehicle accident (child as passenger, pedestrian, or cyclist)
- High-speed bicycle, scooter, or electric vehicle accident
- Fall from a window, balcony, or stairs
- Struck by a heavy or fast-moving object
- Any penetrating head wound
- Blast or explosion exposure
- Any fall onto a hard surface with direct head impact in an infant under 1 year

## Recognition of Spinal Injury in Children

### When to Suspect Spinal Injury

- Child complains of **neck or back pain** after trauma
- **Tingling, numbness, or weakness** in arms or legs
- **Inability to move** arms or legs (paralysis)
- **Loss of bladder or bowel control**
- **Neck or back is in an abnormal position** (deformity)
- **Tenderness** when the spine is touched
- Any of the **high-risk mechanisms** listed above
- **Altered consciousness** following trauma (cannot reliably report spinal symptoms)

### Critical Rule

**If head injury is suspected, ALWAYS suspect spinal injury as well.** The two commonly co-occur, especially in children.

## First Aid Protocol -- Head Injury in Children

### Step 1: Ensure Scene Safety
- Ensure the area is safe for you and the child
- Wear gloves if available

### Step 2: Assess Responsiveness
- Gently tap the child and call their name
- Do NOT shake the child
- **Do NOT move the child** unless they are in immediate danger (fire, traffic, structural collapse)

### Step 3: Call 101 (MDA)
- **Always call 101 for any suspected head or spinal injury in a child**
- Even if the child appears alert, call 101 for any high-risk mechanism
- Provide: location, child's age, mechanism of injury, current symptoms, level of consciousness

### Step 4: Keep the Child Still
- **DO NOT MOVE the child** -- this is the single most important instruction
- If the child is conscious, calmly instruct them to stay still
- Reassure the child -- speak calmly and maintain eye contact
- If the child is lying down, do not sit them up
- If the child is sitting, do not lay them down (unless they become unconscious and need airway management)

### Step 5: Stabilise the Head and Neck
- If trained: place your hands on either side of the child's head and hold it steady, aligned with the spine
- Do NOT twist, bend, or rotate the head or neck
- Maintain this position until MDA arrives

### Step 6: Control Visible Bleeding
- If the scalp is bleeding, apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth or sterile bandage
- **Do NOT apply direct pressure** if you suspect a skull fracture (depressed or soft area of skull)
- **Scalp bleeding in children can cause significant blood loss and shock** due to smaller blood volume

### Step 7: Monitor and Record
- Monitor breathing and level of consciousness continuously
- Note the time of injury and any changes in symptoms
- If the child vomits, carefully log-roll them onto their side while supporting head and neck alignment (requires training)
- If the child loses consciousness and stops breathing, begin CPR per MDA protocol (maintaining spinal alignment as much as possible)

### Step 8: Prevent Hypothermia
- Cover the child with a blanket or coat to maintain warmth
- Children lose body heat faster than adults

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT move the child** unless in immediate danger
- **Do NOT remove a helmet** if one is being worn (leave this for MDA paramedics; removal requires two trained people)
- **Do NOT allow the child to stand up or walk** even if they want to -- standing or walking does NOT rule out spinal injury
- **Do NOT give food or drink** (the child may need surgery or imaging)
- **Do NOT apply pressure to depressed skull fractures** or push back any protruding matter
- **Do NOT insert anything into a wound**
- **Do NOT attempt to "straighten" the neck** if it is in an abnormal position
- **Do NOT leave the child alone** -- continuous monitoring is essential

## After Emergency Evaluation -- Home Monitoring (24-48 Hours)

If MDA paramedics or hospital staff evaluate the child and determine discharge is appropriate:

### Monitor for the following warning signs and return to emergency department immediately if any appear:
- Prolonged unusual sleepiness or difficulty waking
- Repeated vomiting
- Behavioural changes, confusion, or irritability
- Seizures or convulsions
- Sudden unsteady movement or walking
- Nosebleed or fluid draining from ears
- Weakness in any limb
- Sudden severe headaches (children age 5+)
- Worsening of any symptom

### General care:
- Allow the child to rest in a calm, quiet environment
- Reduce screen time and stimulation
- Follow up with paediatrician or family doctor within 48 hours
- A cool compress (not ice directly on skin) may be applied to the bump/swelling area

## Israel-Specific Context

### Climate Considerations
- In hot weather, children playing outdoors are at increased risk of falls due to fatigue and dehydration
- Heat can exacerbate symptoms and increase risk of shock in injured children
- Ensure shade and hydration while waiting for MDA

### Security Context
- Blast and shrapnel injuries from rockets or explosions can cause combined head and spinal trauma
- In such events, follow PHTLS systematic assessment: Safety, then hemorrhage control, then ABCDE
- Multiple casualties may require triage -- children are priority patients

### Prevention
- **Helmet use is critical** for bicycles, scooters, electric kick-scooters, and skateboards
- Israeli Ministry of Health recommends helmets for all wheeled activities
- Playground surfaces should be impact-absorbing (rubber, sand)
- Window guards (soragim) are required by Israeli law in residential buildings with children
- Secure changing tables; never leave an infant unattended on an elevated surface

## Glasgow Coma Scale -- Paediatric Modification

For MDA first responders (BLS level, AVPU scale is used; ALS level uses full GCS):

| Response | AVPU Scale |
|----------|-----------|
| Alert | A -- child is awake, responsive, oriented |
| Verbal | V -- responds to voice/verbal stimuli |
| Pain | P -- responds only to painful stimuli |
| Unresponsive | U -- no response |

**Any trauma patient exhibiting hostile, combative, or uncooperative behaviour should be suspected of suffering from hypoxia or head injury until proven otherwise** (per United Hatzalah / PHTLS protocol, consistent with MDA training).

## Key Differences: Children vs Adults

| Factor | Children | Adults |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Head size | Proportionally larger; higher centre of gravity | Proportionally smaller |
| Skull | Softer, more compliant; fractures more easily in infants | Rigid |
| Fontanelle | Open in infants (can bulge with raised intracranial pressure) | Closed |
| Cervical spine | More flexible; upper cervical injuries predominate | Less flexible; lower cervical injuries predominate |
| Communication | May not articulate symptoms; watch for behavioural changes | Can usually describe symptoms |
| Blood volume | Smaller; scalp bleeding can cause shock more quickly | Larger blood volume reserve |
| Concussion signs | Irritability, inconsolable crying, vomiting, refusal to eat | Headache, confusion, amnesia |
