## Important Context

MDA protocols are aligned with international ILCOR (International Liaison Committee on Resuscitation) consensus guidelines. The key difference in paediatric CPR compared to adult CPR is the underlying cause: in children, cardiac arrest is almost always secondary to respiratory failure or hypoxia, NOT a primary cardiac event. This makes rescue breaths critically important in paediatric CPR.

MDA training materials note: "In children, as a result of illness or injury, there is continuous deterioration in respiratory or cardiac function that eventually leads to respiratory arrest and cardiac arrest."

## Lay Rescuer (Bystander) Protocol

### Step 1: Scene Safety and Assessment

1. Ensure the scene is safe.
2. Check responsiveness:
   - **Infants**: Tap the sole of the foot.
   - **Children**: Tap and shout; squeeze the trapezius gently.
3. Check for breathing: Look, listen, feel for no more than **10 seconds**.
4. If unresponsive and not breathing (or only gasping):

### Step 2: Call for Help

- If **alone**: Perform **2 minutes of CPR first**, THEN call 101 (MDA).
- If **someone else is present**: Send them to call 101 immediately while you begin CPR.

**Rationale**: In children, the arrest is usually respiratory in origin. Two minutes of CPR before calling provides critical early oxygenation.

### Step 3: Chest Compressions (C - Circulation)

**Location**: Centre of the chest, lower third of the sternum, on a line between the nipples.

#### Infant (under 1 year)

- **Single rescuer**: Place **2 straight fingers** (at 90 degrees) on the centre of the chest, on the imaginary line between the nipples.
- **Two rescuers**: Encircle the torso with both hands; compress with **both thumbs** while fingers support the back.
- **Depth**: Approximately **4 cm** (about one-third of chest depth).
- **Rate**: 100-120 compressions per minute.

#### Child (1 year to puberty)

- **Technique**: Heel of **one hand** OR **two hands**, depending on the child's size.
- **Depth**: Approximately **5 cm** (about one-third of chest depth).
- **Rate**: 100-120 compressions per minute.

**Key points**:
- Push hard and fast.
- Allow full chest recoil between compressions.
- Minimise interruptions.

### Step 4: Airway (A)

- **Infant**: Keep head in **neutral position** (sniffing position). Do NOT hyperextend the neck. Tilt head back only gently and lift the chin.
- **Child**: **Head-tilt chin-lift** with mild extension.
- Do NOT insert fingers into the infant's or child's mouth (risk of pushing objects deeper).

### Step 5: Rescue Breaths (B - Breathing)

#### Infant

- **Seal your mouth over the infant's mouth AND nose** (because the face is small enough to cover both).
- Deliver gentle breaths from your cheeks (NOT deep lung breaths) -- just enough to see the chest rise.
- Each breath over approximately **1 second**.

#### Child

- **Pinch the nose** and seal your mouth over the child's mouth.
- Deliver breaths until chest rise is visible.
- Each breath over approximately **1 second**.

If chest does not rise: reposition the head/airway and try again.

### Step 6: Compression-to-Breath Ratios

| Scenario | Ratio |
|----------|-------|
| Single rescuer (infant or child) | **30:2** (30 compressions, 2 breaths) |
| Two rescuers (infant or child) | **15:2** (15 compressions, 2 breaths) |

Continue cycles without interruption until:
- Professional help arrives (MDA ambulance).
- The child starts breathing and responding (place in recovery position on their side).
- An AED becomes available.
- You are physically unable to continue.

## AED (Defibrillator) Use

- Attach and use an AED as soon as one is available.
- Use **paediatric pads or a paediatric dose attenuator** if available.
- If only adult pads are available, use them -- but ensure the pads do not touch each other.
- **Pad placement for small children/infants**: If the chest is too small for standard anterolateral placement, use **anteroposterior placement** (one pad on the front of the chest, one on the back).
- Follow AED voice prompts.
- Resume CPR immediately after any shock is delivered.

## Professional (EMS) Protocol Additions

The following details come from MDA professional BLS/ALS training materials and are included for completeness. These go beyond lay rescuer scope.

### Neonatal Resuscitation (Newborns)

- **Ratio**: **3:1** (3 compressions to 1 breath), targeting approximately 120 total events per minute (90 compressions + 30 breaths).
- **Sequence**: A-B-C (Airway-Breathing-Compressions) -- NOT C-A-B. Neonates are a special case.
- This is distinct from infant CPR and applies to newborns in the delivery room or immediate postnatal period.

### Compression Initiation Criteria

Begin compressions if:
- No pulse detected, OR
- Pulse is **below 60 bpm with signs of poor perfusion** despite adequate ventilation and oxygenation.

### Ventilation with Advanced Airway

- Once an advanced airway is in place: continuous compressions at 100-120/min; deliver 1 breath every **6-8 seconds** (8-10 breaths/min) independently of compressions.
- Infant breathing intervals (pulse present, no compressions needed): 1 breath every 3-5 seconds.

### Bag-Valve-Mask (BVM) Specifications

| Patient | BVM Volume | Mask Size |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Infant | 250-350 cc | Size 0 |
| Child | Paediatric BVM | Size 1-2 |
| Adult | Adult BVM | Size 3-4 |

- Paediatric BVM includes a **pressure-relief valve** to prevent excessive positive pressure.
- Mouth-to-mouth is prohibited for MDA professional personnel (use BVM).

### Oral Airway Device Sizing

| Patient | Sizes |
|---------|-------|
| Infant | 00-0 |
| Child | 1-2 |
| Adult | 3-4 |

### Post-Shock Protocol

After defibrillation: immediately resume 5 cycles of C-A-B before reassessing rhythm.

### Suction

Use lower suction pressure for children and infants compared to adults.

### Cessation of Resuscitation

Stop resuscitation only if:
- Danger to rescuers.
- Obvious signs of death (as determined by medical authority).
- Return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) confirmed.

## Key Differences: Child/Infant vs Adult CPR

| Parameter | Infant (<1 yr) | Child (1 yr - puberty) | Adult |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|-------|
| Most common cause of arrest | Respiratory failure | Respiratory failure | Cardiac (primary) |
| CPR sequence | C-A-B (lay) / A-B-C (neonate) | C-A-B | C-A-B |
| Compression depth | ~4 cm (1/3 chest) | ~5 cm (1/3 chest) | >=5 cm (>=2 inches) |
| Compression technique | 2 fingers (single) / 2 thumbs (dual) | 1 or 2 hands | 2 hands |
| Compression rate | 100-120/min | 100-120/min | 100-120/min |
| Ratio (single rescuer) | 30:2 | 30:2 | 30:2 |
| Ratio (two rescuers) | 15:2 | 15:2 | 30:2 |
| Rescue breaths | ESSENTIAL | ESSENTIAL | Recommended but compression-only acceptable |
| Breaths technique | Mouth over mouth AND nose | Mouth to mouth (pinch nose) | Mouth to mouth (pinch nose) |
| Lone rescuer: when to call | After 2 min CPR | After 2 min CPR | Immediately |
| AED pads | Anteroposterior if needed | Paediatric pads preferred | Standard anterolateral |

## MDA Training Note

MDA states: "If you have NOT taken a CPR course, it is recommended to perform chest compressions only, without rescue breaths." However, this guidance is primarily aimed at adult CPR. For children and infants, rescue breaths are significantly more important due to the respiratory origin of most paediatric arrests, and trained rescuers should always provide ventilations.

## Gaps and Limitations

1. **MDA website (mdais.org) blocked automated access** -- the CPR course page, BLS guide PDF, and ALS handbook PDF could not be directly fetched. Protocol details were reconstructed from search result snippets, Israeli health service publications, and an Israeli BLS training reference that explicitly cites MDA protocols.

2. **Publication dates are not current** -- the most recent MDA document found is the ALS Professional Handbook dated April 2024. The BLS Guide is dated January 2016. MDA may have updated lay rescuer protocols since then but the updated content could not be directly accessed.

3. **2025 AHA/ERC guideline changes** -- The 2025 AHA guidelines eliminated the two-finger technique for infant compressions in favour of two-thumb encircling hands or heel-of-one-hand for all rescuers. It is not confirmed whether MDA has adopted this change yet. The MDA training materials referenced still describe the two-finger technique for single-rescuer infant CPR.

4. **Neonatal vs infant distinction** -- The 3:1 ratio and A-B-C sequence apply specifically to neonatal resuscitation (delivery room / immediate postnatal). This is a professional protocol, not a lay rescuer scenario. Standard infant CPR (1 month to 1 year) uses 30:2 (single) or 15:2 (dual) with C-A-B sequence.

5. **ERC alignment** -- Israel likely follows ILCOR/ERC guidelines as a non-AHA country, but explicit confirmation of which framework MDA formally adopts could not be found in publicly available English-language sources.
