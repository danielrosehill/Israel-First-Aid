## Definition

Anaphylaxis is "a sharp, multi-system allergic reaction that is life-threatening." The condition was first documented in 1902. Current estimates suggest 0.05-2% of the population will experience an anaphylactic event, with incidence rising in recent years.

## Pathophysiology

The reaction is primarily mediated by IgE involving mast cells and basophils. The immune response triggers rapid, multi-system involvement affecting skin, respiratory, cardiovascular, and gastrointestinal systems.

## Critical Warning

Over 50% of deaths from allergic reactions occur within the first 60 minutes of onset. The difficulty in treatment lies in distinguishing between a mild allergic reaction and a severe anaphylactic attack.

## Signs and Symptoms

### Skin
- Localised or widespread rash
- Itching and flushing
- Facial pallor and excessive sweating
- Swelling around eyes, mouth, neck, and throat

### Respiratory / Cardiovascular
- Breathing difficulty, stridor, wheezing
- Hypoxia
- Low blood pressure and shock symptoms
- Loss of consciousness

### Gastrointestinal
- Abdominal cramping
- Vomiting and diarrhoea

## Diagnostic Criteria (Sampson Classification)

United Hatzalah uses the same Sampson criteria as MDA:

1. Acute onset with skin involvement PLUS respiratory difficulty OR low blood pressure
2. Two or more of: skin symptoms, breathing problems, low BP, persistent GI symptoms -- occurring rapidly after allergen exposure
3. Sudden hypotension after known allergen exposure

## First Aid Treatment Protocol

### Immediate Actions

1. **Remove the offending agent** when possible (e.g., remove a bee stinger, stop an infusion)
2. **Call for emergency help** (101 for MDA)
3. **Secure the airway** -- assume airway swelling exists if facial swelling is present

### Patient Positioning

- **Responsive and breathing**: Position upright if respiratory distress is primary; supine with legs elevated if hypotension is primary
- **Unconscious and breathing**: Recovery position
- **Unconscious and not breathing**: Begin CPR, administer oxygen if available

### EpiPen (Epinephrine Auto-Injector) Administration

Epinephrine is described as "the most rapid and effective treatment." It causes blood vessel constriction and airway dilation.

**Two formulations available:**
- Adult dose
- Paediatric dose (half the adult dose)

**Technique:**
1. Remove from plastic case
2. Remove the safety cap
3. Position at 90 degrees to the outer thigh
4. Apply firmly for **10 seconds**
5. Can be administered **through clothing**
6. Massage injection site for 10 seconds after removal
7. Dispose in sharps container

**For children:** Pinch the thigh slightly to avoid bone contact.

### Reassessment

- Reassess within **5 minutes**
- A second dose may be needed if no improvement

## ALS-Level Protocol (Professional Responders)

### Epinephrine Dosing by Weight (IM Administration)

| Weight | Dose |
|--------|------|
| Up to 10 kg | 0.01 mg/kg |
| 10-25 kg | 0.15 mg |
| 25-50 kg | 0.3 mg |
| Over 50 kg | 0.5 mg |

### Supportive Care (ALS)
- Maintain airway (including preventive intubation if needed)
- Position patient supine, elevate legs
- Administer oxygen as needed
- Crystalloid fluids (Ringer's lactate or normal saline) aggressively for hypotension
- Antihistamines for symptom relief (supplementary, not primary treatment)
- Corticosteroids per protocol (though emerging evidence questions efficacy for preventing biphasic reactions)

### Repeat Dosing
- Up to **3 doses** at **10-minute intervals** per protocol

## Adrenaline (Epinephrine) -- Mechanism of Action

From United Hatzalah's educational material on adrenaline:

- **Alpha-1 receptors**: Vasoconstriction in skin and GI tract, reducing peripheral bleeding and redirecting blood to vital organs
- **Alpha-2 receptors**: Sympathetic nervous system stimulation
- **Beta-1 receptors**: Increases heart rate, cardiac output, and electrical conduction
- **Beta-2 receptors**: Dilates bronchial airways, relaxes smooth muscle

In anaphylaxis specifically, adrenaline works through three mechanisms:
1. Vasoconstriction -- reduces swelling
2. Bronchial dilation -- improves breathing
3. Blood pressure elevation -- counteracts shock

## Comparison with MDA Protocol

United Hatzalah's protocol is **substantially aligned** with MDA's guidelines. Key observations:

- **Dosing**: Identical weight-based IM dosing schedule
- **Technique**: Same 10-second hold, through-clothing administration
- **Repeat dosing**: Both allow up to 3 doses at 10-minute intervals
- **Positioning**: Minor difference -- United Hatzalah explicitly notes upright positioning for respiratory-predominant anaphylaxis, while MDA general guidance defaults to supine with legs elevated. This is a clinical judgement call, not a true protocol conflict.
- **Diagnostic criteria**: Both use the Sampson classification

**No material conflicts identified between MDA and United Hatzalah protocols.**
