## Notes on Authority

United Hatzalah does **not** publish detailed, standalone AED operation protocols on their public-facing website. Their website focuses on organisational information, volunteer stories, and fundraising. AED training is delivered through their 180-hour EMT training course, which is not publicly documented in protocol form online.

United Hatzalah's medical protocols are developed in coordination with Israeli medical standards and are broadly consistent with MDA/AHA guidelines. The information below is compiled from:

1. United Hatzalah's public website pages (about, resources, blog)
2. EZ-CPR app documentation
3. News coverage of United Hatzalah volunteer AED use
4. General organisational information about volunteer equipment and training

**Gap note**: United Hatzalah's specific internal AED protocols could not be located on their public website. The information below reflects what is publicly available. For detailed protocol comparison with MDA, contact United Hatzalah's medical directorate.

## United Hatzalah Volunteer AED Equipment

### Standard Volunteer Equipment

Every United Hatzalah volunteer carries a **medic bag** that includes a defibrillator (AED) alongside other equipment:

- IV supplies
- Infant delivery kit
- Blood pressure cuff
- Stethoscope
- Glucometer
- Oximeter
- Haemorrhage and trauma control supplies
- Oxygen tank
- Neck brace
- **Defibrillator (AED)**

United Hatzalah states: "It is imperative that each of our volunteers carries this piece of lifesaving equipment."

### Volunteer Training

- Volunteers must complete a **180-hour EMT training course**
- An additional **100 supervised training calls** are required
- Rigorous testing must be passed before becoming a fully fledged first responder
- AED operation is a core component of the EMT curriculum

## AED Operation (Based on United Hatzalah Public Guidance)

United Hatzalah's publicly available guidance on AED use is consistent with MDA/AHA protocols. The organisation does not publish a distinct protocol that differs from the national standard.

### General Protocol (Adults)

1. **Recognise cardiac arrest** -- person is unresponsive and not breathing normally
2. **Call for help** -- dial 101 (MDA) or 1221 (United Hatzalah dispatch)
3. **Begin CPR** -- chest compressions at 100-120 per minute, depth of 5-6 cm
4. **Apply AED as soon as available** -- follow device voice prompts
5. **Continue CPR and AED cycles** until professional help arrives

### For Children

United Hatzalah follows the same paediatric AED guidelines as MDA (based on AHA):

- Use paediatric pads/mode for children under 8 years or under 25 kg
- Use anteroposterior pad placement for small children and infants
- If paediatric pads are unavailable, use adult pads -- do not delay defibrillation

## EZ-CPR App

United Hatzalah developed the **EZ-CPR** app, which provides:

- **Step-by-step CPR instructions** with visual and audio guidance
- **AED usage instructions** -- detailed guidance on how to use a defibrillator if one is located nearby
- **Emergency button** -- notifies emergency medical service providers of the user's location
- **Practice mode** -- users can practise CPR technique during non-emergency time
- **Direct connection** to United Hatzalah's medical command centre, which dispatches volunteer EMTs, paramedics, and doctors

The app is available free of charge on Google Play and the App Store, with no advertisements. It can be downloaded from www.ez-cpr.com.

### AED Location via the App

The EZ-CPR app can help locate nearby defibrillators and connect users to the command centre for guidance. When a cardiac arrest is reported, United Hatzalah's dispatch system alerts the nearest available volunteers (average response time: under 3 minutes, with a goal of under 90 seconds in major cities).

## United Hatzalah Response Model

### Rapid Response for Cardiac Arrest

United Hatzalah's operational model is particularly relevant to AED use:

- **8,000+ volunteers** across Israel
- **2,000+ emergency responses per day**
- Average response time: **under 3 minutes** (under 90 seconds in major cities)
- Volunteers are deployed on **ambucycles** (emergency motorcycles) and in personal vehicles
- Each volunteer carries an AED in their medic bag
- The organisation's dispatch system uses GPS to identify and alert the nearest available volunteer

This rapid response model means that in many Israeli cardiac arrest situations, a United Hatzalah volunteer with an AED may arrive before the MDA ambulance, providing critical early defibrillation.

## Comparison with MDA

| Aspect | MDA | United Hatzalah |
|--------|-----|-----------------|
| **Role** | National EMS, ambulance service | Volunteer rapid-response supplement |
| **Emergency number** | 101 | 1221 |
| **AED protocol basis** | AHA guidelines | AHA guidelines (same) |
| **AED infrastructure** | 1,000+ smart stands, national database | Volunteer-carried AEDs |
| **Public AED access** | Smart stands, Eifo-Defi app | EZ-CPR app, volunteer deployment |
| **Training** | EMT courses, public first aid courses, National AED Day | 180-hour EMT course, corporate training |
| **Published protocols** | Hebrew training materials, defibrillator info page | Not publicly published |
| **Response model** | Ambulance dispatch | Nearest volunteer on ambucycle |

### Key Difference

MDA focuses on **public access defibrillation** (smart stands accessible to bystanders) while United Hatzalah focuses on **volunteer-carried defibrillation** (getting a trained volunteer with an AED to the scene as fast as possible). Both approaches complement each other in the Israeli emergency response system.
