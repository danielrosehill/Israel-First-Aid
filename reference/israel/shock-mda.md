## Definition

Shock (הלם) is a life-threatening condition characterised by a decrease in blood supply to the tissues as a result of a decrease in blood pressure. The body cannot maintain adequate circulation to vital organs.

Three factors determine blood pressure and, when impaired, can cause shock:
1. **Blood volume** (normally approximately 5-6 litres in an adult)
2. **Blood vessel diameter** (vasoconstriction / vasodilation)
3. **Cardiac output** (the heart's pumping efficiency)

## Types of Shock

### 1. Hypovolemic (Hemorrhagic) Shock — הלם היפובולמי
- **Most common type in trauma**
- **Causes**: Massive blood loss (external or internal bleeding), severe dehydration, extensive burns, prolonged vomiting/diarrhoea
- **Signs**: Rapid pulse, pale/cold/clammy skin, cold perspiration, altered consciousness, declining blood pressure
- **Israeli context**: Particularly relevant in trauma/security incidents and severe dehydration from desert heat

### 2. Neurogenic Shock — הלם נוירוגני
- **Cause**: Spinal cord injury preventing proper blood vessel constriction below the injury site
- **Distinctive signs**: Normal or slow pulse (unlike other shock types), red and warm skin below injury site (from vasodilation), along with head/spinal injury indicators

### 3. Cardiogenic Shock — הלם קרדיוגני
- **Causes**: Heart contusion, myocardial infarction, arrhythmias, cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax
- **Signs**: Elevated jugular venous pressure, tracheal deviation, rapid or slow pulse, classic shock signs

### 4. Septic Shock — הלם ספטי
- **Cause**: Bacterial toxins triggering widespread vasodilation from infection
- **Signs**: Develops gradually; accompanied by high fever and infection history

### 5. Anaphylactic Shock — הלם אנפילקטי
- **Cause**: Severe allergic reaction releasing histamine, causing extreme vasodilation
- **Signs**: Redness and itching, respiratory wheeze, characteristic rash, facial/throat swelling, stridor from laryngeal oedema
- **Treatment**: Epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if available

## Recognition — Signs and Symptoms

General indicators of shock (across all types unless noted):

- Rapid, weak pulse
- Pale, cold, clammy (moist) skin
- Cold perspiration
- Rapid, shallow breathing
- Decreased consciousness / confusion / agitation
- Falling blood pressure
- Weakness, dizziness, or fainting
- Nausea or vomiting
- Thirst
- Enlarged (dilated) pupils

### Compensation Mechanism
The body initially compensates for shock through increased heart rate, increased respiratory rate, and peripheral vasoconstriction. This redirects blood to vital organs (heart, brain, lungs) away from extremities — which is why the skin becomes pale and cold.

## First Aid Treatment Protocol

### Step 1: Call for Help
- **Call 101 (MDA)** immediately
- Provide: location, number of casualties, nature of incident, patient's condition

### Step 2: Ensure Scene Safety
- Confirm the scene is safe for you and the patient before approaching

### Step 3: Primary Assessment (ABC)
- **A — Airway**: Ensure the airway is open and clear; tilt chin up slightly if needed
- **B — Breathing**: Check if the person is breathing effectively
- **C — Circulation**: Check for pulse; control any severe bleeding

### Step 4: Control Bleeding
If shock is caused by haemorrhage:
- Apply direct pressure to wounds
- Pack wounds if direct pressure is insufficient
- Apply tourniquet to limbs if bleeding cannot be controlled by other means
- *Note: Tourniquet use is widely taught in Israeli civilian first aid due to security context*

### Step 5: Position the Patient
- **If conscious**: Lay the patient on their back and gently elevate legs approximately 30 cm (12 inches) above the level of the heart to improve blood flow to vital organs
  - **Do NOT elevate legs if**:
    - Head, neck, or spinal injury is suspected
    - Broken hip or leg bones are suspected
    - It causes pain or further injury
    - The patient has difficulty breathing (keep them semi-upright instead)
- **If unconscious but breathing**: Place in the recovery position (on their side)
- **If not breathing**: Begin CPR

### Step 6: Maintain Body Temperature
- Cover the patient with a blanket, coat, or any available covering to prevent heat loss
- Protect from cold ground (place insulation underneath if possible)
- In Israeli summer conditions: shade the patient from direct sun but still keep warm — shock patients lose body heat even in hot weather
- Do not overheat the patient

### Step 7: Do NOT Give Food or Drink
- Do not give the patient anything to eat or drink
- If thirsty, moisten lips with water only

### Step 8: Monitor and Reassure
- Monitor breathing and pulse continuously
- Reassure and comfort the patient; keep them calm
- Stay with the patient until MDA emergency services arrive
- If the patient vomits and no spinal injury is suspected, turn them on their side to prevent choking

### Step 9: Document and Report
- Note the time of injury/onset
- Note any treatments given (e.g., tourniquet application time)
- Report all information to arriving MDA team

## Israeli-Specific Considerations

- **Climate**: In hot weather (common in Israel, especially Negev/Arava/Jordan Valley), dehydration-induced hypovolemic shock is a significant risk. Move the patient to shade. Shock patients may still need insulation from heat loss despite ambient warmth.
- **Security incidents**: Blast injuries and shrapnel wounds frequently cause haemorrhagic shock. Israeli civilian first aid training includes tourniquet application and wound packing. Emergency bandages (Israeli bandage / אמצא"ש) are widely available.
- **Emergency numbers**: MDA = 101, Police = 100, Fire = 102
