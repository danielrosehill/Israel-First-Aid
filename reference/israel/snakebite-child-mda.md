## Venomous Snakes Relevant to Children in Israel

The primary venomous snake of concern in Israel is:

- **Palestine viper** (*Daboia palaestinae*, formerly *Vipera palaestinae*) -- the most common cause of venomous snakebite in Israel, responsible for 100-300 reported envenomations per year across all age groups.

Other venomous species present in Israel include: Israeli viper, horned viper, burrowing asp, black desert cobra, large and small Sahara sand vipers. However, the Palestine viper accounts for the overwhelming majority of clinically significant bites.

## Why Children Are at Higher Risk

- **Proportionally more venom per kilogram of body weight** -- the same venom dose from a bite affects a child more severely than an adult.
- **Faster symptom progression** -- systemic signs (tachycardia, vomiting, hypotension) may develop more rapidly.
- **Smaller blood volume** -- greater risk of haemodynamic instability.
- **Fear and agitation** -- children are harder to keep calm and still, which can accelerate venom spread through the lymphatic system.
- **Bite locations** -- 63% on lower limbs, 33% on upper limbs, 4% on head/neck (Israeli pediatric data, Paret et al. 1997).

## First Aid Protocol: Snakebite in Children

### Step 1: Ensure Safety
- Move the child (and bystanders) away from the snake. Maintain at least 2 metres distance.
- Do NOT attempt to catch, kill, or handle the snake.
- If safe to do so from distance, photograph the snake for hospital identification.

### Step 2: Call 101 (MDA) Immediately
- Call Magen David Adom at **101** or use the MDA app.
- Request an ambulance. Follow dispatcher instructions.
- **Treat every snakebite as a venomous bite** until proven otherwise -- do not wait for symptoms.
- Report the child's age, weight, bite location, and time of bite.

### Step 3: Keep the Child Calm and Still
- Reassure the child. Speak calmly and confidently.
- Have the child lie down. Minimise all movement.
- **Immobility is critical** -- snake venom travels through the lymphatic system, which is activated by body movement, not heartbeat.
- Movement and agitation accelerate venom spread.

### Step 4: Position the Bitten Limb
- Keep the bitten limb **below heart level** to slow venom distribution.
- Immobilise the limb against the body (as for a fracture -- a splint may be used).

### Step 5: Remove Constrictive Items
- Remove rings, bracelets, watches, anklets, and tight clothing from the affected limb.
- Swelling can develop rapidly, and constrictive items can cause secondary injury.

### Step 6: Clean the Wound (if materials available)
- Gently clean the bite site with **water and soap only**.
- Do not apply antiseptic, alcohol, or any other substance.

### Step 7: Monitor and Prepare for Transport
- Monitor for signs of systemic envenomation:
  - Swelling spreading beyond the bite site
  - Tachycardia (rapid heartbeat)
  - Vomiting, nausea, abdominal pain
  - Difficulty breathing or swallowing
  - Altered consciousness, pallor
  - Hypotension (dizziness, fainting)
  - Rash, itching (allergic reaction)
- If the child loses consciousness and stops breathing, begin CPR immediately.
- Transport urgently to hospital -- all snakebite victims require medical evaluation.

## What NOT to Do (Critical Prohibitions)

| Prohibited Action | Reason |
|-------------------|--------|
| Do NOT apply a tourniquet | Concentrates venom, causes tissue damage |
| Do NOT cut the bite site | Causes additional injury, does not remove venom |
| Do NOT attempt to suck out venom | Ineffective and risks infection |
| Do NOT apply ice or cold | Can cause tissue damage, does not slow venom |
| Do NOT apply alcohol or antiseptic to the wound | Interferes with assessment |
| Do NOT give the child food or drink | Risk of vomiting, may need anaesthesia at hospital |
| Do NOT induce vomiting | Not relevant and dangerous |
| Do NOT chase or try to catch the snake | Risk of additional bites |
| Do NOT use a pressure immobilisation bandage | Not recommended for viper bites (Israeli protocol) |

## Prevention (Child-Specific, Israel Context)

- Children should wear **closed, high-topped shoes** and **long trousers** when in nature areas, fields, or rocky terrain.
- Walk only in **well-lit areas** at night; use a torch/flashlight on trails.
- Do not flip rocks, logs, or debris (common hiding spots for vipers).
- Maintain home gardens -- trim vegetation, remove rubble and debris from courtyards.
- **Peak season**: Snakebites in Israel are most common in warmer months (spring through autumn). The Palestine viper is active from March to November.
- Supervise children closely in hiking and outdoor recreation areas.

---

## Seasonal and Geographic Context

- The Palestine viper is found throughout Israel, from the Golan Heights to the Negev margins.
- Encounters are more frequent in rural areas, but 43% of pediatric bites in one Israeli study occurred in urban settings.
- Israel's hot climate means children are outdoors more and encounter snakes more during peak activity seasons.
- Antivenom (monovalent equine anti-V. palaestinae) is stocked at Israeli hospitals.
