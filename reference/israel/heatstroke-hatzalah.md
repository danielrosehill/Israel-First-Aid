## Body Temperature Regulation

The human body maintains optimal function within a narrow temperature range of **36.5-37.5 degrees C**. A deviation of just two degrees in either direction impairs normal physiological function. The hypothalamus controls temperature equilibrium through several mechanisms: reduced heat production, increased cardiac output, sweating, peripheral vasodilation, and increased respiratory rate.

When body temperature exceeds **40 degrees C**, proteins undergo denaturation -- structural changes that render them non-functional, creating immediate life-threatening risks.

### Primary Heat Sources in the Body
- **Skeletal muscles**: Primary heat source through continuous energy production. Intense physical exertion (sports, hiking) can cause dangerous overheating.
- **Liver**: Largest metabolic organ, generates significant heat.
- **Blood circulation**: Vessels dilate during overheating to release excess heat through the skin.

## Three Stages of Heat Injury

### 1. Dehydration (התייבשות)

Fluid loss exceeding fluid intake. Can occur in any conditions but is most common in hot environments.

**Symptoms by severity**:
- **Mild**: Thirst, rapid pulse, nausea, restlessness, decreased general function
- **Moderate**: Dizziness, headache, nausea, dryness, weakness, pale skin
- **Severe**: Decreased consciousness, hallucinations, signs of hypovolemic shock

**Urine indicator**: Dark yellow urine signals dehydration.

**Treatment**:
- Place patient at complete rest
- For mild dehydration where patient is NOT vomiting: provide small, frequent amounts of water
- **If vomiting occurs: withhold fluids** to prevent aspiration
- Hospitalisation is necessary for electrolyte replacement in moderate-severe cases
- Call emergency services

### 2. Heat Exhaustion (תשישות חום)

The most common heat-related condition. Results from fluid and electrolyte loss during prolonged heat exposure or physical exertion.

**Symptoms**:
- Rapid pulse
- Low blood pressure
- Diarrhoea
- Nausea, vomiting
- Fainting
- Minimal urine output
- Dizziness
- Muscle cramps
- Headache
- Heavy sweating
- Warm, moist skin

**Treatment**:
- Remove patient from heat source
- Ensure ABC (airway, breathing, circulation) support
- Complete rest
- Fluid administration (if conscious and not vomiting)
- Transfer to hospital for electrolyte correction

### 3. Heat Stroke (מכת חום)

**Critical emergency** -- core body temperature exceeding **40 degrees C** with disrupted thermoregulation.

**Warning signs**:
- Elevated temperature (above 40 degrees C)
- Rapid pulse
- Confusion
- Vomiting
- Loss of consciousness
- Nausea
- Staggering gait (zigzag walking)
- Seizures
- Hallucinations
- Low blood pressure
- **Cardiac arrest** (in severe cases)

## Treatment Protocol

### BLS Level (First Responder):
1. Move patient to a shaded, cool location
2. Remove excess clothing
3. **Active cooling**:
   - Wet towels on body
   - Water spray
   - Cold packs (neck, armpits, groin)
4. Offer small amounts of cool water **only if conscious**
5. Elevate legs if blood pressure drops
6. Call ALS / evacuate if no improvement

### ALS / Paramedic Level:
1. Continuous vital monitoring including core temperature
2. Aggressive cooling methods
3. Oxygen if respiratory distress
4. Anticonvulsants if seizures occur
5. IV fluids for dehydration
6. **Never administer fever-reducing medications** (antipyretics do not work for environmental hyperthermia)
7. **Rectal temperature measurement** is the gold standard (restricted to paramedic/physician use)
8. Urgent hospital evacuation

### Important Note on Fluids in Heat Stroke:
United Hatzalah's "Hot Climate Injuries" page states to **avoid fluid administration in heat stroke** to prevent cardiac strain. This contrasts slightly with MDA guidance which allows small sips if the patient is fully conscious. The concern is that heat stroke patients may have compromised cardiac function, and fluid overload can worsen outcomes. This distinction likely reflects that at the BLS level, if uncertain whether a patient has heat exhaustion vs. heat stroke, err on the side of caution.

## Prevention Guidelines

### General:
- **Drink 2-3 litres of water per person daily** (cool water)
- Check weather forecasts before outdoor activities
- Monitor hydration status through urine colour

### Activity Planning:
- Schedule outdoor activities for early morning and evening hours
- Avoid peak sun hours (10:00-17:00)
- Take **10-15 minute rest breaks in shaded areas** during activities
- Adjust hiking or work schedules to cooler periods

### Clothing and Protection:
- Wear a hat (protective headwear)
- Use sunscreen
- Wear light, appropriate clothing

### Supervision:
- Avoid bringing young children to locations lacking adequate shade
- Remain alert to the condition of others
- Be vigilant during heat wave (sharav/hamsin) events

### High-Risk Groups:
- Infants and young children
- Elderly people
- People with disabilities
- Those with chronic medical conditions

## Israel-Specific Context from United Hatzalah

- United Hatzalah specifically warns about the **sharav (hamsin)** -- intense heat waves common in Israel
- Their guidelines emphasise hiking safety given Israel's desert terrain
- They note that during heat waves, there is an increase in fainting, dehydration, and emergency medical calls
- **Dozens of young children have died in recent years** when left in vehicles during extreme temperatures -- this is a recurring warning
- United Hatzalah dispatchers guide callers through first aid steps while simultaneously dispatching volunteer medics (average response time: 3 minutes)

## Comparison with MDA Guidelines

The protocols are largely consistent between United Hatzalah and MDA. Key points of agreement:

- **Same temperature threshold**: 40 degrees C defines heat stroke
- **Same cooling methods**: wet towels, water spray, cold packs, shade
- **Same prohibition** on fever-reducing medications
- **Same prevention advice**: 2-3L water/day, avoid midday sun, rest breaks

**Minor difference**: United Hatzalah's advanced protocols suggest withholding fluids in confirmed heat stroke to avoid cardiac strain, while MDA allows small sips if the patient is fully conscious. Per project rules, **MDA is the authoritative default** -- small sips of water are acceptable for conscious patients.
