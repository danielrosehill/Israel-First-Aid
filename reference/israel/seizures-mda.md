## Definitions

**Seizure (התקף / פרכוס)**: A sudden, uncontrolled electrical disturbance in the brain that can cause changes in behaviour, movements, feelings, and levels of consciousness.

**Tonic-clonic seizure (התקף טוני-קלוני / Grand Mal)**: The most recognised type -- involves loss of consciousness, body stiffening (tonic phase), followed by rhythmic jerking (clonic phase). This is the type most likely to require bystander first aid.

**Status epilepticus (סטטוס אפילפטיקוס)**: A seizure lasting longer than 5 minutes, or repeated seizures without recovery between them. This is a medical emergency that can cause irreversible brain damage if lasting more than 30-60 minutes.

---

## Recognition / Signs of a Tonic-Clonic Seizure

- Sudden loss of consciousness -- the person may fall to the ground
- Body stiffening (tonic phase) -- may last 10-30 seconds
- Rhythmic jerking of limbs and body (clonic phase) -- may last 1-3 minutes
- Eyes may roll upward or to the side
- Jaw may clench; the person may bite their tongue or inner cheek
- Excessive salivation or foaming at the mouth
- Possible loss of bladder or bowel control
- The person may make grunting or snoring sounds
- Skin may turn bluish (cyanosis), especially around the lips
- After the seizure: confusion, drowsiness, headache, muscle soreness (postictal state)

## Protocol: First Aid for an Adult Having a Seizure

### Step 1 -- Scene safety and protect the person

1. **Stay calm.** A seizure may look frightening but most seizures end on their own within 1-3 minutes.
2. **Note the time the seizure started.** Timing is critical for determining whether emergency services are needed.
3. **Clear the area** around the person. Move sharp objects, furniture, hard items, or anything that could cause injury during convulsions.
4. **Place something soft and flat under the head** -- a folded jacket, towel, cushion, or blanket -- to prevent head injury from repeated impact with the ground.
5. **Loosen tight clothing** around the neck (ties, scarves, collars, necklaces, belts) to aid breathing.

### Step 2 -- Position the person

1. **Gently guide the person to the ground** if they are standing or seated when the seizure begins.
2. **If the person is vomiting or producing excessive saliva**, turn them onto their side (recovery position / תנוחת הצלה) with the mouth pointing downward. This prevents aspiration and keeps the airway clear.
3. **Tilt the head slightly to the side or downward** to allow fluids to drain from the mouth.

### Step 3 -- Stay with the person

1. **Remain with the person throughout the seizure** and for several minutes after it ends. There is a risk of additional seizures.
2. **Do NOT leave the person alone** until they are fully alert and oriented.
3. **Speak calmly and reassuringly** when the person begins to regain consciousness. They will likely be confused and disoriented (postictal state).

### Step 4 -- After the seizure ends

1. **Place the person in the recovery position** (on their side) if not already done.
2. **Check that the person is breathing normally.**
3. **Do NOT offer food, water, or medication by mouth** until you are certain the person is fully conscious and able to swallow without choking.
4. **Stay with the person** until they have fully recovered or until emergency services arrive.
5. **Tell the person what happened** calmly -- they will likely have no memory of the seizure.

---

## DO NOT -- Critical Prohibitions

- **DO NOT restrain the person** or try to hold them down. You cannot stop a seizure by force, and restraining can cause injuries (fractures, dislocations, soft tissue injury).
- **DO NOT put anything in the person's mouth** -- no spoons, wallets, sticks, fingers, or any other object. The person **cannot swallow their tongue**. Putting objects in the mouth risks breaking teeth, injuring the jaw, or causing the person to bite your fingers.
- **DO NOT attempt mouth-to-mouth resuscitation** during the seizure. People usually resume breathing on their own once the seizure ends.
- **DO NOT pour water on the person** or attempt to "wake them up."
- **DO NOT offer food or drinks** during or immediately after the seizure until the person is fully alert.
- **DO NOT crowd around the person** -- give them space and privacy. Ask bystanders to step back.
- **DO NOT move the person** unless they are in immediate danger (e.g., in the middle of a road, near a ledge, next to fire or water).

## When to Call 101 (MDA)

Call Magen David Adom (101) immediately if:

1. **The seizure lasts more than 5 minutes** -- this is status epilepticus and a medical emergency
2. **A second seizure follows without the person regaining consciousness** between episodes
3. **The seizure is different from the person's typical pattern** (if they have known epilepsy) -- longer, more intense, or different type
4. **The person does not regain consciousness** or does not return to their normal state within the expected timeframe
5. **Injury occurs during the seizure** that requires medical evaluation (head injury, bleeding, suspected fracture)
6. **The person is not known to have epilepsy** -- a first-time seizure always requires medical evaluation
7. **The seizure occurs in water** (risk of drowning)
8. **The person is pregnant**
9. **The person has diabetes** (seizure may be related to blood sugar)
10. **The person has difficulty breathing** after the seizure ends

## Rescue Medications (for known epilepsy patients)

These medications are prescribed by a neurologist and should only be administered if the person has a pre-existing prescription and the caregiver/family has been trained in their use:

| Medication | Route | When to give |
|---|---|---|
| **Midazolam (Buccolam / דורמיקום)** | Buccal (inside the cheek) | Seizure lasting >3 minutes |
| **Diazepam (Stesolid / סטזוליד)** | Rectal | Seizure lasting >3 minutes |
| **Clonazepam (Rivotril / ריווטריל)** | Oral drops | Seizure lasting >3 minutes |
| **Clobazam (Frisium / פריזיום)** | Oral | As directed by physician |

**Important**: Administer rescue medications at the dose prescribed by the treating neurologist. If a rescue medication is given and the seizure does not stop, call 101 (MDA) immediately.

## Israel-Specific Context

### Climate considerations
- **Heat-related seizures**: Israel's extreme summer heat and desert climate can exacerbate seizure risk through dehydration and electrolyte imbalance. Ensure the person is moved to shade if the seizure occurs outdoors in hot conditions.
- **Post-seizure heat exposure**: A person in the postictal state may be unable to protect themselves from sun/heat. Provide shade and hydration (only once fully alert).

### Security/emergency context
- **Seizure management in shelters (mamad / ממ"ד)**: The Israeli League for Prevention of Epilepsy published specific guidelines (February 2026) for managing seizures in safe rooms during emergencies. Key points:
  - Keep at least 48 hours of regular medications pre-divided into doses in the safe room
  - Keep rescue medications (Buccolam, Stesolid, Rivotril) accessible
  - Syrups remain stable outside refrigeration for 24-48 hours
  - If a specific medication is unavailable, a generic substitute (with pharmacist/doctor approval) is preferable to stopping treatment
- **Israeli Epilepsy Association support line**: 02-5000283

### Emergency numbers
- **MDA (ambulance)**: 101
- **Police**: 100
- **Fire**: 102
