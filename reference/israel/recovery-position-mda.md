## When to Use the Recovery Position

Use the recovery position when a person is:
- **Unconscious (Mekhusar Hakara) but BREATHING**
- Not suspected of having a spinal injury
- Not requiring CPR (i.e., they have a pulse and are breathing)

### The Immediate Danger of Unconsciousness (MDA Teaching)

MDA training materials identify two primary risks for an unconscious person:

1. **Tongue collapse (Nefilat Bsis HaLashon)**: The jaw drops and the tongue falls back, blocking the airway
2. **Aspiration (Shei'ufah)**: Inhalation of secretions — food, saliva, or stomach acid — into the airways and lungs

The recovery position addresses both risks by keeping the airway open and allowing fluids to drain.

## Assessment Before Placing in Recovery Position

### Step 1: Check Responsiveness
- Try to make contact: call out "Are you OK?" (Ata beseder?)
- If no response, tap or gently shake the shoulders
- Pinch the shoulders if needed

### Step 2: Check Airway
- Open the person's mouth and look inside
- Tilt the body to the side
- Use a gripping motion to clear any visible obstructions (food, dentures, vomit)

### Step 3: Check Breathing
- Look for chest movement
- Listen for breathing sounds
- Feel for breath on your cheek
- Take no more than 10 seconds

### Step 4: Call for Help
- If the person is unresponsive but breathing, call MDA at 101
- If NOT breathing, begin CPR — do NOT place in recovery position

## How to Place in Recovery Position

### Standard Technique (Israeli Protocol)

1. **Kneel beside the person** who is lying on their back
2. **Extend the arm nearest to you** at a right angle to the body, palm facing up
3. **Take the far arm** and fold it across the chest so the back of the hand rests against the cheek closest to you — hold it in place
4. **Bend the far knee** up to a right angle using your free hand
5. **Roll the person toward you** by pulling on the bent knee — they should roll smoothly onto their side
6. **Adjust the top leg** so both the hip and knee are bent at right angles (stabilising the position)
7. **Tilt the head back slightly** to keep the airway open
8. **Adjust the hand under the cheek** to maintain head tilt

### Result
The person is lying on their side with:
- The higher shoulder angled toward the ground
- The knees bent for stability
- The airway open and tilted to allow drainage
- Stomach contents can flow out freely if the person vomits

## Monitoring

- **Stay with the person** until MDA arrives
- **Check breathing regularly** — if breathing stops, roll them onto their back and begin CPR
- **Monitor for vomiting** — the position should allow natural drainage, but clear the mouth if needed
- **Keep warm** — cover with a blanket; unconscious people lose heat rapidly
- **If in the recovery position for more than 30 minutes**, turn the person to the opposite side to prevent pressure injuries (this is a standard recommendation)

---

## Exceptions — When NOT to Use Recovery Position

### Suspected Spinal Injury
- **Do NOT move the person** unless they are in immediate danger (fire, drowning)
- If you must move them, support the head and neck in line with the body (manual in-line stabilisation)
- Suspect spinal injury if: fall from height, road accident, blow to the head/neck, diving accident, or the person complains of neck/back pain

### Not Breathing / No Pulse
- **Begin CPR immediately** — do NOT place in recovery position
- The person must be on their back for effective chest compressions

### Pregnant Women
- Place on the **LEFT side** when possible to maintain proper blood flow to the baby and prevent compression of the inferior vena cava

## Israeli Context

- **Hebrew terminology**: Tanukhat Hit'oshshut (תנוחת התאוששות) — literally "recovery posture"; also Tanukhat Tzad (תנוחת צד) — "side posture"
- **MDA first aid courses** teach this technique as a core skill — it is included in the 22-hour basic course, the 44-hour provider course, and the family safety course
- **Security context**: In mass-casualty events, recovery position is used for unconscious but breathing casualties while awaiting triage
