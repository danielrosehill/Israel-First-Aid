## Blast Injury Protocol (from Ichud Hatzalah)

### Blast Injury Classification

**Primary Injuries**
- From pressure waves affecting air-filled body cavities
- Affects lungs, digestive system, ears, skull
- Blast lung is the most immediately life-threatening

**Secondary Injuries**
- From projectiles and shrapnel embedded in munitions or environmental debris
- Multiple small fragment wounds common
- Fragments may cause widespread injuries across different body areas

**Tertiary Injuries**
- Victim thrown against structures
- Blunt trauma, fractures, abdominal injuries

### Treatment Protocol (PHTLS / ABCDE)

Ichud Hatzalah follows **PHTLS (Prehospital Trauma Life Support)** methodology:

**S -- Scene Safety**
- Verify scene is safe before approaching
- Risk of secondary explosive devices
- Structural collapse risk

**A -- Airway**
- Ensure airway patency
- Check for thermal airway injury (stridor = warning sign)
- Manual airway manoeuvres as needed

**B -- Breathing**
- Assess breathing symmetry and quality
- Asymmetric chest rise indicates thoracic trauma (pneumothorax)
- Monitor respiratory rate and effort

**C -- Circulation**
- Evaluate bleeding control
- Check radial and carotid pulses
- Capillary refill assessment (>4--5 seconds = shock)
- Apply tourniquet for uncontrollable limb haemorrhage
- Direct pressure for compressible bleeding

**D -- Disability**
- Neurological status assessment (AVPU or GCS)
- Pupil assessment

**E -- Exposure**
- Full body examination for additional injuries
- Prevent hypothermia during examination

### Tourniquet Application (from case study)
- Improvised tourniquet successfully used for femoral artery bleeding from open fracture
- Applied proximal to wound site
- Time of application recorded

## Child-Specific Considerations

### From Ichud Hatzalah Paediatric Materials
- **No dedicated paediatric blast injury protocol** published on 1221.org.il
- Paediatric treatment approach page emphasises general principles applicable to all injuries:
  - Children have smaller airways, more prone to obstruction
  - Infants utilise approximately 95% of oxygen supply
  - Children lose heat rapidly -- hypothermia prevention critical in blast-injured children
  - Allow children to remain with parents/caregivers when possible
  - Communicate honestly with children about procedures

### Paediatric Blast Injury Considerations (from Israeli context)
- Smaller body mass = greater energy transfer per kilogram
- Thinner chest wall transmits blast wave more easily
- Less blood volume = haemorrhage becomes critical faster
- Multi-organ injury more likely due to organ proximity
- Children at ground level may have different shrapnel trajectory
- Psychological trauma: Ichud Hatzalah operates a dedicated psychotrauma unit

### Psychological First Aid
- Ichud Hatzalah's Psychotrauma and Crisis Response Unit provides psychological first aid
- Basic psychological first aid taught to all 8,000+ volunteers using the "Six C's Model"
- Children may be non-communicative after blast events
- Keeping child with familiar caregiver is critical

### School and Shelter Context
- Israeli education system mandates shelters (miklat/mamad) in schools
- Injuries may occur during rush to shelter (crushing, falling)
- Ichud Hatzalah volunteers respond to schools during rocket attacks

## Gaps and Limitations
- No standalone paediatric blast injury protocol on 1221.org.il
- Paediatric modifications to ABCDE protocol not detailed for blast-specific scenarios
- Tourniquet sizing for children not addressed in available materials
- Triage protocols for mass casualty events involving children not publicly available
