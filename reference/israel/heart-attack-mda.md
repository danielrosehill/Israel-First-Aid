## Recognising a Heart Attack (Symptoms)

**Classic symptoms:**

1. **Chest pain or pressure** — sensation of pressure, tightness, squeezing, heaviness, or burning in the centre of the chest, lasting more than 15 minutes. Described as feeling like "a vice crushing the chest."
2. **Radiating pain** — pain may spread to the left arm (most common), both arms, shoulders, neck, jaw, back, or upper abdomen.
3. **Shortness of breath** — difficulty breathing, choking sensation.
4. **Cold sweating** — profuse, clammy perspiration unrelated to exertion or heat.
5. **Nausea or vomiting**.
6. **Dizziness or lightheadedness**.
7. **Sudden weakness or fatigue**.
8. **Numbness or tingling** — especially in the left arm or hand.
9. **Intense fear or anxiety** — a feeling of impending doom.

**Atypical presentation (especially in women):**

- Extreme fatigue without obvious cause
- Breathing difficulties without significant chest pain
- Upper back or shoulder pain
- Pain resembling heartburn or indigestion
- Symptoms may be less dramatic and easier to dismiss

**Key principle**: Not all heart attacks present with all symptoms. Any combination of the above warrants immediate action. Women are particularly at risk of late diagnosis due to atypical presentation.

## First Aid Protocol — Step by Step

### 1. Call 101 (MDA) immediately

- Dial **101** (Magen David Adom emergency number).
- State clearly that you suspect a heart attack.
- Provide the location and the patient's condition.
- Stay on the line — the MDA dispatcher can guide you through first aid steps.
- Simultaneously, if possible, ask someone else to call a family member.

### 2. Stop all physical activity

- The patient must **rest completely**. Do not allow them to walk, go to the bathroom, or make any physical effort.
- Any exertion, even minor, can worsen damage to the heart muscle.

### 3. Position the patient

- **Seat the patient in a comfortable, half-sitting position** (semi-reclined), with their back supported and knees slightly bent toward the chest.
- Alternatively, allow them to lie on their side if they prefer, but the half-sitting position is generally preferred as it eases breathing.
- **Loosen tight clothing** — unbutton collar, loosen belt, remove tie.
- **Keep the patient calm** — speak reassuringly, reduce anxiety. Stress increases the heart's oxygen demand.

### 4. Administer aspirin (if appropriate)

- **First, confirm**: Ask the patient whether they have a known allergy or sensitivity to aspirin. If allergic, do NOT give aspirin — inform the arriving medical team.
- **Dosage**: **100-300 mg** of aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid).
  - This is typically one regular aspirin tablet (100 mg in Israel) or up to three 100 mg tablets.
  - Some sources specify 300 mg as the target dose.
- **Method**: The patient must **chew** the aspirin, not swallow it whole. Chewing ensures faster absorption into the bloodstream.
- **Why**: Aspirin inhibits platelet aggregation (blood clotting), helping to limit the blockage in the coronary artery and reducing damage to the heart muscle.
- **Important**: Paracetamol (acetaminophen / Acamol) is NOT a substitute for aspirin.

### 5. Nitroglycerin (if prescribed)

- If the patient has a **personal prescription** for nitroglycerin (spray or sublingual tablets), help them take it as directed by their physician.
- Do NOT administer nitroglycerin that was not prescribed to this specific patient.

### 6. Monitor the patient continuously

- Stay with the patient at all times.
- Watch for changes in consciousness, breathing, and skin colour.
- Be prepared to escalate to CPR if the patient loses consciousness.

### 7. Use an AED (if available)

- If an automated external defibrillator (AED / דפיברילטור) is available nearby (common in public places in Israel), retrieve it.
- Turn it on — it will provide voice instructions in Hebrew, step by step.
- No prior training is required; the device guides the user.
- Apply pads as shown in the diagrams on the device.

### 8. If the patient loses consciousness — begin CPR

- **Verify unresponsiveness**: Tap and shout. Check for normal breathing (look, listen, feel — no more than 10 seconds).
- **Open the airway**: Head-tilt, chin-lift manoeuvre.
- **Begin chest compressions**:
  - Place the heel of one hand on the centre of the chest (sternum), other hand on top.
  - Compress hard and fast: depth of at least 5 cm, rate of **100-120 compressions per minute**.
  - Allow full chest recoil between compressions.
- **Rescue breaths** (if trained and willing): 30 compressions to 2 breaths ratio. Use a protective barrier (face shield/pocket mask) if available.
- **If untrained or unwilling**: Hands-only CPR (continuous compressions without breaths) is acceptable and effective.
- **Continue until**: MDA team arrives, an AED is applied, or the patient regains consciousness and starts breathing normally.

## Critical Do's and Don'ts

### DO:

- Call 101 immediately — every minute of delay increases irreversible heart muscle damage.
- Keep the patient completely at rest.
- Give aspirin (chewed, 100-300 mg) if no allergy.
- Keep the patient calm and reassured.
- Loosen tight clothing.
- Be prepared to start CPR.
- Use an AED if available.

### DO NOT:

- Do NOT allow the patient to walk, exert themselves, or deny symptoms.
- Do NOT give the patient food or beverages.
- Do NOT leave the patient alone (except briefly to call for help).
- Do NOT give aspirin if the patient is allergic to it.
- Do NOT give nitroglycerin that was not prescribed to this patient.
- Do NOT wait to see if symptoms resolve on their own.
- Do NOT drive the patient to hospital yourself — call 101 and wait for the ambulance (MDA has cardiac monitoring equipment and can begin treatment en route).

## Israeli Context Notes

- **Emergency number**: 101 (MDA). Alternative: 1221 (United Hatzalah — dispatches nearby volunteer first responders, often arrives faster in urban areas).
- **Climate consideration**: In Israel's hot climate, keep the patient cool and shaded while waiting for the ambulance. Heat stress can compound cardiac distress.
- **AED availability**: AEDs are widely deployed in Israeli public spaces (malls, train stations, public buildings). MDA maintains a registry of AED locations.
- **Aspirin formulation**: Standard aspirin tablets in Israel are commonly 100 mg (Micro-Aspirin / אספירין). Three tablets = 300 mg.
- **MDA response**: MDA dispatches both a Basic Life Support (BLS) ambulance and, for suspected cardiac events, a Mobile Intensive Care Unit (MICU / ניידת טיפול נמרץ) with paramedics capable of 12-lead ECG and advanced interventions.

## Notes on Source Authority and Gaps

1. **MDA's public-facing web content** (mdais.org/101/mi-signs) provides symptom recognition guidance but is relatively brief on the full first aid protocol. The detailed steps above are synthesised from MDA's public guidance, their BLS training materials, and corroborating Israeli medical sources (Maccabi, Israeli cardiology sites) that are consistent with MDA teaching.

2. **MDA's full BLS and ALS protocol PDFs** (blsguide2016.pdf, als-2024.pdf) are available on the MDA website but are aimed at trained EMTs/paramedics rather than lay first aiders. The BLS guide dates from January 2016; the ALS guide was updated in April 2024. These could not be directly fetched for content extraction during this research session due to access restrictions, but their URLs are recorded above for manual review.

3. **Aspirin dosage**: Israeli sources consistently recommend 100-300 mg chewed. This aligns with international guidelines (AHA recommends 162-325 mg). The exact dose within this range is less critical than ensuring the aspirin is chewed promptly.

4. **W-position**: The term "W-position" (sometimes used in Israeli first aid training for the half-sitting/semi-reclined posture) was not explicitly found in the online sources reviewed, though the described positioning (seated, back supported, knees bent) matches what is commonly taught in MDA first aid courses under this name.

5. **Gap**: MDA does not appear to publish a comprehensive, publicly accessible, step-by-step heart attack first aid guide in English on their website. Their Hebrew content is more detailed but is mostly in PDF training materials rather than web pages. The AFMDA (American Friends of MDA) offers a downloadable "Israel First-Aid Skills" resource but requires a form submission to access.
