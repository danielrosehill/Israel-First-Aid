## Overview

Electrocution (Hebrew: hithashmlelut / התחשמלות) occurs when electrical current passes through the human body. The body is an electrical conductor, and the severity of injury depends on the voltage, current type (AC vs DC), duration of contact, pathway through the body, and tissue resistance.

Alternating current (AC), which is the standard in Israeli household and industrial supply, is generally more dangerous than direct current (DC) because it causes sustained muscle contraction that may prevent the victim from releasing the electrical source.

---

## Types of Electrical Injury

### Low-Voltage (under 1,000 volts)
- Most common type -- household and workplace accidents
- Standard Israeli household supply is 220V / 50Hz AC
- Can still cause cardiac arrest, burns, and death
- Muscle tetany may lock the victim's grip onto the source

### High-Voltage (1,000 volts and above)
- Power lines, industrial equipment, electrical substations
- Causes severe deep tissue burns along the current pathway
- Entry and exit wounds may appear small but conceal massive internal damage
- Arc flash can ignite clothing and cause secondary thermal burns
- High voltage can arc across gaps -- maintain safe distance (at least 10 metres from downed power lines)
- In addition to calling MDA (101), call the Israel Electric Corporation (IEC) at 103

### Lightning Strike
- Least common but highly dangerous
- Can cause cardiac arrest, burns, blast injuries, and neurological damage

---

## Injury Mechanisms and Effects

Electrical current can cause:

- **Burns** -- external (entry/exit wounds) and internal tissue destruction along the current pathway. Internal injuries may be far more severe than visible skin damage suggests.
- **Cardiac effects** -- arrhythmias, ventricular fibrillation, cardiac arrest
- **Respiratory effects** -- respiratory muscle paralysis, respiratory arrest
- **Neurological effects** -- loss of consciousness, confusion, disorientation, memory loss, seizures, nerve damage, numbness, paralysis
- **Musculoskeletal effects** -- severe muscle contractions, muscle tears, fractures (from falls or violent contraction)
- **Vascular injuries** -- internal bleeding, blood vessel damage
- **Kidney damage** -- from muscle breakdown products (rhabdomyolysis)

## First Aid Protocol -- Step by Step

### 1. Ensure Scene Safety -- DO NOT Touch the Victim

**This is the most critical step.** Contact with an electrocution victim who is still connected to the power source can be fatal to the rescuer. There have been many documented cases of "chain electrocution" where rescuers were injured or killed.

- **DO NOT touch the victim with bare hands or any wet/damp object** while they may still be in contact with the electrical source
- **DO NOT approach** if high-voltage lines are involved until confirmed safe by IEC (103)

### 2. Disconnect the Power Source

- **Low-voltage (household):** Turn off the power at the mains breaker/fuse box, or unplug the appliance from the wall socket
- **If you cannot disconnect the power:** Use a dry, non-conducting object (dry wooden broom handle, dry rope, dry rubber mat, dry clothing) to push or pull the victim away from the electrical source. Ensure the object is completely dry.
- **Stand on a dry, insulating surface** (dry wood, dry rubber mat, stack of newspapers) while attempting to separate the victim from the source
- **High-voltage:** DO NOT approach. Call MDA (101), Fire Services (102), and Israel Electric Corporation (103). Maintain at least 10 metres distance. Wait for professional teams to de-energise the source.

### 3. Call Emergency Services

- Call **MDA at 101** immediately
- For high-voltage incidents, also call **Fire Services at 102** and **Israel Electric Corporation at 103**
- Report: location, number of casualties, whether the power source has been disconnected, victim's condition

### 4. Assess the Victim

Once the scene is confirmed safe (power disconnected or victim separated from source):

- **Check consciousness:** Call out to the victim, tap their shoulders
- **Check breathing:** Look, listen, and feel for breathing for no more than 10 seconds

### 5. Begin CPR if Needed

- If the victim is **unresponsive and not breathing** (or only gasping), begin **full CPR immediately**:
  - 30 chest compressions followed by 2 rescue breaths
  - Compression rate: 100--120 per minute
  - Compression depth: 5--6 cm
  - Allow full chest recoil between compressions
- **Use an AED (defibrillator) if available** -- ensure the victim's chest is dry before applying pads
- **DO NOT stop resuscitation** until professional EMS teams arrive
- Electrocution victims, particularly younger patients, have a higher-than-average survival rate from cardiac arrest if CPR is started promptly

### 6. If the Victim Is Unconscious but Breathing

- Place in the **recovery position** (on their side)
- Monitor breathing continuously until EMS arrives
- Be prepared to begin CPR if breathing stops

### 7. Treat Burns

- Do not attempt to cool electrical burns with water while the victim may still be connected to the source
- Once the scene is safe, cool visible burns with cool (not cold) running water for at least 10 minutes
- Cover burns loosely with cling film or a clean, non-fluffy dressing
- Do NOT apply creams, ointments, or home remedies to electrical burns
- Remember: external burns may be small but internal tissue damage can be extensive

### 8. Suspect Spinal Injury

- If the victim fell from height or was thrown by the electrical force, suspect spinal injury
- Minimise movement of the head, neck, and spine
- Professional removal may be necessary

### 9. Treat for Shock

- Keep the victim lying down
- Keep them warm (cover with a blanket)
- Do not give food or drink
- Elevate legs if no spinal injury is suspected (and victim is not having difficulty breathing)

## What NOT to Do

- **DO NOT touch the victim** while they may still be in contact with the electrical source
- **DO NOT use wet objects** to separate the victim from the source
- **DO NOT approach high-voltage incidents** -- wait for professional teams
- **DO NOT perform CPR on a conscious victim**
- **DO NOT assume the injury is minor** based on small visible burns -- internal damage may be severe
- **DO NOT apply water to burns** while the victim is still connected to the electrical source

---

## When to Go to Hospital

**All electrocution victims must be evaluated at a hospital**, even if the injury appears minor. This is a firm MDA recommendation. Reasons include:

- Cardiac arrhythmias can develop hours after the incident
- Internal tissue damage may not be immediately apparent
- Rhabdomyolysis (muscle breakdown) can cause kidney failure
- Neurological symptoms may be delayed
- Hidden entry/exit wound burns may require specialist assessment

## Israel-Specific Context

### Electrical Supply
- Standard household voltage in Israel: **220V AC, 50Hz** -- higher than USA (120V), meaning household electrocution in Israel carries greater risk
- Israeli electrical outlets use Type H (SI 32) plugs; older buildings may have Type C
- Many older buildings in Israel may have outdated electrical systems without modern circuit breakers (differential switches / מפסק פחת)

### Climate Considerations
- Israel's hot climate means people may be sweating, wet, or barefoot, all of which lower skin resistance and increase electrocution severity
- Outdoor electrical hazards increase during the rainy season (October--March) when water and electricity may combine
- Construction sites, common throughout Israel, are frequent locations for electrocution incidents

### Emergency Numbers
- **MDA (ambulance):** 101
- **Fire Services:** 102
- **Israel Electric Corporation (IEC):** 103

### High-Voltage Infrastructure
- Israel Electric Corporation (Chevrat HaChashmal) operates the national grid
- High-voltage lines and substations are present throughout the country
- Agricultural areas, construction sites, and industrial zones carry elevated risk
