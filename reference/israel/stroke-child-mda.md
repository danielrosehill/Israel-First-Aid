## Key Context: Pediatric Stroke

- Pediatric stroke is **rare but under-recognised**. Approximately 40,000 children worldwide experience stroke each year.
- Peak incidence occurs in the perinatal period, before age 5, and in adolescence.
- Because stroke in children is often not suspected, diagnosis is frequently delayed, resulting in missed opportunity to intervene.
- Pediatric stroke can present similarly to adult stroke but can also present in **nonspecific ways** and mimic other childhood diseases (migraine, seizure, traumatic brain injury, brain tumour).
- **MDA does not publish a separate pediatric stroke protocol**; the general stroke FAST protocol applies to all ages. The information below supplements MDA's general guidance with pediatric-specific considerations drawn from AHA 2024 first aid guidelines.

## Recognition: FAST Protocol (All Ages)

MDA uses the internationally recognised FAST mnemonic for stroke recognition:

| Letter | Check | What to look for |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| **F** - Face (panim / פנים) | Ask the person to smile | Facial drooping or asymmetry - one side of the face does not move normally |
| **A** - Arms (zro'ot / זרועות) | Ask the person to raise both arms | One arm drifts downward or cannot be raised; unequal strength between sides |
| **S** - Speech (dibur / דיבור) | Ask the person to repeat a simple phrase | Slurred, confused, or incomprehensible speech; difficulty forming words |
| **T** - Time (zman / זמן) | Note the time symptoms began | Every minute counts. Call 101 immediately. |

**MDA guidance**: If any one of these signs is present, call 101 immediately and state that you suspect a stroke (שבץ מוחי / אירוע מוחי). Rapid evacuation to a hospital with a stroke unit is critical.

## Pediatric-Specific Signs and Symptoms

While FAST applies to children, the AHA 2024 first aid guidelines note that adult stroke screening tools (FAST, Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale) are **not validated in the pediatric population** and should not solely be used to identify stroke in children. Children may present with additional or different signs.

### Focal Signs and Symptoms (Table 1, AHA 2024)
- Hemiparesis (weakness on one side)
- Limb weakness
- Facial droop
- Altered sensation
- Visual disturbance
- Speech disturbance

### General Signs and Symptoms (Table 1, AHA 2024)
- **Altered mental status** (confusion, decreased responsiveness)
- **Seizure** (especially new-onset, often one-sided)
- **Headache** (sudden and severe, especially with vomiting and sleepiness)
- **Ataxia** (loss of balance, difficulty walking)
- **Vertigo / dizziness**
- **Nausea / vomiting**

### How Pediatric Presentation Differs from Adults
- Seizures at onset are more common in children than adults
- Sudden severe headache with vomiting and sleepiness is a prominent warning sign
- Symptoms may appear gradually rather than suddenly
- Presentation may be mistaken for migraine, epilepsy, or behavioural changes
- In very young children, symptoms may be subtle (irritability, feeding difficulty, lethargy)

## Risk Factors in Children

Pediatric stroke has different risk factors from adult stroke:

- **Sickle cell disease** - a significant risk factor, particularly relevant in Israel's diverse population
- Congenital heart disease
- Coagulation disorders / blood clotting abnormalities
- Vascular malformations (arteriovenous malformations, aneurysms)
- Infections (meningitis, encephalitis)
- Moyamoya disease
- Head/neck trauma (including sports injuries)
- Dehydration (relevant in Israeli climate - extreme desert heat)

## First Aid Steps for Suspected Pediatric Stroke

### Immediate Actions

1. **Call 101 (MDA) immediately**
   - State clearly: "I suspect a child is having a stroke" (אני חושד/ת שיש לילד/ה שבץ מוחי)
   - Describe the symptoms observed
   - MDA dispatchers will guide you while sending an ambulance

2. **Note the exact time symptoms began**
   - This is critical information for the medical team
   - Certain treatments (clot-dissolving medications) are only effective within a limited time window
   - If you did not witness onset, note the last time the child was seen acting normally

3. **Do NOT give food or drink**
   - Stroke may impair the swallowing reflex
   - Risk of aspiration/choking

4. **Do NOT give any medications**
   - Do not give aspirin or any other medication unless explicitly instructed by medical personnel
   - Aspirin is particularly dangerous: if the stroke is haemorrhagic (bleeding), aspirin will worsen it

5. **Keep the child calm and still**
   - Lay the child down in a comfortable position
   - If conscious, slightly elevate the head and shoulders
   - If unconscious but breathing, place in the recovery position (on their side)
   - If unconscious and not breathing, begin CPR and follow MDA dispatcher instructions

6. **Monitor the child continuously**
   - Watch for changes in consciousness, breathing, and symptoms
   - Be prepared to report progression to MDA paramedics on arrival

7. **Loosen any tight clothing** around the neck and chest

8. **If the child has a seizure**
   - Do not restrain the child
   - Do not put anything in the child's mouth
   - Protect from injury by clearing the area
   - Place on their side after the seizure stops
   - Note the duration and type of seizure for medical personnel

## What NOT to Do

- Do NOT delay calling 101 to "wait and see" if symptoms resolve
- Do NOT give food, drink, or medications
- Do NOT attempt to drive the child to hospital yourself unless MDA explicitly advises it (MDA ambulances can begin treatment en route and take the child to the appropriate stroke centre)
- Do NOT assume a child cannot have a stroke - they can, at any age

---

## Time-Critical Nature

- MDA emphasises that stroke is a disease where **rapid diagnosis and treatment are critical**
- Brain tissue is extremely sensitive to oxygen deprivation; blood supply must be restored quickly
- Therapeutic window: approximately 4.5 hours for clot-dissolving medication (alteplase) in children ages 28 days to 18 years (AHA 2026 guideline)
- Mechanical clot removal may be effective for children 6+ years within 6-24 hours in certain cases
- **Late treatment can cause permanent brain damage and death**

## Israeli Context Notes

- **Emergency number**: 101 (MDA) - available 24/7 nationwide
- **Stroke centres**: Major hospitals in Israel have dedicated stroke units. MDA dispatchers will route to the appropriate facility
- **Climate consideration**: Dehydration from extreme heat is a contributing risk factor for stroke, particularly in the Negev desert region and during summer months. Ensure children stay well hydrated
- **Sickle cell disease**: Present in some Israeli populations (Ethiopian-Israeli community and others); parents/caregivers of children with sickle cell disease should be especially aware of stroke signs
- **Hebrew FAST**: The FAST acronym is used in Israel as-is (English letters), as it is widely taught in MDA first aid courses

## Gaps and Limitations

1. **MDA does not publish a separate pediatric stroke first aid protocol.** The general FAST stroke protocol is applied to all ages. Pediatric-specific guidance in this document is supplemented from AHA 2024 first aid guidelines and the AHA 2026 stroke guideline.
2. **The MDA stroke page (mdais.org/101/stroke) was blocked by bot protection** and could not be fully extracted. Content was reconstructed from search engine caches and is consistent with MDA's known teaching.
3. **No validated pediatric stroke recognition scale exists for first aid providers** (AHA 2024 note). FAST is a reasonable adjunct but may miss atypical pediatric presentations.
4. **Stroke in children is extremely rare** and most MDA first aid courses focus on adult stroke recognition. Parents and caregivers of high-risk children (sickle cell disease, congenital heart disease) should seek additional education.

## AHA 2024 First Aid Guidelines - Key Recommendations for Pediatric Stroke

These are the formal AHA/ARC 2024 recommendations, included for cross-reference:

> **2024 (New):** If pediatric stroke is suspected, EMS should be activated, and the person should be transported to an emergency department.

> **2024 (New):** It is reasonable to consider stroke when common pediatric symptoms are present in association with other neurological signs and symptoms.

> **2024 (New):** Adult stroke scores are not validated in the pediatric population and should not solely be used to identify the broad presentation of stroke in children.
