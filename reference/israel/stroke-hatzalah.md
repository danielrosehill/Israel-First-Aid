## Overview

United Hatzalah publishes a series of educational articles on stroke on their Hebrew-language website (1221.org.il). These cover stroke types, the FAST recognition method, hemorrhagic stroke, and stroke damage by brain location. The content is primarily educational rather than a step-by-step bystander protocol, but contains clinically relevant information consistent with MDA guidance.

---

## FAST Recognition Method (per United Hatzalah)

United Hatzalah uses the same FAST acronym:

### F -- Face (Facial Drooping)
- Check for asymmetry of the face
- Observe whether one side appears weaker or droops compared to the other

### A -- Arm Weakness
- Look for unilateral limb weakness
- Inability to move one side of the body normally

### S -- Speech Difficulty
- Listen for slurred speech
- Difficulty expressing language or comprehension problems
- Patients may know what they want to say but struggle producing words clearly

### T -- Time
- Dual purpose: (1) noting symptom onset time to determine eligibility for thrombolytic treatment, and (2) identifying disorientation regarding time and place

## Neurological Background (Unique to Hatzalah Material)

United Hatzalah's educational material provides additional neurological context not found in the MDA bystander guidance:

- Approximately 87-90% of the population is right-handed, making the left hemisphere dominant
- Blood clots from the heart preferentially enter the left carotid artery
- Left-hemisphere strokes are therefore statistically more common in right-handed individuals
- The left frontal lobe controls both motor function and speech production, explaining why FAST captures the most frequently observable stroke symptoms in field settings

## Stroke Types

### Ischemic (approximately 85%)
- Caused by blood clot blocking an artery
- Can result from atherosclerosis or embolism originating from cardiac arrhythmias (especially atrial fibrillation)

### Hemorrhagic (approximately 13-15%)
- Rupture of a blood vessel with bleeding into brain tissue
- Clot formation and increased intracranial pressure

#### Hemorrhagic Subtypes (detailed in Hatzalah material)
- **Epidural hemorrhage**: Bleeding above the dura mater, typically arterial. Characterised by a "lucid interval" -- temporary loss of consciousness, recovery, then prolonged unconsciousness as bleeding progresses
- **Subdural hemorrhage**: Between dura and arachnoid membrane (30% of head injuries). Acute: symptoms within 72 hours (80% mortality). Subacute: within 21 days (25% mortality). Chronic: over months (50% mortality)
- **Subarachnoid hemorrhage**: Into the subarachnoid space, often from aneurysms. Presents with sudden severe headache
- **Intracerebral hemorrhage**: Direct bleeding into brain tissue, typically from small vessels damaged by hypertension

### TIA
- Symptoms resolve within 1-2 hours (up to 24 hours)
- 40% of TIA patients experience a full stroke within three months

---

## Treatment Window

- Approximately 6 hours from symptom onset
- For patients who went to sleep with normal function and woke with symptoms, onset time is recorded as when sleep began

---

## Stroke Damage by Brain Location

United Hatzalah provides detailed educational material on how symptoms vary by the specific artery affected:

- **Middle Cerebral Artery (Left)**: Facial drooping, right-side weakness/paralysis, speech difficulties (Broca's and Wernicke's aphasia), vision field problems
- **Middle Cerebral Artery (Right)**: Left-side weakness, neglect of left side of body, patient may be unaware of deficits
- **Anterior Cerebral Artery**: Lower limb weakness, emotional disturbances
- **Posterior Cerebral Artery**: Balance problems, vision issues, swallowing difficulties
- **Basilar Artery**: Brainstem and cerebellum damage, potential "locked-in syndrome"

## Emergency Response

- **Call 1221 (United Hatzalah dispatch) or 101 (MDA)** immediately upon recognising FAST signs
- Treatment focuses on ABC protocols -- maintaining airway, breathing, and circulation
- Recognising any change in consciousness level serves as a critical diagnostic indicator
- Additional warning signs beyond FAST: sudden consciousness changes, seizures in adults without prior history, abrupt vision loss or memory problems

---

## Comparison with MDA Guidance

United Hatzalah's stroke material is largely consistent with MDA's guidance. Key observations:

- Both organisations use the FAST recognition method identically
- Both cite the same 6-hour treatment window
- Both emphasise the same stroke type breakdown (85% ischemic, 15% hemorrhagic)
- United Hatzalah provides more detailed neurological educational content (brain anatomy, artery-specific symptoms)
- MDA provides more specific bystander action steps (positioning, do-not-give-food/drink, note time)
- No contradictions were found between the two sources

---

## Gaps and Limitations

- United Hatzalah's website content is primarily educational/informational rather than a step-by-step bystander first aid protocol
- No explicit "what not to do" list was found on the Hatzalah pages
- The articles are not dated, making it difficult to assess currency
- The content focuses more on medical understanding than on actionable bystander steps
