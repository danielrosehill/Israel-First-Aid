## Overview

Stroke (in Hebrew: shavatz mochi / airu'a mochi -- literally "cerebral event") is the third leading cause of death in Israel, after cancer and heart disease. One in six people worldwide will experience a stroke during their lifetime. In stroke, blood supply to the brain is disrupted, causing rapid neuronal death.

**Critical statistic cited by MDA**: "In stroke, every minute two million neurons die in the brain." -- Dr. Shfir Botner, MDA

## FAST Recognition Method

MDA uses the internationally recognised FAST acronym for bystander stroke recognition:

### F -- Face (Panim)
- Check for facial drooping or asymmetry
- Look for distortion or drooping of mouth corners
- One side of the face may appear weaker or numb

### A -- Arm (Zro'a)
- Ask the person to extend both arms forward
- Look for drooping of one arm or inability to raise it
- Unilateral limb weakness or inability to move one side normally

### S -- Speech (Dibur)
- Listen for slurred speech
- Difficulty forming or expressing words
- Language confusion, comprehension problems
- The person may know what they want to say but cannot produce words clearly

### T -- Time (Zman)
- Note the exact time when symptoms first appeared
- This is critical for determining eligibility for thrombolytic treatment
- If the person went to sleep normal and woke with symptoms, onset time is recorded as when sleep began

**MDA guidance: Even a single one of these signs warrants immediate emergency evaluation.**

## Treatment Window

- The window of opportunity for treatment is approximately **6 hours** from symptom onset
- The goal is hospital arrival within **60 minutes** ("golden hour")
- MDA has reduced average pre-hospital time for stroke patients from 54 minutes to approximately 24 minutes through protocol optimisation and pre-arrival hospital notification

## Bystander First Aid Protocol

### What to Do

1. **Recognise the signs** -- Use FAST to identify a possible stroke
2. **Call 101 (MDA)** immediately -- Do not wait to see if symptoms resolve
3. **Note the time** of symptom onset -- This information is critical for hospital treatment decisions
4. **Stay with the person** until the ambulance arrives
5. **Position the person** -- If conscious, lay them down with head slightly raised and supported; if vomiting or at risk of vomiting, place on their side (recovery position) to protect the airway
6. **Loosen restrictive clothing** -- Remove or loosen scarves, ties, tight collars that may restrict breathing
7. **Monitor consciousness and breathing** -- Be prepared to report the person's condition to arriving paramedics
8. **Provide information to paramedics** on arrival: time of symptom onset, current medications, medical history if known

### What NOT to Do

1. **Do not give food or drink** -- The person's swallowing reflex may be impaired (dysphagia is common in stroke), creating aspiration risk
2. **Do not give medications** -- Aspirin or other blood thinners can worsen a hemorrhagic stroke; only medical professionals should decide on medication
3. **Do not delay calling 101** -- Do not drive the person to hospital yourself unless absolutely unavoidable; the ambulance crew will pre-notify the hospital and begin treatment en route
4. **Do not ignore transient symptoms** -- Even if symptoms resolve (possible TIA), the person still needs emergency evaluation; approximately 40% of TIA patients experience a full stroke within three months

## Types of Stroke

### Ischemic Stroke (Airu'a Mochi Ischemi)
- Approximately 85% of all strokes
- Caused by a blood clot blocking an artery supplying the brain
- Treatable with thrombolysis (clot-dissolving medication) within the treatment window

### Hemorrhagic Stroke (Airu'a Mochi Hemoragi)
- Approximately 15% of all strokes
- Caused by rupture of a blood vessel in the brain
- Results in bleeding into brain tissue and increased intracranial pressure
- Thrombolysis is contraindicated -- this is why bystanders must NOT give aspirin

### TIA (Transient Ischemic Attack / Airu'a Ischemi Choleif)
- Symptoms resolve within 1-2 hours (up to 24 hours)
- Still a medical emergency requiring evaluation
- Approximately 40% of TIA patients go on to have a full stroke within three months

## Risk Factors

- Hypertension
- Elevated cholesterol
- Smoking
- Obesity
- Diabetes
- Prior cardiac or cerebrovascular events
- Atrial fibrillation
- Advanced age (55+)

---

## Israel-Specific Context

- **Emergency number**: 101 (Magen David Adom)
- **Hospital destination**: Patients should be transported to hospitals with neurosurgical departments and neurointerventional capability
- MDA ambulance crews pre-notify receiving hospitals to ensure stroke teams are prepared
- Only 50% of stroke patients globally arrive by ambulance -- MDA emphasises calling 101 rather than self-transport
- MDA has trained approximately 500,000 people in first aid courses annually, including stroke recognition
