## What IS Available from United Hatzalah

### General Cardiac Emergency Response

- United Hatzalah volunteers are trained to respond to cardiac incidents including heart attacks, cardiac arrest, chest pain, and arrhythmias
- Their volunteer network of over 8,000 responders provides an average response time of approximately 90 seconds in urban areas
- Volunteers are equipped with personal emergency kits and many carry portable defibrillators (AEDs)
- The organisation responds to pediatric emergencies, including cases involving children

### EZ CPR Application

- United Hatzalah's EZ CPR app is designed to guide bystanders through CPR in real time
- The app alerts nearby United Hatzalah volunteers when a cardiac emergency is reported
- Available for download on iOS and Android
- Provides step-by-step CPR instructions (general -- not specifically pediatric-focused based on available information)

### Family Safety Course

- United Hatzalah offers a "Family Safety" training course aimed at parents, grandparents, teachers, and babysitters
- The course is described as preparing participants to act in medical emergencies involving children
- Specific curriculum content (including whether pediatric cardiac emergencies are covered) is not published online
- Course availability: check https://1221.org.il/ or contact United Hatzalah directly

### Documented Pediatric Cardiac Response

- United Hatzalah has publicly documented cases of responding to pediatric cardiac emergencies, including a case involving CPR on a 13-year-old (referenced on their website)
- This confirms their volunteers are trained and equipped for pediatric cardiac arrest scenarios

## Synthesised Guidance (Based on United Hatzalah's General Approach)

Since United Hatzalah does not publish a standalone pediatric chest pain protocol, the following is based on their general cardiac emergency response approach and training philosophy:

### If a child has chest pain or suspected cardiac symptoms:

1. **Call for help immediately**: Dial **1221** (United Hatzalah) or **101** (MDA)
2. **Stay with the child** and keep them calm
3. **Position the child comfortably** -- sitting up or semi-reclined
4. **Do NOT give aspirin or medications** to a child unless directed by a medical professional
5. **Monitor the child's breathing and consciousness**
6. **If the child becomes unresponsive and stops breathing**: begin CPR
   - Use the EZ CPR app for real-time guidance if available
7. **Use an AED** if available and the child is in cardiac arrest

### United Hatzalah's dispatch (1221) can:

- Send the nearest trained volunteer (often arriving within 90 seconds in cities)
- Provide phone-based first aid guidance while help is en route
- Coordinate with MDA ambulance dispatch if advanced care/transport is needed

## Comparison with MDA

| Aspect | United Hatzalah | MDA |
|---|---|---|
| **Role** | Rapid first response; volunteer-based | National EMS; ambulance transport and advanced care |
| **Emergency Number** | 1221 | 101 |
| **Published Protocols** | Not publicly available | BLS/ALS guides available (Hebrew PDFs) |
| **Pediatric Cardiac Specificity** | No specific published protocol found | General pediatric BLS covered; no standalone child heart attack protocol |
| **Training Courses** | Family Safety course (general) | Pediatric CPR/resuscitation courses |
| **Response Time** | ~90 seconds (urban, first responder) | Varies; ambulance with full equipment |
| **Transport to Hospital** | No (stabilisation only) | Yes (ambulance transport) |

**Note**: In practice, both organisations work together. United Hatzalah volunteers often arrive first and provide initial care; MDA ambulances provide transport and advanced life support. For a child with cardiac symptoms, calling either 1221 or 101 (or both) is appropriate.
