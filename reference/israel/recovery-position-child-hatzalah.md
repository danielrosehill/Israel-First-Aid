## Recovery Position Information

**GAP IDENTIFIED**: Ichud Hatzalah does not publish a standalone recovery position page for any age group. The recovery position (תנוחת התאוששות) is mentioned in passing within several articles but not described step-by-step.

### References to Recovery Position in Ichud Hatzalah Materials

1. **Respiratory Distress page**: Mentions positioning as part of airway management for unconscious patients
2. **Missile Strike Response page**: References recovery position in the context of maintaining airway in unconscious trauma patients
3. **Pediatric Treatment Approach page**: Emphasises airway management differences in children but does not detail the specific recovery position technique

### General Principles (from context across Ichud Hatzalah materials)
- Used for unconscious patients who are breathing
- Purpose: maintain open airway and allow drainage of secretions
- Part of the ABCDE assessment protocol

## Child-Specific Considerations (from Paediatric pages)

### Airway Differences
- Children have anatomically distinct airways requiring special consideration
- Air passages are smaller and more prone to obstruction
- The tongue is large relative to the mouth and can block airways
- Infants are obligate nasal breathers initially
- Airway structure is softer and more easily compromised by improper positioning

### Age Classifications
- Newborns: birth to 4 weeks
- Infants: 3 weeks to 1 year
- Children: 1--8 years
- Adults: 8+ years

### Infant Recovery Position
- Infants should NOT be placed in the standard lateral recovery position
- Hold infant in arms in a head-down, face-down position on the rescuer's forearm
- This allows gravity-assisted drainage of airway secretions
- Support head and jaw; keep head lower than body

### Children (1--8 years)
- Use standard lateral recovery position (same as adults)
- Perform with greater gentleness
- Account for proportionally larger head -- position carefully to avoid neck flexion
- Ensure coverage to prevent hypothermia

### Critical Warning Signs Requiring ALS (not just recovery position)
- Significant shortness of breath
- Central cyanosis
- Abnormal or absent breath sounds
- Accessory muscle use
- Behavioural changes
- Rapid heart rate
- Pallor and sweating
