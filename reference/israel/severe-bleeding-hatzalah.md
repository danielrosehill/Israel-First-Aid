## Protocol: How to Stop Bleeding (עצירת דימומים)

### Background

- External bleeding occurs when blood exits the body through a wound.
- Loss of **1.5 litres of blood is life-threatening**.
- Even smaller amounts reduce oxygen delivery to cells, potentially causing tissue death.
- The primary danger is **hypovolemic shock and death**.

### Types of Bleeding

| Type | Description | Appearance |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Arterial** | Most dangerous | Rapid, forceful spurting synchronised with heartbeat |
| **Venous** | Serious | Steady, continuous dark-red flow |
| **Capillary** | Least dangerous | Slow oozing from small vessels |

### Types of Hemorrhage

- **External**: Blood flows outside the body; severity depends on damaged vessel type and intensity.
- **Internal**: Bleeding into body cavities; often life-threatening; requires surgical intervention.
- **Subcutaneous**: Blood accumulation under skin from small vessels; usually non-threatening but may indicate serious internal injury.

### Assessment

- A rapid pulse is an early sign of significant blood loss -- **do not assume tachycardia is caused by anxiety or pain alone**.
- Suspect shock in any trauma patient with bleeding and rapid heart rate.
- **Do not wait for additional shock signs** -- transport immediately.

## Control Methods (in order of escalation)

### 1. Direct Pressure (Primary Method)

1. Apply continuous pressure using **gloved hands or sterile gauze**.
2. Initially use your hand directly on the wound.
3. Replace hand pressure with **pressure dressings** (field dressings or Israeli bandages).
4. Continuous pressure enables clot formation -- do not interrupt.

### 2. Israeli Bandage (Emergency Bandage) Application

United Hatzalah provides specific technique for the two-pad Israeli bandage for penetrating wounds or large injuries:

1. Apply the **white sterile portion** directly to the wound.
2. Wrap around the injury once.
3. Make **two side wraps** with the bandage edges.
4. Loop through the **plastic pressure tab** and rotate the bandage toward the opposite direction.
5. Continue wrapping until complete.

**Warning**: Elastic components can occlude arteries -- **verify distal pulse** after application.

### 3. Wound Packing (PACKING Technique)

For deep wounds in areas where tourniquets cannot be applied: **head, shoulder, groin, axilla (junctional areas)**.

1. Insert specialised gauze **finger-by-finger** into the wound.
2. Fill internally in a **"sunburst" pattern** to create internal pressure against the bleeding vessel.
3. Apply the attached dressing with **direct pressure for 1-2 minutes**.
4. Tighten bandage straps to maintain pressure.

**Contraindications:**
- Do NOT use packing for penetrating stab wounds.
- Do NOT enlarge wound openings to facilitate packing.

### 4. Pressure Points

Compress arteries against underlying bone to reduce distal blood flow as an adjunct:

| Pressure Point | Location | Effect |
|---------------|----------|--------|
| Carotid | Neck | Reduces head blood flow |
| Subclavian | Above collarbone / upper arm | Reduces arm blood flow |
| Brachial | Inner upper arm | Reduces forearm blood flow |
| Femoral | Groin / inner thigh | Reduces leg blood flow |
| Radial-Ulnar | Wrist | Reduces hand blood flow |

### 5. Tourniquet (חוסם עורקים) -- Last Resort

**Indications:**
- Direct pressure fails to control bleeding
- Severe crush injury with exposed bone
- Uncontrollable bleeding
- Multiple wound sites on the same limb
- Cardiac arrest requiring resuscitation (hands must be freed)
- Provider safety concerns (e.g., active threat environment)

**Advantages**: Nearly always stops bleeding effectively.

**Disadvantages**: Completely blocks blood flow distal to application, risking tissue death and gangrene.

**Key Rules:**
- Apply above the bleeding source.
- Tighten until bleeding stops completely.
- Record time of application.
- **Do not remove** -- conversion (replacing tourniquet with direct pressure) may only be performed under **paramedic or physician orders**.

## Comparison with MDA Protocol

United Hatzalah's hemorrhage control protocol is **largely consistent** with MDA guidelines. Notable points:

| Aspect | United Hatzalah | MDA |
|--------|----------------|-----|
| Escalation order | Direct pressure -> Israeli bandage -> Packing -> Tourniquet | Direct pressure -> Israeli bandage -> Tourniquet |
| Wound packing | Detailed "sunburst" technique described | Less detail in public-facing materials |
| Pressure points | Explicitly listed as adjunct | Less emphasis in civilian materials |
| Tourniquet conversion | Requires paramedic/physician order | Requires paramedic/physician order |
| Tourniquet time recording | Required | Required (write on forehead) |

**Synthesis**: Both organisations agree on the fundamentals. MDA's civilian guidance is slightly simpler (appropriate for lay rescuers), while United Hatzalah provides more detail suitable for trained volunteers/EMTs. Where they disagree, **MDA is the authoritative default** per project convention.
