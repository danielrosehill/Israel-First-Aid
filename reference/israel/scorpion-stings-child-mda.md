## Dangerous Scorpion Species in Israel

Israel has approximately 21 species of scorpions, of which only a few (approximately 5) are considered medically significant. The two most dangerous are:

- **Deathstalker scorpion** (*Leiurus quinquestriatus*, Hebrew: עקצן צהוב / "yellow stinger") -- the most dangerous scorpion in Israel. Found throughout the country from the Upper Galilee to the Negev and Arava. Highly neurotoxic venom. While not typically lethal to healthy adults, it **can be deadly for young children**, the elderly, and the infirm.
- **Fat-tailed scorpion** (*Androctonus crassicauda*, Hebrew: עקרב שמן-זנב) -- also found in Israel and capable of causing severe envenomation.

**Critical warning**: The common belief that black scorpions are non-toxic is incorrect. Black scorpions can also be dangerous and potentially lethal.

## Why Children Are at Higher Risk

- **Lower body weight** -- the same venom dose produces a proportionally higher venom-to-body-weight ratio in children, leading to more severe systemic effects.
- **Faster systemic reaction** -- symptoms progress more rapidly in children than in adults.
- **Smaller blood volume** -- greater risk of cardiovascular instability.
- **Developing nervous system** -- more vulnerable to neurotoxic effects.
- **Higher exploratory behaviour** -- children are more likely to disturb scorpions in hiding places (shoes, rocks, toys left outdoors).

### Israeli Pediatric Clinical Data

From a review of 54 pediatric scorpion sting cases at hospitals in the Jerusalem area (Paret et al. 1991):

- Children presented with a range of symptoms from local pain to severe systemic envenomation.
- Intravenous antivenom administration was effective and safe in children with significant symptoms.
- Two fatalities in the study were in patients who did NOT receive intravenous antivenom.
- The study recommends that **specific antivenom should be given intravenously to all children who show significant symptoms**.

## First Aid Protocol: Scorpion Sting in Children

### Step 1: Ensure Safety
- Move the child away from the scorpion immediately.
- Do NOT attempt to catch or handle the scorpion.
- If safe to do so, photograph or note the scorpion's appearance (colour, size) for identification at hospital.

### Step 2: Call 101 (MDA) Immediately
- Call Magen David Adom at **101** or use the MDA app.
- **All scorpion stings in children require emergency medical evaluation** -- do not "wait and see."
- Report the child's age, weight, approximate time of sting, and location on the body.
- Also consider calling the **Israel Poison Information Center at 04-7771900** for guidance while waiting for the ambulance.

### Step 3: Keep the Child Calm and Still
- Reassure the child. Speak calmly and confidently.
- Have the child lie down or sit still. Minimise all movement.
- **Immobilise the stung limb** -- movement accelerates venom distribution through the lymphatic and circulatory systems.
- Anxiety and crying can increase heart rate, which accelerates venom spread.

### Step 4: Cool the Sting Area
- Apply an **ice pack or cold compress** wrapped in cloth to the sting site.
- Cooling achieves two goals: **relieves pain** and **slows the absorption and spread of venom** via vasoconstriction.
- Most effective during the first 2 hours following the sting.
- Apply ice for 10 minutes on, 10 minutes off. Do not apply ice directly to skin.

### Step 5: Immobilise the Affected Limb
- Keep the stung limb **still and below heart level**.
- Immobilise the limb against the body (as for a fracture).

### Step 6: Monitor for Systemic Symptoms
- Watch closely for signs of systemic envenomation, which may develop rapidly in children:

**Neurological symptoms:**
  - Restlessness, agitation, irritability
  - Altered consciousness, drowsiness, confusion
  - Seizures or convulsions
  - Numbness or tingling
  - Breathing difficulties

**Cardiovascular symptoms:**
  - Rapid heartbeat (tachycardia)
  - Irregular heart rhythms
  - Chest pain
  - Signs of heart failure or pulmonary oedema (in severe cases)

**Autonomic symptoms:**
  - Excessive sweating
  - Excessive salivation, drooling
  - Excessive tearing
  - Vomiting, nausea, abdominal pain
  - Muscle contractions, twitching

- **Delayed reactions may occur** -- even if the child initially appears well, hospital observation is essential.

### Step 7: Transport to Hospital
- Rapid evacuation to hospital is critical for all pediatric scorpion stings.
- At hospital, the child will receive:
  - Pain relief
  - Supportive treatment for cardiovascular and respiratory systems
  - Monitoring for arrhythmias and delayed reactions
  - **Scorpion antivenom** in cases with significant systemic symptoms
- Observation period: minimum 6 hours from time of sting; admission if any systemic symptoms develop.

## What NOT to Do (Critical Prohibitions)

| Prohibited Action | Reason |
|-------------------|--------|
| Do NOT apply a tourniquet | Concentrates venom, causes tissue damage |
| Do NOT cut or incise the sting site | Causes additional injury, does not remove venom |
| Do NOT attempt to suck out venom | Ineffective and risks infection |
| Do NOT apply alcohol or antiseptic to the wound | Interferes with assessment |
| Do NOT give the child medication without medical advice | May interact with venom effects or mask symptoms |
| Do NOT underestimate a sting based on scorpion colour | Black scorpions can be as dangerous as yellow ones |
| Do NOT delay calling 101 for children | Children deteriorate faster than adults |

## Prevention (Child-Specific, Israel Context)

### In and Around the Home
- Remove piles of waste, rubble, wood, and debris from the vicinity of living areas and children's play areas.
- Seal gaps around doors, windows, and foundations.
- Use insect screens on windows and doors.
- Keep gardens trimmed and clear of ground debris.

### Before Use
- **Always shake out shoes, boots, and sandals** before putting them on -- especially if left outside overnight.
- Shake out sleeping bags, towels, and clothing that have been on the ground.
- Check bedding before putting children to bed, particularly in ground-floor rooms or tents.

### Outdoors and Nature
- Children should wear **closed, high-topped shoes** and **long trousers** in nature areas, fields, and rocky terrain.
- Do not allow children to put hands into holes, cracks, under rocks, or into dark spaces.
- Use tools, not hands, to move rocks, logs, or debris.
- Use a flashlight when walking at night -- scorpions are nocturnal.
- When camping, remove rocks and debris from the sleeping area and inspect the area before settling in.

### Peak Season
- Scorpion stings are most common during warm months (spring through autumn), particularly in the Negev, Arava, and other arid regions.
- From June to August 2023, MDA treated 212 scorpion sting patients nationally, with 109 cases in the Negev region alone.

## Seasonal and Geographic Context

- The deathstalker scorpion is found throughout Israel, from the Upper Galilee to the Negev and Arava, but encounters are most frequent in the Negev desert region.
- Israel's hot, arid climate in the south provides ideal scorpion habitat.
- Scorpions are nocturnal -- most stings occur in evening and nighttime hours.
- Antivenom for scorpion stings is available at Israeli hospitals.

## Note: Difference from Snakebite Protocol

The MDA scorpion sting protocol differs from the snakebite protocol in one important respect: **cooling/icing the sting site IS recommended for scorpion stings** (to relieve pain and slow venom absorption), whereas ice is **NOT recommended for snakebites**. This is a meaningful protocol difference -- do not confuse the two.
