## Overview

Heat-related illness is a spectrum ranging from mild dehydration through heat exhaustion to life-threatening heat stroke. MDA treats these as distinct stages requiring escalating intervention.

## Definitions

### Dehydration (התייבשות)
- Fluid loss exceeding fluid intake
- Common in hot weather but can occur in any conditions
- High-risk groups: children, infants, elderly

### Heat Exhaustion (תשישות חום)
- Most common heat-related condition
- Results from fluid and electrolyte loss during prolonged heat exposure or physical exertion
- NOT immediately life-threatening but can progress to heat stroke if untreated

### Heat Stroke (מכת חום)
- Core body temperature exceeds **40 degrees C**
- The body's thermoregulation mechanism fails -- sweating stops
- Proteins undergo denaturation, causing organ damage (nervous system, liver, kidneys)
- **Life-threatening emergency requiring immediate intervention**

## Symptoms

### Heat Exhaustion Symptoms
- Heavy sweating
- Warm, moist skin
- Dizziness, fainting sensation
- Nausea, vomiting
- Headache
- Weakness, fatigue
- Muscle cramps
- Rapid pulse

### Heat Stroke Symptoms
- **Elevated body temperature (above 40 degrees C)**
- **Hot, dry skin** (sweating may have stopped)
- Rapid pulse
- Confusion, disorientation
- Staggering gait
- Nausea, vomiting
- Seizures (פרכוסים)
- Hallucinations
- Low blood pressure
- Loss of consciousness
- **Cardiac arrest** (in severe cases)

## First Aid Protocol (BLS -- Bystander / First Responder Level)

### For Heat Exhaustion:
1. Move the person to a shaded or air-conditioned location
2. Have them lie down; elevate legs slightly if blood pressure appears low
3. Remove excess or tight clothing
4. Begin cooling: apply wet towels, spray with cool water, fan the person
5. **If fully conscious and able to swallow**: offer small sips of cool water continuously
6. Monitor for deterioration
7. If symptoms do not improve or worsen, call MDA at **101**

### For Heat Stroke:
1. **Call MDA immediately at 101** -- this is a life-threatening emergency
2. Move the person to shade or an air-conditioned area immediately
3. Remove unnecessary clothing
4. **Begin aggressive cooling**:
   - Apply wet towels to the body (especially neck, armpits, groin)
   - Spray or pour cool/cold water on the body
   - Fan the person vigorously
   - If available, use cold packs on neck, armpits, and groin
5. **If conscious and able to swallow**: offer small amounts of cool water
6. **If NOT fully conscious: do NOT give fluids** (aspiration risk)
7. Elevate legs if blood pressure drops (signs of shock)
8. If the person becomes unresponsive and stops breathing, begin CPR
9. Stay with the patient until MDA ambulance arrives

### Critical Warnings:
- **Never administer fever-reducing medications** (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) -- these do not work for environmental hyperthermia and may cause harm
- Do not give fluids to a person who is not fully conscious
- Do not delay calling 101 for suspected heat stroke

## ALS / Paramedic Level (MDA Ambulance Crews)

- Continuous vital sign monitoring including core temperature
- Aggressive cooling methods (cooling pools at mass events)
- High-concentration oxygen if respiratory distress
- Anticonvulsants if seizures occur
- IV fluids for dehydration (with caution -- avoid fluid overload causing cardiac strain)
- Rectal temperature measurement (gold standard, restricted to paramedic/physician level)
- Urgent hospital evacuation

---

## Prevention (MDA Recommendations)

- **Drink plenty of water** -- at least 2-3 litres per day in hot weather
- **Avoid sun exposure between 10:00 and 17:00** (peak heat hours)
- Wear light, loose-fitting clothing
- Wear a hat and sunglasses
- Use sunscreen
- Stay in air-conditioned spaces when possible
- Never leave children (or anyone) in a parked vehicle
- During physical activity or hiking, take 10-15 minute rest breaks in shade
- Monitor urine colour -- dark yellow indicates dehydration
- Be alert to early warning signs in yourself and others

## Israel-Specific Context

- **Extreme desert heat**: The Negev, Dead Sea region (lowest point on earth), and Arava Valley regularly exceed 40 degrees C in summer. The Dead Sea area can reach 50 degrees C.
- **Hiking risks**: Dehydration and heat stroke are common among hikers, especially on trails in the Judean Desert, Negev, and around the Dead Sea. MDA responds to multiple heat-related hiking emergencies every summer.
- **Sharav (hamsin)**: Hot, dry desert wind events that can raise temperatures dramatically and occur in spring and autumn as well as summer.
- **Mass events**: MDA sets up dedicated treatment stations with cooling equipment at large outdoor events (e.g., Tel Aviv Pride Parade, marathons).
- **Military context**: IDF soldiers in training are a high-risk group; MDA coordinates with military medical services.
