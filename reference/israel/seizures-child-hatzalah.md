## Overview

**Seizure**: A transient event of abnormal electrical activity in the brain that can affect motor, sensory, autonomic, or mental functions.

**Epilepsy**: A chronic condition with increased risk of recurrent seizures. A single seizure does not constitute epilepsy.

**Prevalence**: Approximately 5-10% of the population experience a seizure during their lifetime; only 0.3-0.5% have epilepsy.

## Seizure Types

### Focal (Partial) Seizures
- Limited to one brain hemisphere
- Symptoms: repetitive movements (hand, foot, mouth), incoherent speech
- May include sensory disturbances: flashing lights, balance issues, unusual tastes
- Autonomic signs: heart rate changes, blood pressure shifts, sweating
- Consciousness typically preserved
- Risk of progression to generalised seizure

### Absence Seizures
- Characterised by disconnection and unresponsiveness
- Person appears withdrawn but resumes normal activity afterward
- No confusion or other symptoms post-event
- **Child-specific note**: Absence seizures are particularly common in children and may be mistaken for daydreaming or inattention

### Generalised Tonic-Clonic Seizures
Most common type, with two distinct phases:

**Tonic Phase (10-20 seconds)**:
- Sustained muscle contractions throughout the body
- Chest/throat tightening may produce a loud vocalisation
- Respiration impaired, carbon dioxide accumulates
- Skin pallor develops
- Jaw tension can cause tongue biting

**Clonic Phase (up to 1 minute)**:
- Rhythmic muscle contractions and relaxation cycles
- Visible body jerking

**Post-Ictal Phase**:
- Patient fatigued, confused, possibly unresponsive
- Risk of airway obstruction due to secretions
- May experience urinary/faecal incontinence
- Recovery takes minutes to hours
- Reports of muscle soreness, headache, fatigue

## Status Epilepticus (Emergency)

Continuous seizure lasting **5 or more minutes**, or multiple consecutive seizures without recovery of consciousness between events.

- Requires immediate intervention
- Untreated cases beyond 30 minutes risk **permanent brain damage**
- Complications include pulmonary oedema, aspiration, and spinal fractures

## Assessment and Response Protocol

### During an Active Seizure

1. **Alert dispatch** requesting an ALS (Advanced Life Support) team
2. **Establish airway** -- position patient on their side (reduces aspiration risk)
3. **Administer 100% oxygen** via mask (first responder/EMT level)
4. **Check central pulse**; monitor blood sugar if possible
5. **Pad surroundings** with pillows/blankets to prevent injury
6. **Maintain patient privacy** and body temperature

### Critical Things NOT to Do

- **Never force seizure cessation**
- **Do not insert an airway device** during an active seizure
- **Avoid forced airway opening**

### Post-Seizure Assessment

**Medical history to gather**:
- Prior seizure occurrence, frequency, last episode
- Medication adherence (carbamazepine, levetiracetam, phenobarbital)
- Substance use, recent medication changes
- Sleep deprivation or other triggers

**Seizure characteristics to document**:
- Duration and whether typical for this patient
- Aura/prodromal symptoms
- Localised vs. generalised pattern
- Associated tongue biting, incontinence
- Consciousness recovery time

**Physical examination**:
- Consciousness level and amnesia
- Pupil assessment
- Blood pressure, oxygen saturation, glucose testing
- Evidence of incontinence (helps differentiate from syncope)
- Signs of head trauma

## Syncope vs. Seizure Differentiation

| Factor | Syncope | Seizure |
|---|---|---|
| Onset position | Typically standing/triggered activity | Any body position |
| Pre-event warning | Dizziness/weakness sensation | Minimal/no warning |
| Consciousness recovery | Immediate after lying down | Minutes to hours |
| Muscle activity | Light myoclonic jerking | Sustained tonic-clonic movements |
| Heart rate | Bradycardia (vagal response) | Tachycardia (muscular exertion) |

## Relevance to Paediatric Seizures / Febrile Seizures

The United Hatzalah page does **not** contain a dedicated section on febrile seizures in children. The protocol is written for general seizure response across all age groups. For child-specific febrile seizure guidance, MDA's guidance is more directly relevant.

Key points from this protocol applicable to children:
- The same "do not restrain, do not insert objects in mouth, position on side" principles apply
- Status epilepticus threshold (5 minutes) is the same for children
- Absence seizures are noted as particularly relevant to paediatric populations
- The post-ictal assessment framework applies to children, though medication history will differ

## Comparison with MDA Guidance

The United Hatzalah protocol is more clinically detailed (aimed at volunteer EMTs/first responders) compared to MDA's public-facing guidance which is aimed at layperson first aid. Key points of agreement:

- Position on side during seizure
- Do not insert anything in the mouth
- Do not restrain
- Call for emergency help if seizure exceeds 5 minutes
- Protect from injury by padding surroundings

No significant disagreements between the two sources were identified.
