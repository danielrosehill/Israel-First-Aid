## Drowning First Aid Protocol -- Children

### Step 1: Ensure Scene Safety

- Do not enter the water unless you are a trained water rescuer. Drowning rescuers frequently become victims themselves.
- Use reaching aids (pole, rope, towel) or throwing aids (flotation device) to bring the child to safety.
- If you must enter water, bring a flotation device.

### Step 2: Remove Child from Water

- Remove the child from the water as quickly as possible without risking your own life.
- Support the head and neck during removal if spinal injury is suspected (e.g., diving accident).

### Step 3: Call MDA -- Dial 101

- Call Magen David Adom immediately at **101**.
- If alone with the child, perform CPR for 2 minutes (5 cycles of 30:2) BEFORE calling 101 (per MDA pediatric protocol).
- If a second person is present, have them call 101 while you begin resuscitation.

### Step 4: Assess Responsiveness and Breathing

- Tap the child's shoulder firmly and call out loudly: "Are you okay?"
- If unresponsive, place the child on their back on a firm, flat surface away from the water's edge.
- Open the airway: gently tilt the head back and lift the chin with two fingers.
- Look, listen, and feel for breathing for 5-10 seconds: observe chest rise, listen for breath sounds, feel for air on your cheek.

### Step 5: Begin CPR if Not Breathing

Drowning is a hypoxic (oxygen-deprivation) emergency. **Rescue breaths are critical** -- compression-only CPR is less effective for drowning victims than for cardiac arrest from heart-related causes.

#### Rescue Breaths

1. Pinch the child's nose closed.
2. Seal your mouth over the child's mouth.
3. Give **2 gentle rescue breaths**, each lasting approximately 1 second.
4. Watch for visible chest rise with each breath.
5. If the chest does not rise, reposition the head and try again.

#### Chest Compressions

1. Place the heel of one hand on the centre of the chest, between the nipples, on the lower third of the sternum.
2. Press down firmly to a depth of **one-third to one-half the depth of the chest**.
3. Compress at a rate of **100-120 compressions per minute**.
4. Allow full chest recoil between compressions.
5. Count compressions aloud.

#### Compression-to-Breath Ratio

| Scenario | Ratio |
|---|---|
| Solo rescuer | 30 compressions : 2 breaths |
| Two rescuers | 15 compressions : 2 breaths |

- Perform cycles continuously until MDA arrives or the child shows signs of life (breathing, coughing, movement).
- Switch rescuers every 2 minutes (5 cycles) if two rescuers are available, to prevent fatigue.

### Step 6: Recovery Position

If the child resumes spontaneous breathing:

- Place the child on their side (recovery position).
- Upper knee bent forward to stabilise the position.
- This maintains airway patency and allows water/vomit to drain from the mouth.
- Continue monitoring breathing until MDA arrives.

### Critical "DO NOT" Rules

- **DO NOT attempt to remove water from the lungs** -- this is impossible and can endanger the victim.
- **DO NOT perform abdominal thrusts (Heimlich manoeuvre)** to expel water -- this is not effective for drowning and risks aspiration of stomach contents.
- **DO NOT delay CPR** to attempt water removal.
- **DO NOT assume the child is fine** if they regain consciousness -- always transport to hospital. Dangerous electrolyte imbalances and secondary drowning (delayed pulmonary oedema) can develop hours after the event.

## Israeli Epidemiological Context

- Drowning is the **second leading cause of unintentional death** in children under 18 in Israel, after traffic accidents.
- Child drowning mortality has risen over 15 years: 72 deaths (2008-2012), 85 deaths (2013-2017), 100 deaths (2018-2022).
- 257 Israeli children died by drowning between January 2008 and December 2022 (Beterem -- Safe Kids Israel / Journal of Safety Research, 2024).
- Boys drown significantly more frequently than girls (70.5% of cases are male).
- Low socioeconomic status is a risk factor for drowning, beyond caregiver inattention alone.
- The overwhelming majority of drownings in children under 10 occur in swimming pools, especially private/domestic pools.

Sources:
- Beterem -- Safe Kids Israel: "Child drowning mortality in Israel: Trends and measures for prevention" (Journal of Safety Research, 2024; PubMed ID 38858046)
- "Drowning rates among children and adolescents (aged 7-17) in Israel during the years 2008-2018" (BMC Public Health, 2023; PubMed ID 37658286)
- "National trends in pediatric drowning" (Israeli Ministry of Health registry-based cohort; PubMed ID 39283324)

## Prevention Guidelines (MDA / Israeli Sources)

### Supervision

- **Never leave children unattended** near any water source -- pools, bathtubs, buckets, decorative ponds, toilets.
- Children under 5 are most vulnerable: their proportionally large, heavy heads can cause them to topple into shallow water sources.
- Children can drown in as little as 5-10 cm (2-4 inches) of water, in under 2 minutes.
- Drowning is often **silent** -- the child may not splash, scream, or call for help.
- Designated adult "water watcher" should maintain constant visual supervision -- no reading, phone use, or other distractions.

### Swimming Education

- Teach children to swim starting from age 1 (per American Academy of Pediatrics recommendation, also cited in Israeli guidance).
- MDA recommends swimming lessons be introduced in 2nd grade (approximately age 7-8) for all populations.

### Environmental Safety

- Install barriers (fences with self-closing, self-latching gates) around private and domestic pools.
- Empty buckets, basins, and containers immediately after use.
- Only swim at beaches with active lifeguard services.
- Obey warning flags: do not enter water when black or red flags are displayed.

### Regulatory Context (Israel)

- The **Regulation of Bathing Places Law (1964)** and associated 1970 regulations govern public swimming facilities, including construction standards, barriers, depth markings, non-slip surfaces, and lifeguard requirements.
- **Private domestic pools are not covered** by these regulations -- this is a significant gap identified by safety researchers.
- Recommended legislative measures include: mandatory fencing of domestic pools, extended lifeguard hours, additional declared beaches, safe behaviour programmes for adolescents, and universal swimming lessons in 2nd grade.

## Climate and Seasonal Notes (Israel-Specific)

- Israel's long, hot summers (May-October) drive high pool and beach usage.
- Desert heat increases dehydration risk, which can impair swimming ability and judgement.
- The bathing season sees a concentrated spike in drowning incidents.
- The Dead Sea, Sea of Galilee (Kinneret), Mediterranean coast, and Red Sea (Eilat) are all common drowning locations.

## Gaps and Limitations

1. **MDA does not publish a standalone drowning first aid protocol document** on its public website. The protocol above is reconstructed from multiple MDA-sourced materials.
2. **MDA's professional ALS protocol (als-2024.pdf)** was not accessible for public review. It likely contains more detailed clinical guidance including medication protocols, advanced airway management, and hospital transport criteria.
3. **Drowning-specific modifications to pediatric CPR** (such as the ABC approach -- airway/breathing first -- rather than the standard CAB approach) are consistent with the 2024 AHA/AAP focused update on drowning resuscitation. MDA's alignment with this specific recommendation could not be independently confirmed from publicly available MDA materials, though MDA's general guidance emphasises rescue breaths for drowning.
4. **In-water rescue breathing** guidance (per 2024 AHA update: trained rescuers may provide rescue breaths while victim is still in water) was not found in MDA public materials.
