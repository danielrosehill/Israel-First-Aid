## Notes on Source Authority

MDA is Israel's sole authorised national emergency medical service under Israeli law. **MDA does not publish a separate public-facing pediatric severe bleeding protocol** -- their civilian guidance applies to all ages with the same fundamental steps (direct pressure, pressure dressing, tourniquet). The pediatric-specific considerations documented below are synthesised from:

1. MDA's general bleeding control guidance (which applies to children)
2. Israeli pediatric medical sources (rofeyeladim.co.il, ynet)
3. Peer-reviewed medical literature on pediatric hemorrhage and tourniquet use
4. Contextual adaptation of MDA adult protocols to pediatric physiology

**Gap identified**: MDA does not publish child-specific hemorrhage control guidelines as a separate document. The protocol below applies MDA's standard bleeding control steps with pediatric modifications drawn from medical evidence.

## Protocol: Severe Bleeding in Children

### Critical Differences from Adult Protocol

| Factor | Adult | Child |
|--------|-------|-------|
| **Blood volume** | ~70 mL/kg (~5 litres in 70 kg adult) | ~80-90 mL/kg (but total volume is much smaller) |
| **Shock threshold** | Loss of ~750 mL+ is significant | A 10 kg child has only ~800 mL total -- loss of 200 mL can cause shock |
| **Compensatory response** | Hypotension appears with ~30% volume loss | Children compensate longer -- hypotension is a **late, ominous sign** (may not appear until 35-45% volume loss) |
| **Early shock signs** | Tachycardia, pallor, cool skin | **Tachycardia is the earliest reliable sign**; also pallor, mottled skin, prolonged capillary refill |
| **Tourniquet sizing** | Standard CAT fits adult limbs | CAT Gen 7 effective for children aged 6+ with limb circumference 16 cm+; evidence supports use from age 2 with minimum 13 cm circumference |
| **Emotional state** | Can usually be directed verbally | Requires age-appropriate communication, calming, and parental involvement |

### Scene Safety

1. Ensure your own safety before approaching.
2. Wear disposable gloves if available.
3. If a parent or caregiver is present, involve them -- they can help keep the child calm.

### Step 1 -- Call 101 (MDA) Immediately

- Call **101** immediately for any severe or uncontrolled bleeding in a child.
- **Emphasise to the dispatcher that the patient is a child** and provide approximate age/weight if known -- this information affects MDA crew preparation.
- Provide location, number of casualties, and nature of injuries.
- Children deteriorate faster than adults; do not delay the call.

### Step 2 -- Apply Direct Pressure (Primary Method)

This is identical to the adult protocol and is the primary intervention:

1. Apply strong, direct pressure to the source of bleeding -- **initially with your bare or gloved hand**.
2. Replace hand pressure with a **sterile bandage, towel, or clean cloth** as soon as available.
3. Maintain **continuous, firm pressure** -- do not lift the dressing to check the wound, as this disrupts clot formation.
4. If the first dressing soaks through, **add additional layers on top** (do not remove the original dressing).
5. **Pediatric note**: Smaller hands may be sufficient for wound coverage. On a small child, one adult hand may cover the entire wound area effectively.

### Step 3 -- Apply a Pressure Dressing (Israeli Bandage)

The Israeli Emergency Bandage is standard in Israeli first aid kits:

1. Apply the white sterile pad directly over the wound.
2. Wrap the elastic bandage around the injured area, passing through the pressure bar.
3. Reverse direction and continue wrapping.
4. Secure using the built-in closure bar.
5. **Pediatric warning**: The elastic component can more easily occlude arteries in smaller limbs. **Verify distal pulse after application** -- check for warmth, colour, and pulse below the bandage. If pulse is absent, loosen slightly and re-secure.

### Step 4 -- Elevate the Limb

- If possible, raise the injured limb above heart level.
- This is an adjunct to direct pressure, not a replacement.

### Step 5 -- Tourniquet (Last Resort)

**Indications (same as adult):**
- Amputation or partial amputation
- Bleeding that cannot be controlled by direct pressure
- Severe crush injury with exposed bone
- Multiple wound sites on the same limb
- Need to free hands for CPR

**Pediatric tourniquet considerations:**

- **CAT Gen 7** is the only tourniquet type with published evidence in pediatric patients.
- Effective on **upper extremities**: 100% success rate in studies; **lower extremities**: 94.5% success rate.
- Evidence supports use from **age 2+** with a minimum limb circumference of **13 cm**.
- For children aged **6+** with limb circumference **16 cm+**, distal pulse occlusion was achieved reliably on both upper and lower extremities.
- **For infants and very small children (under 2)**: No evidence exists for manufactured tourniquets. Use **direct pressure** as the primary and sustained method. Consider improvised narrow pressure devices only under medical direction.

**Application (same as adult):**
1. Place the tourniquet **5-10 cm above the source of bleeding** (proximal, toward the heart).
2. Tighten the windlass until bleeding **completely stops**.
3. Secure the windlass in place.
4. **Record the time of application** in 4-digit format.
5. **Do not remove or loosen** unless instructed by a paramedic or physician.

**Warning**: A tourniquet will cause significant pain. A frightened child in pain will likely scream and struggle -- this is expected. Do not loosen the tourniquet because the child is in distress. Enlist a caregiver to comfort the child while you maintain hemorrhage control.

### Step 6 -- Embedded/Impaled Objects

- **Never remove an embedded object** from the body.
- Stabilise the object in place using padding and bandages around it.
- Apply pressure around the object, not on it.
- **Pediatric note**: A distressed child may try to pull at an embedded object. Gently but firmly restrain the child's hands if necessary, and explain (age-appropriately) that the object must stay in place.

### Step 7 -- Monitor for Shock

Children enter hypovolemic shock faster than adults due to smaller blood volumes. Signs in children:

- **Tachycardia** (rapid heart rate) -- the **earliest and most reliable sign**
- Pale, cool, clammy, or mottled skin
- Bluish discolouration around lips and fingernails
- Fast, shallow breathing
- Weakness, lethargy, confusion
- **Hypotension is a LATE sign** in children -- do not wait for it

**Shock management:**
1. Keep the child lying down.
2. If conscious, **elevate the legs above the level of the head** to improve blood flow to the brain.
3. If unconscious, place in the **recovery position**.
4. Maintain body temperature -- cover with a blanket or clothing. **Israeli climate note**: Even in hot weather, a child in shock can become hypothermic due to poor perfusion.
5. Moisten the lips with water but **do not give food or drink**.
6. Calm the child and reassure them continuously.
7. Monitor breathing and pulse until MDA arrives.

## Emotional Management of the Injured Child

This is a critical component of pediatric first aid that is not typically covered in adult protocols:

1. **Speak softly and calmly** -- use simple, age-appropriate language. Avoid medical jargon.
2. **Involve the parent/caregiver** -- their presence provides comfort. Position them where the child can see and hear them.
3. **Explain what you are doing** in simple terms: "I'm going to press on your arm to help the bleeding stop."
4. **Provide comfort items** if available -- a favourite toy, blanket, or stuffed animal.
5. **Acknowledge the child's fear and pain** -- "I know this hurts. You're being very brave."
6. **Maintain eye contact** and physical contact (hold a hand) when possible.
7. **Do not lie** -- if something will hurt, say "this will press hard and it might hurt, but it's helping you."
8. **Shield the child from the sight of blood** if possible -- cover wounds with dressings promptly, and position the child so they cannot see the wound.

## Pediatric Blood Volume Reference Table

| Age | Approximate Weight | Blood Volume (mL/kg) | Total Blood Volume | Dangerous Loss (~25%) |
|-----|--------------------|----------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Infant (1 year) | 10 kg | 80 mL/kg | 800 mL | 200 mL |
| Toddler (3 years) | 15 kg | 80 mL/kg | 1,200 mL | 300 mL |
| Child (6 years) | 20 kg | 80 mL/kg | 1,600 mL | 400 mL |
| Child (10 years) | 30 kg | 70-80 mL/kg | 2,100-2,400 mL | 525-600 mL |
| Adolescent (14 years) | 50 kg | 70 mL/kg | 3,500 mL | 875 mL |

**Context**: An adult has approximately 5,000 mL of blood. A 3-year-old child has less than a quarter of that. What appears to be moderate bleeding in an adult may represent life-threatening blood loss in a small child.

## Israeli Context Notes

- **Security environment**: Israeli children may be exposed to blast/shrapnel injuries. In mass casualty events, standard hemorrhage control applies to children -- do not hesitate to apply a tourniquet if direct pressure fails, even on a young child.
- **Climate**: In Israel's hot climate, dehydration compounds the effects of blood loss. A child who is already dehydrated from heat has reduced circulating volume and will enter shock even faster.
- **Availability of equipment**: CAT tourniquets and Israeli bandages are widely available in Israeli homes, schools, and public spaces. Many parents have received basic hemorrhage control training through MDA or school preparedness programs.
- **Emergency number**: Always call **101 (MDA)**. If a United Hatzalah volunteer arrives first (typical in urban areas -- 90-second average response), they will begin treatment and coordinate with MDA.
