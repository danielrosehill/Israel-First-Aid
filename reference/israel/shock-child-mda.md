## Definition of Shock

Shock (הלם) is a state of inadequate tissue perfusion — the body's blood supply fails to deliver sufficient oxygen and nutrients to organs and tissues. It results from impairment in one or more of three components:

1. **Blood volume** (the fluid) — e.g., hemorrhage, dehydration
2. **Blood vessel tone** (the container) — e.g., anaphylaxis, sepsis
3. **Cardiac output** (the pump) — e.g., cardiac dysfunction

In children, untreated shock is rapidly fatal.

## Pediatric-Specific Considerations

Children are physiologically different from adults in ways that critically affect shock recognition and management:

### Blood Volume
- Children have much lower total blood volumes than adults
- A newborn (~3 kg) has only approximately 240 mL of total blood
- A one-year-old (~10 kg) has approximately 800 mL of total blood
- **Every drop of fluid is critical in children** — small volumes of blood loss that would be insignificant in adults can be life-threatening in children

### Compensation and Sudden Deterioration
- Children have strong cardiovascular compensatory mechanisms
- They can maintain near-normal blood pressure even with significant fluid loss (compensated shock)
- **Children compensate longer, then crash suddenly** — they may appear relatively stable during compensated shock and then deteriorate rapidly without warning
- **Normal blood pressure does NOT exclude shock** — the child may still be in the compensated stage
- Transition from compensated to decompensated shock can be sudden and catastrophic

### Progression of Shock (Three Stages)

1. **Compensated stage:** The sympathetic nervous system activates, releasing catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine). Heart rate increases, peripheral blood vessels constrict. **Tachycardia is the earliest sign.**

2. **Uncompensated (decompensated) stage:** Blood pressure begins falling. Rapid, severe deterioration. Acidosis develops, making treatment less effective. Mottled skin from head to toe.

3. **Washout (irreversible) stage:** Peripheral blood containing toxins and lactic acid floods central circulation. Typically fatal.

## Signs and Symptoms of Shock in Children

### Early Signs (Compensated Shock)
- **Tachycardia** (rapid pulse) — the EARLIEST and most reliable sign
- Mild tachypnea (slightly increased breathing rate)
- **Prolonged capillary refill time** (>2 seconds) — more useful in children than adults
- Cool extremities (hands and feet)
- Mild anxiety or irritability
- Normal blood pressure (deceptively reassuring)

### Progressive Signs
- Rapid, weak, or thready pulse
- Rapid, shallow breathing
- **Pale skin** — blood is shunted away from the periphery
- **Cold, clammy skin** with perspiration
- Dry mucous membranes, thirst
- **Mottled skin** — patchy discoloration indicating poor perfusion
- Increased anxiety and restlessness
- Reduced urine output

### Late Signs (Decompensated Shock) — EMERGENCY
- **Hypotension** (falling blood pressure) — a LATE sign in children
- **Altered mental status** — confusion, lethargy, decreased responsiveness
- Weak or absent peripheral pulses
- Bradycardia (abnormally slow heart rate — a pre-arrest sign)
- Loss of consciousness
- Non-detectable distal pulses

## First Aid Treatment Protocol

### Immediate Actions

1. **Call 101 (MDA) immediately** — do not delay. Shock in children requires urgent professional medical care and hospital treatment.

2. **Assess and manage life threats** — check airway, breathing, circulation (ABC). If the child is not breathing, begin CPR.

3. **Control any visible bleeding** — apply direct pressure to wounds. Hemorrhage is the most common cause of hypovolemic shock in trauma.

### Positioning

4. **If conscious:** Lay the child flat on their back. **Gently elevate the legs** above the level of the head (e.g., rest legs on a chair or pillow) to increase blood flow to the brain and vital organs.

5. **If unconscious but breathing:** Place in the **recovery position** (lateral/side-lying position) to protect the airway.

6. **If spinal injury is suspected:** Do NOT move the child. Stabilise and wait for EMS.

### Temperature Management

7. **Keep the child warm** — cover with a blanket or clothing. One function of circulating blood is thermoregulation; when perfusion is inadequate, the body loses heat rapidly. Protect from cold ground as well (insulate from below).

### Comfort and Monitoring

8. **Remove or loosen restrictive clothing** that may impair breathing or circulation.

9. **Calm and reassure the child** — anxiety and distress worsen the physiological response. Speak gently and stay with the child.

10. **Moisten lips with water if the child is thirsty** — do NOT give food or drink (the child may need surgery or anaesthesia).

11. **Monitor continuously** — track breathing, pulse, skin colour, and level of consciousness until EMS arrives. Be prepared for sudden deterioration.

### What NOT To Do

- Do NOT sit the child upright (reduces blood flow to brain)
- Do NOT give the child anything to eat or drink
- Do NOT leave the child unattended
- Do NOT delay calling 101 — early hospital treatment with IV fluids and medications is critical

## Israeli Context Notes

- **Climate:** Israel's extreme heat (especially Negev/Arava desert regions) increases dehydration risk in children, which can precipitate or worsen hypovolemic shock. Heat exposure should be considered as a contributing factor.
- **Security context:** Blast and shrapnel injuries from security incidents can cause hemorrhagic shock. Tourniquet use is part of civilian first aid training in Israel.
- **Anaphylaxis triggers common in Israel:** Bee and wasp stings (common in warm months), food allergies. EpiPen auto-injectors are available in child and adult doses.
- **MDA response time:** MDA and United Hatzalah volunteer first responders typically arrive within minutes in urban areas. Call 101 immediately.
