## 1. Definitions

### Heat Exhaustion (תשישות חום)
A condition where the body overheats due to fluid and electrolyte loss. Body temperature is typically 38-40 degrees C. This is the more common and less severe condition but can progress to heat stroke if untreated.

### Heat Stroke (מכת חום)
A life-threatening medical emergency where the body's temperature regulation fails completely. Body temperature exceeds 40-41 degrees C. Can cause multi-system organ damage (brain, heart, liver, kidneys, blood clotting) and death within minutes to hours.

### Two types of heat stroke:
- **Classic heat stroke (מכת חום קלאסית):** From exposure to high ambient temperatures. This is the type most relevant to children, infants, and elderly.
- **Exertional heat stroke (מכת חום מאמצית):** From strenuous physical activity in hot/humid conditions. More relevant to young adults in sports or military training, but also relevant to children on school tiyulim (hikes).

---

## 2. Why Children Are at Higher Risk

- Children's thermoregulation mechanisms are less developed and less efficient than adults'.
- Children have a higher surface-area-to-body-mass ratio, absorbing heat faster.
- Children aged 0-4 are at the highest risk.
- Infants and toddlers cannot communicate distress effectively.
- Young children may become "floppy or sleepy" rather than showing typical adult symptoms.
- Children depend on adults for hydration and shade -- they may not drink enough on their own.

### Key risk scenarios for children in Israel:
1. **Children left in vehicles (שכחת ילדים ברכב):** Temperatures inside locked cars in Israel can reach 70 degrees C (160 degrees F). Between 2010-2020, 34 babies and children in Israel died after being left in parked cars (Beterem data). Israeli law (2021) requires safety alert systems in child car seats.
2. **School trips / tiyulim (טיולים):** Extended hiking in open terrain, especially in the Negev, Judean Desert, or Jordan Valley.
3. **Prolonged outdoor play** in summer months without adequate shade or hydration.
4. **Beach outings** with extended sun exposure.

## 3. Symptoms

### Heat Exhaustion (early stage):
- Intense thirst
- Headache
- Rapid, shallow breathing
- Nausea and vomiting
- Profuse sweating
- Pale, clammy skin
- Dizziness or faintness
- Muscle cramps (arms, legs, stomach)
- Extreme weakness (in older children)
- Loss of appetite
- In young children: becoming floppy or unusually sleepy

### Heat Stroke (severe -- EMERGENCY):
- Body temperature above 40-41 degrees C
- Flushed, dark red appearance
- Hot, DRY skin (sweating mechanism has failed)
- Altered consciousness: confusion, disorientation, loss of awareness
- Elevated heart rate and respiratory rate
- Severe headache
- Extreme weakness
- Seizures / convulsions
- Hallucinations
- Altered gait
- Low blood pressure

**Critical distinction:** In heat exhaustion the skin is pale and sweaty. In heat stroke the skin is red, hot, and dry. The cessation of sweating is a danger sign.

## 4. First Aid Protocol -- Field Treatment

### Immediate actions (in parallel):

**Step 1: Call for help**
- Call MDA at 101 immediately or use the "My MDA" app
- Follow dispatcher instructions until ambulance arrives

**Step 2: Move to shade/cool area**
- Remove the child from the heat source immediately
- Move to shade, air-conditioned room, or coolest available location

**Step 3: Remove clothing**
- Remove excess and tight clothing to allow heat dissipation

**Step 4: Active cooling**
- Pour/spray water over the child's body (tepid to cool water, not ice-cold)
- Fan the child to promote evaporative cooling
- Place ice packs or cold wet cloths in armpits (בתי השחי) and groin (מפשעות) -- these are areas with major blood vessels close to the surface
- If available, direct air conditioning or fan toward the child

**Step 5: Maintain airway**
- If the child is unconscious, ensure the airway is open
- Place in recovery position if unconscious but breathing

**Step 6: Rehydration (conscious children only)**
- Give small, frequent sips of cool water or oral rehydration solution (ORS)
- Do NOT force fluids if the child is vomiting or has altered consciousness
- A child who is vomiting should NOT drink (risk of aspiration and impaired digestion)

### IMPORTANT -- Do NOT:
- Do NOT give fever-reducing medications (acetaminophen / Acamol, ibuprofen / Nurofen, naproxen). They will NOT reduce heat stroke temperature and may cause harm.
- Do NOT use ice-cold water immersion for young children without medical supervision.
- Do NOT leave the child unattended.

## 5. Prevention Guidelines (MDA Recommendations)

### Hydration:
- Ensure children drink frequently, even if not thirsty
- Adults: 8-10 glasses of water per day; children proportionally
- Offer water to children regularly -- do not wait for them to ask
- Monitor urine output as an indicator of hydration

### Clothing and sun protection:
- Light-coloured, breathable, loose-fitting clothing
- Wide-brimmed hat (כובע רחב שוליים)
- Sunglasses
- Sunscreen SPF 30+ (per Ministry of Health recommendation)

### Activity management:
- Avoid strenuous outdoor activity during peak heat hours (approximately 10:00-16:00)
- Schedule outdoor activities for early morning or late afternoon/evening
- During tiyulim: rest in shade for 10-15 minutes regularly
- Cancel outdoor activities during extreme heat warnings (per Ministry of Health hiking situation room -- חדר מצב טיולים)
- During school trips, carry a first aid kit adapted for heat emergencies

### Vehicle safety:
- NEVER leave a child unattended in a vehicle, even for a moment
- Even with windows open or air conditioning running, a car can become deadly
- Check the back seat every time you leave the car
- Use reminder systems (required by Israeli law for car seats since 2021)
- Temperatures inside a closed car in Israeli summer can reach 70 degrees C

### Environment:
- Stay in air-conditioned environments as much as possible during heat waves
- Avoid taking small children to places where shade is unavailable
- Keep cold beverages accessible for children at all times

---

## 6. When to Call MDA (101)

Call immediately if a child shows ANY of the following:
- Body temperature above 40 degrees C
- Hot, dry, red skin
- Confusion, disorientation, altered consciousness
- Seizures
- Loss of consciousness
- Symptoms of heat exhaustion that do not improve within 30 minutes of cooling

## 7. Israeli Context Notes

- **Climate:** Israeli summers regularly exceed 35-40 degrees C in many regions. The Negev, Jordan Valley (Arava), and Dead Sea area can exceed 45 degrees C. High humidity along the coastal plain makes evaporative cooling less effective.
- **Sharav/Hamsin (שרב):** Hot, dry desert wind events that push temperatures 5-10 degrees above normal. MDA issues special warnings during sharav conditions.
- **School system:** The Ministry of Education and Ministry of Health jointly issue guidelines for school tiyulim, including temperature thresholds for cancellation.
- **Military context:** Exertional heat stroke is a known risk during basic training (tironut). While not directly child-related, teenagers entering pre-military programs (mechinot, gadna) should be aware.
- **Beterem (בטרם):** Israel's national child safety organisation, tracks vehicular heat stroke deaths and advocates for prevention.
- **Legal requirement:** Since 2021, Israeli law mandates alert systems in child car seats to prevent children being forgotten in vehicles.
