## 1. Body Temperature Regulation (Background)

United Hatzalah explains that the body maintains optimal function at 36.5-37.5 degrees C, regulated by the hypothalamus. The body manages heat through:
- Decreased heat production
- Increased cardiac output
- Perspiration (sweating)
- Blood vessel dilation
- Accelerated breathing

**Critical thresholds:**
- Hyperthermia: above 40 degrees C -- proteins undergo dangerous denaturation
- Hypothermia: below 35 degrees C -- body processes slow dangerously

**At-risk populations identified by United Hatzalah:** Infants and elderly individuals, people with anaemia, those exposed to chronic cold, alcohol and drug users, patients with liver disease.

## 2. Classification of Heat Injuries

United Hatzalah classifies heat injuries into three levels of severity:

### Level 1: Dehydration (התייבשות)

**Mild dehydration:**
- Thirst
- Rapid pulse
- Nausea
- Restlessness
- Decreased function

**Moderate dehydration:**
- Dizziness
- Headache
- Dryness (mucous membranes)
- Weakness
- Pale skin

**Severe dehydration:**
- Altered consciousness
- Hallucinations
- Signs of hypovolaemic shock

**Treatment:**
- Rest in a cool place
- Small, frequent amounts of water (if NOT vomiting)
- Transport to hospital for electrolyte replacement
- NOTE: A vomiting patient should NOT drink -- risk of impaired digestion and aspiration

### Level 2: Heat Exhaustion (תשישות חום)

The most common heat-related condition. Involves fluid and electrolyte loss.

**Symptoms:**
- Rapid pulse
- Low blood pressure
- Diarrhoea
- Nausea
- Fainting
- Decreased urination
- Vomiting
- Dizziness
- Muscle cramps
- Headache

**Treatment:**
- Remove from heat
- Support ABC (Airway, Breathing, Circulation)
- Provide fluids
- Rest
- Transport to hospital

### Level 3: Heat Stroke (מכת חום)

A life-threatening condition. Body temperature exceeds 40 degrees C.

**Symptoms:**
- Elevated body temperature (above 40 degrees C)
- Rapid pulse
- Confusion
- Vomiting
- Hallucinations
- Altered gait
- Low blood pressure

**Treatment:**
- Provide high-concentration oxygen (if available -- for trained responders)
- Rapidly cool the patient
- Remove clothing
- Monitor for cardiac arrhythmias during transport
- Note: Rectal temperature measurement is for paramedic/physician only

## 3. Prevention Strategies (United Hatzalah Recommendations)

- Check weather forecasts before outdoor activities
- Maintain consistent hydration; monitor urine output
- Rest in shade for 10-15 minutes during hikes
- Avoid peak heat hours; schedule activities for early morning or evening
- Wear protective headwear (hat)
- Remain vigilant about others' conditions -- watch for warning signs in companions and children

## 4. Child-Specific Notes

United Hatzalah identifies infants as an at-risk population for temperature-related emergencies. Their Family Safety course covers paediatric emergencies including heat-related conditions, designed for parents, grandparents, teachers, and babysitters.

**Gap noted:** United Hatzalah's publicly available online protocol for hot climate injuries does not include a dedicated paediatric section. The protocol is general and applies to all ages. Child-specific modifications (e.g., fluid volumes, cooling rates) are not detailed in the public-facing material.

---

## 5. Comparison with MDA Guidelines

United Hatzalah's protocol is broadly consistent with MDA's guidance. Key points of alignment:
- Both emphasise immediate cooling, shade, clothing removal
- Both recommend hydration for conscious patients only
- Both warn about the progression from dehydration to heat exhaustion to heat stroke
- Both identify infants/children as high-risk populations
- Both recommend 10-15 minute shade breaks during outdoor activities

**Differences noted:**
- United Hatzalah's protocol explicitly mentions high-concentration oxygen and arrhythmia monitoring (these are more advanced/professional interventions)
- United Hatzalah's classification system is more structured (three clear levels)
- MDA's public materials place more emphasis on vehicular heat stroke in children and the car-seat alert law
- MDA is more explicit about avoiding antipyretics (acetaminophen/ibuprofen)

**Per project convention, MDA is the authoritative default where sources disagree.** In practice, there are no significant contradictions between the two organisations on heat-related first aid.
